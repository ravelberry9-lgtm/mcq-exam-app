Anti-Defection Law

App Ch85 · Book Ch82 · Indian Polity (Lakshmikanth)

1. Background and Need

The Problem of Defection

Political defection — a legislator switching from the party on whose ticket they were elected — was rampant in Indian politics from the 1960s. The practice was called "aaya ram gaya ram" after Haryana legislator Gaya Lal who switched parties three times in one day in 1967. Between 1967–1979, approximately 40% of all sitting MPs/MLAs defected at some point.

Need for Anti-Defection Law

Political instability — frequent defection caused governments to fall; coalition governments were fragile

Horse-trading — legislators sold their votes for money or ministerial positions

Corruption of the democratic mandate — elected on one party's platform, switching after election

Undermined accountability to voters and political parties

The Dinesh Singh Committee (1967) and the Chandra Shekhar Committee (1969) recommended anti-defection measures

2. Constitutional Basis — Tenth Schedule

52nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1985

Added the Tenth Schedule to the Constitution

Came into force: March 1, 1985

Also amended Articles 101, 102, 190, and 191

Applies to members of both Houses of Parliament and State Legislatures (including Legislative Councils)

Applies to members elected on a party ticket AND members who contest as independents

Does NOT apply to persons elected to office of Speaker/Deputy Speaker (Paras 6 — Speaker exempt while holding office)

Structure of the Tenth Schedule

Paragraph 1: Interpretation (defines "Legislature party", "original party", "merge")

Paragraph 2: Disqualification on grounds of defection

Paragraph 3: [Originally — exception for split; deleted by 91st Amendment 2003]

Paragraph 4: Disqualification on grounds of voting contrary to party direction

Paragraph 5: Exemption — merger of parties

Paragraph 6: Decision by Speaker/Chairman

Paragraph 7: [Originally — bar on judicial review; struck down in Kihoto Hollohan 1992]

Paragraph 8: Rules

3. Grounds for Disqualification

Disqualification Grounds (Para 2)

A member of Parliament or State Legislature is disqualified on grounds of defection if:

(i) Voluntarily gives up membership of the political party they were elected from

(ii) Votes or abstains from voting contrary to party direction (whip) without prior permission and such vote/abstention is not condoned within 15 days

Independent Members

An independently elected member (no party affiliation) who joins any political party after election is disqualified. They are required to remain independent throughout their term.

Nominated Members

A nominated member who has not joined any political party within 6 months of taking their seat may join any party within those 6 months. If they join after 6 months, they are disqualified. If they join within 6 months, they are bound by party discipline thereafter.

What "Voluntarily Giving Up" Means

In Ravi S. Naik v. Union of India (1994), the Supreme Court held that "voluntarily giving up membership" is broader than formal resignation — even conduct that evidences an intent to give up membership can constitute disqualification. A member who campaigns against the party's candidate in an election may be deemed to have voluntarily given up membership.

4. Exceptions and Merger

Exception: Merger (Para 5)

A member is NOT disqualified if they support a merger of their party with another party

A valid merger requires: at least 2/3rds of the members of the legislature party to agree to the merger

The merger must involve merger with another political party (not just creating a splinter group)

The 1/3rd minority who don't agree to the merger are also NOT disqualified if they function as a separate group (this protection was deleted after 91st Amendment eliminated split)

Split Provision Deleted (91st Amendment 2003)

Originally Para 3 allowed a "split" exemption — if 1/3rd of the legislature party split to form a group, they were not disqualified

91st Constitutional Amendment Act 2003 deleted Para 3 — the split exemption was removed

Now: only merger (2/3rds) is exempt — split is no longer a defence

This plugged the loophole that parties used to engineer "splits" to facilitate defections

Speaker/Chairman Exempt

Para 6: The Speaker or Chairman (of Lok Sabha/Rajya Sabha or state legislature) is exempt from disqualification if they give up party membership upon election to the office. They may re-join their party if they cease to hold that office. This preserves the convention of Speaker's impartiality.

5. Decision-Making Authority

Speaker/Chairman as Decision Maker

Para 6: All questions of disqualification under the Tenth Schedule are decided by the Speaker of Lok Sabha / Chairman of Rajya Sabha (for Parliament), or the Speaker/Chairman of State Legislature (for states)

The Speaker's/Chairman's decision is final subject to judicial review

They must follow principles of natural justice — opportunity to be heard

Criticism: Speaker is often a member of the ruling party — conflict of interest

Criticism of Speaker as Adjudicator

Speaker may act in partisan manner, protecting ruling party defectors while disqualifying opposition defectors

Delay in deciding disqualification petitions — some cases pending for years

Supreme Court in Kihoto Hollohan (1992) upheld Speaker's authority but made it subject to judicial review

Supreme Court in Nabam Rebia (2016) held that Speaker facing a no-confidence notice cannot decide disqualification petitions

Reform proposal: Transfer disqualification decision to Election Commission or Supreme Court/High Court

Time Limit for Decision

The Tenth Schedule does not prescribe a time limit for the Speaker's decision. The Supreme Court in various cases (including Rajendra Singh Rana 2007, and later Keisham Meghachandra Singh 2020) has directed Speakers to decide within 3 months. In Subhash Desai (Maharashtra 2023), SC ordered decision within reasonable time.

6. Judicial Review and Key Cases

Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)

Most important case on anti-defection law

Five-judge Constitution Bench (3:2 majority)

Upheld the constitutional validity of the Tenth Schedule

Struck down Para 7 (which barred judicial review) as unconstitutional — violates basic structure (judicial review)

Held: Speaker's decision is subject to judicial review, but only AFTER the Speaker gives a final decision (not during the proceedings)

Held: Tenth Schedule does not violate Articles 105, 194 (freedom of speech of legislators) or the basic structure

Ravi S. Naik v. Union of India (1994)

Expanded the meaning of "voluntarily giving up membership" beyond formal resignation

Conduct evidencing intent to give up membership can constitute disqualification

Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker (2016)

Supreme Court held: a Speaker who is himself facing a motion for removal (no-confidence) cannot decide disqualification petitions under the Tenth Schedule

Conflict of interest — Speaker cannot be judge when his own position is under threat

Context: Arunachal Pradesh political crisis

Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary (Maharashtra Crisis, 2023)

Five-judge Constitution Bench; arose from Maharashtra political crisis (Shiv Sena split — Eknath Shinde faction)

SC held: Maharashtra Governor's direction to CM Uddhav Thackeray to prove majority was wrong

SC held: Eknath Shinde and rebel MLAs were liable for disqualification but cannot decide as Speaker is from rebel faction; referred to new Speaker

SC reaffirmed: disqualification petitions must be decided within reasonable time

SC held: even if a majority of the legislature party defects, they cannot escape disqualification — the "original party" (political party) is what matters, not just the legislature party numbers

7. 91st Amendment and Reforms

91st Constitutional Amendment Act, 2003

Deleted Para 3 of Tenth Schedule — removed the 1/3rd split exemption

Amended Articles 75(1A) and 164(1A) — Council of Ministers limited to 15% of House strength (to prevent large cabinets as bribes to defectors)

A member disqualified under Tenth Schedule is also barred from being appointed as a Minister during their remaining term

Proposed Reforms to Anti-Defection Law

Transfer decision-making from Speaker to President/Governor (on ECI advice) — recommended by Goswami Committee

Transfer to Election Commission of India

Transfer to an independent tribunal or to the Supreme Court/High Court

Prescribe statutory time limit for Speaker's decision (3 months suggested)

Limit whip to only confidence motions and money bills (allow free vote on other matters)

NCRWC (2002) recommended: independent tribunal; Election Commission's role

Criticism of Anti-Defection Law

Stifles legislative debate — members cannot vote against party whip even on conscience issues

Undermines parliamentary sovereignty — party leadership, not member, decides votes

Has not fully stopped defection — engineered mergers continue (2/3rds threshold easily crossed when money is involved)

Speaker's partisanship and delayed decisions undermine the law's effectiveness

Creates a "dictatorial" party system — dissent is punished
