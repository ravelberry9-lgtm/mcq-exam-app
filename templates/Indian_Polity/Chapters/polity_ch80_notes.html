🗳️ Elections

App Chapter 80  |  Lakshmikanth Book Ch77

0. Constitutional Framework of Elections

Key Articles

Art.324: ECI — superintendence, direction and control of elections

Art.325: Single electoral roll — no separate roll based on religion, race, caste, sex

Art.326: Elections to LS and State Assemblies — adult suffrage

Art.327: Parliament may make provision for elections to Parliament and State Legislatures

Art.328: State Legislature may make provision for State Legislature elections

Art.329: Bar to interference by courts in electoral matters

Adult Suffrage (Art.326)

Universal Adult Suffrage: every citizen 18+ years can vote

Originally 21 years; lowered to 18 by 61st Amendment 1988

Disqualifications: non-citizen, unsound mind, electoral crime, corrupt practice

No discrimination on basis of religion, race, caste, sex, literacy, property

1. Types of Elections

General Elections

Lok Sabha general elections: every 5 years (or upon dissolution)

State Assembly elections: every 5 years (or upon dissolution/President's Rule)

First General Elections: 1952; Second: 1957; 18th Lok Sabha election: 2024

Multi-phase elections for large states; all states may vote on different dates

By-Elections and Bi-Annual Elections

By-election (bye-poll): held when a seat becomes vacant mid-term (due to death, resignation, disqualification)

Must be held within 6 months of vacancy (unless less than 1 year remains in term)

Rajya Sabha elections: indirect by elected MLAs; 1/3 members retire every 2 years

Presidential election: indirect — elected by Electoral College (MPs + MLAs)

2. Constituencies and Delimitation

Constituency Types

Single-member constituencies: each LS constituency returns 1 MP

Multi-member constituencies: some State Assembly seats have 2 members (abolished for most; retained in Jammu and Kashmir)

Delimitation: redrawing constituency boundaries based on census (Art.82 for LS, Art.170 for State Assemblies)

Delimitation Commission: independent body; orders final, not challengeable in court

Voter Roll

Art.325: Single unified electoral roll — no separate rolls for communities

EPIC (Electoral Photo Identity Card): Voter ID card; not mandatory for voting (though needed for identification)

Electoral roll updated annually — continuous revision; special drives in August-November

NRI (Non-Resident Indians) can vote in their home constituency since 2010

3. Conduct of Elections (RPA 1951)

Representation of People Act 1951

RPA 1951: primary legislation governing conduct of elections

Covers: Qualification/disqualification of candidates; corrupt practices; election offences; election petitions; disputes

Corrupt practices (Sec.123 RPA): bribery, undue influence, false statement, booth-capturing, use of government machinery, religious/caste appeals

Election petition: only before HC (LS/Assembly) or SC (Presidential/Vice-Presidential); must be filed within 45 days of result declaration

Election Schedule

ECI issues Model Code of Conduct upon election schedule announcement

Election notification → Filing nominations → Scrutiny → Withdrawal → Polling → Counting → Declaration

Minimum notice period: 7 days from notification to last date of nomination

Polling day: official holiday; voters entitled to paid leave to vote

4. EVM and VVPAT

Electronic Voting Machine (EVM)

EVM introduced: first used in limited way in 1982 (Kerala by-election Paravur constituency); nationwide by 2004

Stands-alone machine — not connected to internet; cannot be hacked remotely

EVM advantages: reduces booth capturing, speeds counting, no invalid votes

Manufactured by: BEL (Bharat Electronics Limited) and ECIL (Electronics Corporation of India Limited)

VVPAT (Voter-Verifiable Paper Audit Trail)

VVPAT introduced: first used in Noksen constituency, Nagaland, 2013 (limited trial)

VVPAT paper slip shows candidate name and symbol for 7 seconds after vote; voter can verify

SC: VVPAT slips must be counted in 5 randomly selected booths per constituency (SC 2019)

ADR case: SC dismissed demands for full VVPAT count (2024) — upheld EVM + VVPAT process

5. Qualifications and Disqualifications

Qualification to Contest

Lok Sabha: Indian citizen, 25+ years, on electoral roll in any constituency

Rajya Sabha: Indian citizen, 30+ years; constitutional requirement: ordinary resident of state removed (2003)

State Assembly: Indian citizen, 25+ years, on electoral roll in any constituency in the state

State Council: Indian citizen, 30+ years

Disqualification to Contest

Art.102 (Parliament) and Art.191 (State Legislature): disqualifications

Holding office of profit under Government

Convicted of offence involving moral turpitude or sentence of 2+ years imprisonment

Sec.8 RPA 1951: convicted of offences → disqualified for 6 years from release

Lily Thomas case (2013): convicted MPs/MLAs cannot continue in office pending appeal

6. Election Expenditure

Election Expenditure Limits

Candidate expenditure limit: set by ECI; currently (2024) LS — Rs.95 lakh (larger states); Rs.75 lakh (smaller states/UTs)

State Assembly: Rs.40 lakh (larger states); Rs.28 lakh (smaller states)

Expenditure account: candidate must file within 30 days of election result

Violation → election void (election petition grounds)

Party expenditure: no legal ceiling on party's overall election spending

Free and Fair Elections

Model Code of Conduct (MCC): ECI's non-statutory code for parties and candidates

Paid news: ECI has guidelines against surrogate advertising paid for by candidates

Exit polls banned until last phase of voting is over (to prevent influence)

Opinion polls regulated: subject to ECI guidelines on publication periods
