Voting Behaviour

App Ch83 · Book Ch80 · Indian Polity (Lakshmikanth)

1. Meaning and Significance

What is Voting Behaviour?

Voting behaviour refers to the manner in which citizens cast their votes in elections. It is the study of how and why voters choose to support a particular candidate or political party. It is a sub-field of political sociology and electoral studies.

Significance of Studying Voting Behaviour

It reveals the relationship between social structure and political choices

Helps understand the sources of political party support

Reveals the functioning and health of democracy

Illuminates the role of identity (caste, religion, region) in Indian politics

Assists parties in formulating electoral strategies

Key Scholars

Rajni Kothari — caste and politics, Congress system

Myron Weiner — political parties and electoral mobilisation

Paul Brass — language, region, and ethnicity in Indian politics

André Béteille — caste system and social stratification

Lokniti (CSDS) — empirical electoral studies in India

Yogendra Yadav — social basis of Indian politics, post-Mandal caste dynamics

2. Theories of Voting Behaviour

Michigan Model (Sociological/Party Identification)

Developed by Angus Campbell, Philip Converse, Warren Miller, and Donald Stokes at the University of Michigan. Emphasises party identification as the central factor — voters develop a long-term psychological attachment to a party. In India, this manifests as caste/community loyalty to a party over generations.

Columbia Model (Social Determinism)

Developed by Paul Lazarsfeld et al. (Bureau of Applied Social Research, Columbia University). Voting behaviour is largely determined by social characteristics — class, religion, and place of residence. "A person thinks politically as he is socially." In India, social identity (jati, region) determines voting choice.

Rational Choice Theory (Economic Model)

Anthony Downs' Economic Theory of Democracy. Voters make rational calculations — they vote for the party that will maximise their utility or benefit. In India, this explains issue-based voting, anti-incumbency, and welfare scheme voting (MGNREGA, free electricity, etc.).

Psephology

Psephology = scientific study of elections and voting behaviour using statistical and survey methods

Term coined by R.B. McCallum (Oxford, 1952)

In India: Lokniti-CSDS (Centre for the Study of Developing Societies) conducts National Election Studies (NES)

Notable Indian psephologists: Yogendra Yadav, Sanjay Kumar, Praveen Rai

3. Caste and Voting Behaviour

Role of Caste

Caste remains the most significant determinant of voting behaviour in India. Rajni Kothari famously observed: "Caste is politicised and politics is casteised." Caste networks serve as ready-made organisations for political mobilisation. Parties nominate candidates from numerically dominant castes in constituencies.

Caste Vote Banks

Jatav/Dalit vote — historically Congress, later shifted to BSP (under Kanshi Ram and Mayawati in UP)

OBC consolidation — post-Mandal Report implementation (1990), major beneficiary was Janata Dal, later SP, RJD, JD(U) etc.

Upper-caste vote — traditionally Congress, BJP gained upper-caste base in 1980s–90s

Yadav vote — SP in UP, RJD in Bihar (Lalu Prasad's core support)

Kurmi/Koeri — JD(U) in Bihar (Nitish Kumar base)

Jat vote — RLD/BJP in western UP, Haryana

Mandal Commission Impact on Caste Politics

Mandal Commission Report (1980) recommended 27% OBC reservation in central services

V.P. Singh government implemented Mandal recommendations in 1990

Led to consolidation of OBC identity in politics — "social justice" vs. "Hindutva" axis emerged

Rise of regional parties based on caste coalitions: SP, RJD, BSP, JD(S), etc.

Post-Mandal, coalition politics became dominant — single-party majority rare at national level (1989–2014)

Caste and Candidate Selection

Parties match candidate's caste to the dominant caste of the constituency

Caste calculations in coalition-building ("social engineering")

Mayawati's BSP used "bahujan" coalition — Dalits + OBCs + minorities + some upper castes

BJP's PANNA PRAMUKH system combined with caste arithmetic (2014, 2019 elections)

4. Religion and Voting Behaviour

Religion as Factor

Religion is a significant but complex determinant of voting. India's major religious communities tend to vote as blocs in certain contexts, though class, caste, region, and issue-based factors also fragment the religious vote.

Hindu Vote

Not a monolithic bloc — divided by caste (Brahmin, OBC, Dalit, Jat, etc.)

BJP's Hindutva platform aimed at consolidating Hindu vote across caste lines

Ram Janmabhoomi movement (1990s) — key moment in Hindu vote consolidation for BJP

L.K. Advani's Rath Yatra (1990) polarised Hindu vote

2014 and 2019: BJP achieved significant Hindu vote consolidation (Modi wave)

Muslim Vote

Historically concentrated on Congress as "secular" party

Post-1989: fragmented across regional parties (SP, RJD, TMC, IUML, etc.) based on local context

AIMIM (Asaduddin Owaisi) attracts Muslim vote in some urban constituencies

Tactical voting: Muslims often vote for the party most likely to defeat BJP in a constituency

National Election Studies show Muslim voter turnout is often higher than national average

Other Religious Communities

Sikhs — Akali Dal (Punjab); Sikh vote significant in Punjab and Delhi

Christians — regional parties in Northeast (NPP, NPF, etc.); Congress in some states

Communal polarisation: BJP's appeal to religious identity vs. Congress's composite nationalism

5. Region, Language, and Ethnicity

Regionalism in Voting

Regional identity strongly shapes voting behaviour in India. Regional parties have proliferated because they can better address local aspirations. The 1990s saw the rise of coalition governments at the Centre, driven by regional party power.

Key Regional Voting Patterns

Tamil Nadu: Dravidian parties (DMK, AIADMK) dominate; caste within Dravidian identity important

Andhra Pradesh/Telangana: TDP, YSRCP, BRS based on regional Telugu identity + caste

West Bengal: TMC dominance through Bengali identity + anti-BJP sentiment + social schemes

Odisha: BJD under Naveen Patnaik — Odia identity + development narrative

Punjab: SAD + BJP vs. Congress vs. AAP — agricultural + Sikh identity + urban–rural divide

Northeast: tribal identity, anti-Bengali sentiment, ethnic affiliations dominate

Language and Ethnicity

Language-based states (reorganisation 1956) reinforced linguistic voting identities

Demand for separate statehood (Gorkhaland, Bodoland, etc.) channels ethnic voting

Sub-regional identity: Purvanchal in UP, Saurashtra in Gujarat, Vidarbha in Maharashtra

Tribe-based voting in Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha — tribal parties like JMM

6. Economic Factors and Class Voting

Class and Economic Interest

Pure class-based voting (as in Western countries) is less prominent in India due to the salience of caste and religion. However, economic interests and class consciousness increasingly influence voting, particularly in urban areas and among youth.

Welfare Schemes and Voting

Welfare scheme-based voting (welfarism) has grown: MGNREGA, PDS, PM-Kisan, Jan Dhan, housing schemes influence rural voter behaviour

Research shows beneficiaries of government schemes tend to favour the ruling party (incumbency advantage through welfare)

Free electricity, water, ration, tablets — "revdi politics" debate; attracts poor voters

Economic distress (unemployment, inflation) triggers anti-incumbency — 2004 Congress win against NDA "India Shining" campaign

Urban vs. Rural Voting

Urban voters tend to be more issue-oriented, less caste-driven, and more influenced by national leadership and economic performance

Rural voters more influenced by caste, community leaders, local issues, and welfare delivery

Small-town/semi-urban voters: blend of caste identity + aspirational/development concerns

Farmer voter bloc: agri distress-driven voting; Punjab 2022 — AAP swept on anti-establishment rural wave

7. Other Factors

Gender and Voting

Historically, women turnout was lower than men's; the gap has narrowed and in many recent elections women turnout exceeded men's

Women's voting is often influenced by husband/family; however, independent women's vote is growing

Welfare schemes targeting women (Ujjwala, Jan Dhan, housing) impact women's electoral preferences

"Laxmi Bai vote" — women beneficiaries of targeted schemes voting for ruling parties

National Election Study 2019: women voted for BJP at higher rates than men in many states

Education, Youth, and First-Time Voters

Educated voters: more exposure to media, issues, and less pure identity voting

Youth vote (18–22): key swing factor; AAP's first major victory (Delhi 2015) driven partly by youth

First-time voters: aspirational, anti-corruption, development-oriented; attracted to new parties and leaders

Social media: dominant influence on youth voting (Facebook, WhatsApp, Twitter/X, YouTube)

Incumbency and Anti-Incumbency

India shows strong anti-incumbency effect at state level — ruling party loses in many elections

Central anti-incumbency less strong when strong national leader is present (e.g., Modi 2019)

Local MP/MLA performance matters: inaccessible or corrupt representatives face anti-incumbency even when party is popular

Anti-incumbency can be countered by welfare delivery, development works, and coalition management

Media and Social Media

Traditional media (TV, newspapers) shape agenda and opinion; news channels influential during campaigns

Social media (WhatsApp especially) used for viral political content, rumours, and micro-targeting

BJP's IT cell and Congress's digital war room: parties invest heavily in social media campaigns

Opinion polls and exit polls influence voter psychology (bandwagon effect)

Leadership and Charisma

Personalised leadership increasingly important: Indira Gandhi "Garibi Hatao" (1971), Rajiv Gandhi sympathy wave (1984), Modi wave (2014, 2019)

Presidential-style campaigning: focus on PM candidate rather than party

Chief Minister's image: Naveen Patnaik, Mamata Banerjee — built personal brands across party

Celebrity candidates: film stars, cricketers, social activists — attract votes independent of party
