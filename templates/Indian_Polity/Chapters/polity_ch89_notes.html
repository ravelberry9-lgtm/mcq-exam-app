Chapter 89 – NCRWC

National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution (2000–2002)

Section 1 — Background and Constitution of NCRWC
Why NCRWC was Constituted

After 50 years of the Constitution (Golden Jubilee 2000), perceived need to assess whether constitutional goals were being achieved

Rise of coalition politics, misuse of Art.356, frequent floor-crossing, judicial overreach concerns — prompted review

BJP election manifesto 1999 promised review of the Constitution; NDA government acted on it

Constituted By

Constituted on February 22, 2000 by the NDA government under PM Atal Bihari Vajpayee

Set up by an executive order (Cabinet Resolution) — NOT by an Act of Parliament or constitutional amendment

Chaired by Justice M.N. Venkatachaliah, former Chief Justice of India

Composition — 11 Members
Justice M.N. Venkatachaliah (Chair) Justice B.P. Jeevan Reddy Justice R.S. Sarkaria K. Punnayya P.A. Sangma Soli J. Sorabjee Subhash Kashyap Bhim Singh (resigned)
Opposition Boycott

Indian National Congress and Left parties boycotted the Commission, fearing it would tamper with secular/socialist features

Congress alleged NCRWC was cover to rewrite the Constitution in RSS/BJP image

Despite boycott, Commission held 24 full sittings and 13 regional consultations across India

Report Submitted

Report submitted on March 31, 2002 — two volumes + 5 consultation papers

Total: 249 recommendations covering all aspects of constitutional governance

NOT a new Constitution; reviewed working of existing Constitution and suggested amendments

Section 2 — Terms of Reference and Working
Terms of Reference (What NCRWC Was Asked to Do)

Examine and review working of the Constitution over 50 years

Consider changes necessary to make democracy more effective, responsive, and consistent with constitutional goals

Recommend changes to legislative-executive-judicial balance

NOT asked to draft a new Constitution or recommend replacement of parliamentary system

Key Constraints Given

Must not touch the basic structure of the Constitution (Kesavananda Bharati doctrine)

Must preserve parliamentary democracy, federalism, secularism, and judicial independence

Sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India to remain inviolable

Method of Working

24 full sittings (plenary sessions) of the Commission

13 regional consultations and public hearings across the country

Solicited views from political parties, constitutional experts, NGOs, and citizens

Published consultation papers inviting public comments before finalising recommendations

Consultation Papers Released
Electoral Reforms Fundamental Rights & DPSP Decentralisation & Local Bodies Centre-State Relations Judiciary
Section 3 — Recommendations: Parliament and Legislature
Constructive Vote of No-Confidence

NCRWC recommended adopting the German/Spanish model: constructive vote of no-confidence

A no-confidence motion against PM can succeed ONLY if simultaneously a new PM is named and supported by majority

Prevents governments from being toppled without alternative majority — addresses instability in coalition era

Smaller Council of Ministers

CoM should not exceed 10% of the strength of Lok Sabha (= ~54 members)

Later implemented: 91st Amendment 2003 set cap at 15% (NCRWC had recommended 10%)

Applies to both Union and States

Criminalisation of Politics

Persons charged with offences punishable by 5+ years should be disqualified from contesting elections

Trial of MPs/MLAs charged with offences should be conducted on day-to-day basis and concluded within 1 year

Special courts for elected representatives

Anti-Defection — Whip Restriction

Whip should be issued ONLY on two occasions: confidence/no-confidence motions AND money bills

For all other voting, members should vote freely according to conscience

Anti-defection law (Tenth Schedule) often stifles legislative debate and party member freedom

Anti-Defection — Speaker Replaced by ECI

Decision on disqualification under Tenth Schedule should be made by the Election Commission of India, NOT the Speaker

Removes conflict of interest: Speaker is a political office-holder deciding against members of rival faction

This recommendation has NOT yet been implemented

Simultaneous Elections

NCRWC recommended holding Lok Sabha and State Assembly elections simultaneously to reduce disruption

Revived as "One Nation One Election" by Kovind Committee 2024

Implementation requires constitutional amendments and coordination

Section 4 — Recommendations: Executive and Judiciary
Prime Minister — Role Clarity

PM's authority over CoM must be clearly stated in the Constitution

PM should have authority to ask a minister to resign; refusal should lead to dismissal

Collegium within Cabinet: sensitive matters of defence/finance/foreign to smaller inner Cabinet

National Judicial Commission (NJC)

Recommended establishing National Judicial Commission for appointment of judges to SC and HCs

NJC to include CJI, 2 senior SC judges, PM, and Leader of Opposition — ending collegium monopoly

Parliament enacted National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) by 99th Amendment 2014, but SC struck it down in 2015

All India Judicial Service (AIJS)

Recommended creation of All India Judicial Service for District Judges — similar to IAS/IPS

Centralised competitive examination; career service across states

Addresses quality inconsistency in subordinate judiciary; not yet implemented

Collegium System — Reform

Collegium (three seniormost SC judges deciding appointments since 1993 Second Judges case) lacks transparency

NCRWC wanted NJC to replace collegium with broader participation

As of 2024, collegium system still operates after NJAC was struck down

Independence of Judiciary

Security of tenure and service conditions of judges must not be altered to their disadvantage during tenure

Retirement age for HC judges: raise to 65 (same as SC) from current 62

National Litigation Policy to reduce government litigation in courts

Section 5 — Recommendations: Federalism and Centre-State
Article 356 — Reform of President's Rule

Art.356 has been most misused provision — imposed 100+ times since 1950 for political purposes

NCRWC: Art.356 should be used only as last resort; state legislature should not be dissolved before Parliament approves

Governor's report must include specific charges; Council of Ministers should have opportunity to be heard

Inter-State Council — Strengthening

Inter-State Council (Art.263) should be activated and meet regularly (at least twice a year)

Should function as a genuine forum for Centre-State dialogue

Sarkaria Commission recommendations on Centre-State relations should be fully implemented

Governor's Role

Governor should be appointed in consultation with the Chief Minister of the State

Governor should not be a person who has been active in politics in the recent past

Governor's discretionary powers (Art.163) should be exercised sparingly and constitutionally

Panchayati Raj and Urban Bodies

Devolution of funds, functions, and functionaries to PRIs must be genuine, not nominal

State Finance Commissions should be constituted timely and their recommendations implemented

District Planning Committees (Art.243ZD) to be constituted and empowered

Finances — Centre-State

Finance Commission should be made a permanent body

States should be given greater fiscal autonomy; concurrent list taxes need clearer division

Goods and Services Tax (GST) framework was implicitly called for to unify indirect taxes

Section 6 — Recommendations: Rights, Elections, Anti-Defection
Fundamental Duties — Enforceability

Fundamental Duties (Art.51A, added by 42nd Amendment 1976) should be made more effective

Verma Committee report on implementation of FDs should be acted upon

Education should instil duties and values in citizens from an early age

Right to Education

NCRWC recommended making Right to Education (6–14 years) a Fundamental Right under Art.21A

Implemented: 86th Constitutional Amendment 2002 inserted Art.21A; RTE Act 2009 followed

This was one of the most significant NCRWC recommendations to be implemented

Electoral Reforms Recommended

State funding of elections to reduce money power; candidates to declare assets and liabilities

NOTA (None of the Above) option on EVM ballot — implemented by Supreme Court in PUCL v. UOI 2013

Ceiling on election expenditure; CBI to be made independent of government

Right to Information

NCRWC recommended transparency in government functioning and a statutory right to information

Implemented: RTI Act 2005 — one of India's landmark governance reforms

NCRWC also recommended freedom of press as explicit fundamental right (currently implied under Art.19(1)(a))

NJAC — Tried but Struck Down

99th Amendment 2014 created National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) — based on NCRWC NJC recommendation

SC struck down NJAC in Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. UOI 2015 (4:1) — held it violated independence of judiciary (basic structure)

Collegium system restored; NCRWC's NJC recommendation effectively shelved

Key One-Liners

NCRWC ≠ Constituent Assembly; it reviewed, not replaced, the Constitution

Constructive vote of no-confidence = Germany model (Bundestag)

CoM 10% recommended → 15% implemented (91st Amendment 2003)

Anti-defection to ECI (not Speaker) — most important unimplemented electoral reform

86th Amendment (RTE) = most significant NCRWC recommendation implemented

NJAC 2014 implemented NJC idea → SC struck it down 2015 (basic structure)

NOTA implemented 2013 per NCRWC/SC direction; simultaneous elections still pending
