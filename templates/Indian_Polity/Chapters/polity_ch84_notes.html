Coalition Government

App Ch84 · Book Ch81 · Indian Polity (Lakshmikanth)

1. Meaning and Features

What is a Coalition Government?

A coalition government is formed when two or more political parties cooperate to form a government because no single party has an absolute majority in the legislature. The parties share power, portfolios, and a common minimum programme (CMP).

Types of Coalitions

Pre-poll alliance: Parties announce seat-sharing before elections (e.g., NDA, UPA alliances)

Post-poll coalition: Parties come together after election results to form government (e.g., 1979, 1996)

Minority government with outside support: Party forms government without coalition partners but with confidence of supporting parties from outside (e.g., Congress 1991–96, BJP 1998)

Common Minimum Programme (CMP): A negotiated document outlining policy consensus among coalition partners

Key Features of Indian Coalitions

Multiple parties sharing cabinet portfolios

Common Minimum Programme as policy basis

Coalition Coordination Committee to manage inter-party disputes

Frequent instability — withdrawal of support, no-confidence motions

Regional parties as kingmakers at the national level

2. Conditions for Coalition Politics in India

Why Coalition Politics?

Multi-party system: India has hundreds of registered parties; 6–8 national parties + 50+ state parties

FPTP electoral system: disproportionate representation; a party can win majority seats with 30–40% votes

Regional diversity: no single party can appeal uniformly across all regions, languages, and communities

Caste diversity: no caste is a national majority; coalition of caste groups needed

Post-Mandal OBC politics: fragmented votes across many regional OBC-based parties

Decline of Congress dominance from 1967 onwards (coalition governments in states)

Anti-incumbency at national level from 1977 onwards

Rajni Kothari's Analysis

Rajni Kothari identified the decline of the "Congress System" from the 1967 elections (when Congress lost several states) as the beginning of the era of coalition politics in India. The 1977 Lok Sabha election — the first defeat of Congress at the Centre — inaugurated national coalition governance.

3. Coalition Governments 1977–1989

Janata Party Government (1977–1979)

PM: Morarji Desai (March 1977 – July 1979)

First non-Congress government at the Centre

Anti-Emergency coalition of Bharatiya Lok Dal, Jana Sangh, Congress(O), Socialists, etc. merged to form Janata Party

Collapsed due to internal divisions over "dual membership" (Jana Sangh members' RSS links)

Charan Singh became PM (July 1979 – January 1980) with Congress(I) outside support; never faced Parliament; resigned before vote of confidence

Congress Dominance 1980–1989

Indira Gandhi won 1980 elections with a landslide (353/542 seats)

Rajiv Gandhi won 1984 elections with record majority (404/508 seats) — sympathy wave after Indira Gandhi's assassination

Congress ruled alone 1980–1989; no coalition at Centre

1989 elections began the era of coalition politics at Centre — Congress lost majority

4. Coalition Governments 1989–1999

V.P. Singh Government (1989–1990)

PM: V.P. Singh (December 1989 – November 1990)

National Front government — JD + regional parties

Outside support: BJP (left) and Left Front (right)

Mandal Commission implemented (1990) → BJP withdrew support → fell after Chandra Shekhar replaced him

Chandra Shekhar PM (November 1990 – June 1991) with Congress support; resigned after Congress withdrew support

P.V. Narasimha Rao Government (1991–1996)

PM: P.V. Narasimha Rao (June 1991 – May 1996)

Congress minority government — 244/543 seats

Survived full term through floor management and economic reforms (LPG — Liberalisation, Privatisation, Globalisation)

Manmohan Singh as Finance Minister — 1991 economic reforms

Not a true coalition but managed as a minority government

United Front Governments (1996–1998)

H.D. Deve Gowda — PM June 1996 – April 1997 (United Front, Congress outside support)

I.K. Gujral — PM April 1997 – March 1998 (United Front, Congress withdrew support)

Both governments fell due to Congress withdrawing outside support

Third Front experiment — regional and left parties forming government with Congress outside support but without Congress in cabinet

BJP-led NDA I (1998–1999)

PM: Atal Bihari Vajpayee (March 1998 – April 1999)

NDA — 13-party coalition; 13 months only

Fell when AIADMK (Jayalalithaa) withdrew support — lost no-confidence motion by 1 vote (269 vs. 270)

This was the only government to fall by a single vote in Indian parliamentary history

Pokhran II nuclear tests (May 1998) conducted during this tenure

5. NDA and UPA Coalitions (1999–2014)

NDA II — Vajpayee Government (1999–2004)

PM: Atal Bihari Vajpayee (October 1999 – May 2004)

First coalition government to complete a full 5-year term

NDA with 24 parties; core: BJP + TDP + Shiv Sena + SAD + JD(U) etc.

CMP adopted; key achievements: Kargil victory (1999), highway development (NHDP), Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan, POTA

Lost 2004 despite "India Shining" — anti-incumbency + rural disconnect

UPA I (2004–2009)

PM: Dr. Manmohan Singh (May 2004 – May 2009)

UPA — Congress + DMK + NCP + RJD + SP + Left Front (outside support)

Left withdrew support in 2008 over Indo-US nuclear deal; survived with SP support

Key legislation: RTI Act 2005, MGNREGA 2005, Right to Education Act 2009, Indo-US nuclear deal

National Advisory Council (NAC) under Sonia Gandhi — key policy body

UPA II (2009–2014)

PM: Dr. Manmohan Singh (May 2009 – May 2014)

Congress won 206 seats (improved); UPA with 322+ seats — less dependent on Left

Key legislation: Food Security Act 2013, Land Acquisition Act 2013, Lokpal Act 2013

Battered by corruption scandals: 2G spectrum, Coal scam, Commonwealth Games

Lost 2014 badly — Congress won only 44 seats

6. Post-2014 and Current Scenario

BJP Majority Governments (2014 onwards)

2014: BJP alone won 282/543 seats — first single-party majority since 1984 (Rajiv Gandhi). End of coalition compulsion for ruling party

NDA 2014: BJP 282 + allies = 336 seats

2019: BJP alone won 303/543 seats; NDA total = 353 seats — strengthened majority

2024: BJP won 240 seats — below majority of 272; NDA total = 293; coalition dependency returned. Allied parties: TDP (16 seats), JD(U) (12 seats) became crucial

PM Narendra Modi formed NDA government in June 2024 for third term; dependent on TDP and JD(U)

Opposition Coalitions

INDIA (Indian National Developmental Inclusive Alliance) formed in 2023 — opposition coalition of 26+ parties led by Congress

Key members: Congress, SP, TMC, AAP, DMK, JMM, RJD, NCP (Sharad Pawar), Shiv Sena (UBT)

INDIA alliance won 234 seats in 2024 Lok Sabha elections; Congress won 99 seats (best since 2009)

State-level INDIA vs NDA contests remain key battleground

7. Merits and Demerits

Merits of Coalition Government

Represents diverse political opinion and regional interests

Prevents authoritarian concentration of power in one party

Encourages consensus-based decision making

Regional parties get voice in national governance

Forces moderation of extremist positions (BJP moderated Hindutva agenda in coalitions)

Reflects federal character of India

Demerits of Coalition Government

Instability — frequent collapse of governments (1989–1999 saw 5 PMs in 10 years)

Policy paralysis — coalition partners block bold reforms

Blackmail by small parties — disproportionate leverage (e.g., DMK in UPA I over Sri Lanka policy)

Compromise on principles — CMP waters down party's ideology

Instability at states also; no-confidence motions, floor-crossing

Corruption: portfolio allocation to allies leads to corruption in respective ministries

Lack of clear accountability — blame-shifting between coalition partners
