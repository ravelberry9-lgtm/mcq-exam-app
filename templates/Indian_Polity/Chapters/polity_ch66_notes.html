Chapter 14 — Federal System (సమాఖ్య వ్యవస్థ)

Federal Features + Unitary Features | India = Quasi-Federal

0. Concept of Federalism

సమాఖ్య భావన

Federal = Two levels of government (Centre + States) with divided powers — each supreme in its sphere.

Unitary = Single central government; states subordinate or may not exist.

Indian Constitution: Neither purely federal nor purely unitary — described as "Quasi-Federal" (K.C. Wheare), "Cooperative Federalism" (Granville Austin), "Bargaining Federalism".

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar: India = "Union of States" — not a compact of independent states; states cannot secede.

Article 1: India = "Union of States" (not federation of states).

Term "Federation" not used in Constitution — deliberate choice.

1. Federal Features of the Constitution

సమాఖ్య లక్షణాలు

1. Dual Polity: Two governments — Centre (Union) + States; both derive authority from Constitution.

2. Written Constitution: Rigid, supreme, written document defining powers of both levels.

3. Division of Powers: Three Lists — Union List (97 subjects), State List (66 subjects), Concurrent List (47 subjects). 7th Schedule.

4. Supremacy of Constitution: Constitution is supreme — laws inconsistent with it are void.

5. Rigid Constitution: Certain amendments require special majority + state ratification (Art.368).

6. Independent Judiciary: SC as guardian of Constitution; adjudicates Centre-State disputes.

7. Bicameralism: Rajya Sabha represents states at Centre.

2. Unitary Features of the Constitution

ఏకీయ లక్షణాలు

1. Strong Centre: Union List has more subjects; residuary powers with Centre (Art.248).

2. States NOT indestructible: Parliament can alter/abolish states (Art.3) without their consent.

3. Single Constitution: States have no separate constitution (except J&K pre-Art.370 abrogation).

4. Single Citizenship: Only Indian citizenship — no separate state citizenship.

5. Governor: Centre-appointed; can withhold assent to state bills; State Emergency.

6. Integrated Judiciary: Single unified judicial system (SC → HC → lower courts).

7. All-India Services: IAS, IPS, IFS serve both Centre and States; controlled by Centre.

8. Emergency provisions: Federal character suspended during National Emergency.

9. Flexible Constitution: Most provisions amendable by Parliament alone.

10. Integrated Audit: Single CAG for both Centre and States.

3. Division of Powers (Three Lists)

అధికారాల విభజన

7th Schedule: Three Lists — Union, State, Concurrent.

Union List (List I): Originally 97 subjects → now 100; Defence, Foreign Affairs, Atomic Energy, Railways, Banking, etc. Only Parliament can legislate.

State List (List II): Originally 66 subjects → now 61; Police, Public Order, Agriculture, Local Govt, etc. Only State Legislature can legislate (normally).

Concurrent List (List III): Originally 47 subjects → now 52; Education, Forests, Criminal Law, Marriage, etc. Both Parliament and State Legislature can legislate.

Conflict on Concurrent List: Central law prevails (Art.254); unless State law received Presidential assent first.

Residuary Powers: Art.248 — Parliament has exclusive power on matters not in any List.

4. Centre's Control over States

కేంద్రం నియంత్రణ

Art.256: States shall ensure compliance with Parliament's laws — executive power of State extends to give effect to Centre laws.

Art.257: Centre can give directions to States for exercise of executive power.

Art.249: Parliament can legislate on State List subject in national interest (Rajya Sabha 2/3 resolution).

Art.250: Parliament can legislate on State List during National Emergency.

Art.252: Parliament can legislate for two or more states on request.

Art.253: Parliament can legislate on State List for implementing international treaties/agreements.

Art.356: President's Rule — Central takeover of state administration.

5. Critical Evaluation of Indian Federalism

విమర్శనాత్మక మూల్యాంకనం

K.C. Wheare: Indian Constitution is "Quasi-Federal" — more unitary in structure.

Granville Austin: "Cooperative Federalism" — Centre and States cooperate.

Ivor Jennings: India has "a federation with a strong centralising tendency".

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar: Constitution can be both unitary and federal depending on circumstances.

Paul Appleby: India has an "Extraordinarily Decentralised" system for a federal government.

Morris-Jones: Indian federation — "bargaining federalism."

6. Cooperative Federalism & Recent Trends

సహకార సమాఖ్యత

Inter-State Council (Art.263): Coordination body for Centre-State relations.

Finance Commission: Every 5 years; distributes tax revenues between Centre and States.

NITI Aayog: Replaced Planning Commission; promotes cooperative federalism.

GST Council: Cooperative federal body — Centre + States jointly decide GST rates.

Sarkaria Commission (1983-87): Reviewed Centre-State relations; recommended stronger cooperative federalism.

Punchhi Commission (2007-10): Reviewed Centre-State relations — recent comprehensive review.
