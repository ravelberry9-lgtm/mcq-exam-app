Chapter 68 — Judicial Activism (న్యాయ క్రియాశీలత)

Judicial Activism | Judges as Policy Makers | PIL | Suo Motu | India

0. Meaning & Origin of Judicial Activism

నిర్వచనం

Judicial Activism: Courts actively interpret Constitution/laws to promote justice beyond traditional judicial role.

Origin: USA — term coined by Arthur Schlesinger Jr. in Fortune Magazine (January 1947).

India: Activism emerged strongly in 1980s — post-Emergency, with expanded PIL jurisdiction.

Opposite of Judicial Restraint — where courts defer to legislature/executive decisions.

1. Judicial Activism vs Judicial Restraint

Activism vs Restraint

Judicial Activism: Courts proactively interpret laws, issue directives, protect rights even without formal petition.

Judicial Restraint: Courts limit role to strict textual interpretation; avoid policy matters.

Activism: judges as "living Constitution" interpreters; Restraint: original intent.

India largely follows activism — expanded FRs, PIL, suo motu cognizance.

2. Forms of Judicial Activism in India

రూపాలు

PIL (Public Interest Litigation): relaxed locus standi — any citizen can file for public interest.

Suo Motu cognizance: Courts take up cases on their own based on media reports, letters.

Expanded FR interpretation: Art.21 — right to life expanded to education, health, environment, livelihood.

Issuing guidelines: Vishaka (1997) — sexual harassment guidelines as law until Parliament acts.

Monitoring of implementation: courts continue jurisdiction to oversee compliance.

3. Landmark Cases of Judicial Activism

ముఖ్య కేసులు

Hussainara Khatoon (1979): Undertrial prisoners' right to speedy trial — Art.21.

Bandhua Mukti Morcha (1984): Bonded labour — SC issued detailed directions to govt.

MC Mehta v. Union of India (1987): Environmental protection — Ganga pollution, Taj Mahal industries.

Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997): Sexual harassment at workplace guidelines.

Vineet Narain (1998): CBI must act independently; CVC oversight.

PUCL v. Union of India (2003): Right to food as part of Art.21.

Olga Tellis v. BMC (1985): Right to livelihood as part of Art.21.

4. Judicial Activism & Art.21 Expansion

Article 21 విస్తరణ

Art.21: "No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."

Maneka Gandhi (1978): Expanded Art.21 — procedure must be fair, just, reasonable (not arbitrary).

Rights read into Art.21: right to education, health, environment, livelihood, privacy, speedy trial, legal aid, reputation.

Unnikrishnan (1993): Right to education up to 14 years = part of Art.21 (later codified as Art.21A by 86th Amendment 2002).

5. PIL & Suo Motu

PIL / Suo Motu

PIL: Started by Justice P.N. Bhagwati and Justice V.R. Krishna Iyer in late 1970s-80s.

Locus standi relaxed: even a postcard/letter can trigger PIL in India.

Suo Motu: Court acts on news reports — e.g., SC suo motu COVID-19 management 2021.

Limits of PIL: misuse for publicity; courts cautious about "PIL industry."

6. Criticism of Judicial Activism

విమర్శలు

Judicial Overreach: Courts entering legislative/executive domain — separation of powers violation.

Judges not elected — making policy decisions without democratic accountability.

Judicial legislation: Vishaka guidelines, BCCI reforms — Parliament's domain.

Docket explosion: PIL misuse increases court burden.

Inconsistency: different benches different approaches.
