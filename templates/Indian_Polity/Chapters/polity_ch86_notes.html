Pressure Groups

App Ch86 · Book Ch83 · Indian Polity (Lakshmikanth)

1. Meaning and Nature

Definition

A pressure group is an organised group of individuals who share common interests and seek to influence government policy and public decisions without directly seeking political power. Unlike political parties, they do not contest elections but try to influence those who do.

Alternative Names

Interest groups (most common in US political science)

Lobbying groups (focus on legislative lobbying)

Advocacy groups (focus on advocacy for a cause)

Civil society organisations

Non-Governmental Organisations (NGOs) — broader term

Trade unions, professional associations, business federations — institutional pressure groups

Key Scholars

Arthur Bentley — "The Process of Government" (1908) — first systematic study of pressure groups

David Truman — "The Governmental Process" (1951) — pluralist theory

Robert Dahl — pluralism — power dispersed among many groups

G.K. Wilson — comparative study of pressure groups

Allen Potter — classified groups into sectional and promotional

2. Pressure Groups vs. Political Parties

Key Differences

Aim: Pressure groups seek to influence power; political parties seek to exercise power

Elections: Pressure groups do NOT contest elections; political parties contest elections

Membership: Pressure groups serve a specific section; parties seek mass support

Programme: Pressure groups have a narrow specific interest; parties have broad manifestos

Accountability: Parties are accountable to voters; pressure groups accountable only to members

Overlap: Some pressure groups become parties (AIADMK evolved from a social movement; TDP from a film star's platform) and some parties maintain pressure group characteristics

Symbiotic Relationship

Pressure groups and political parties often work together: parties adopt pressure group demands to attract their support base; pressure groups back parties that champion their cause. In India, caste associations (Kshatriya Sabha, Maratha Mahasangh) work closely with aligned political parties.

3. Classification of Pressure Groups

Sectional (Interest) Groups

Represent specific sections of society — pursue the material interests of their members. Membership is restricted to the specific section.

Trade unions: INTUC (Indian National Trade Union Congress), AITUC, CITU, BMS (Bharatiya Mazdoor Sangh)

Business/industry: CII (Confederation of Indian Industry), FICCI (Federation of Indian Chambers of Commerce and Industry), ASSOCHAM

Farmers: AICC, Bharatiya Kisan Union (BKU), Swabhimani Shetkari Sanghatna

Professional: Bar Council of India, Indian Medical Association (IMA), All India Federation of University and College Teachers (AIFUCTO)

Promotional (Cause) Groups

Promote a particular cause rather than the interests of their own members. Membership is open to all who support the cause.

Environmental: Greenpeace India, Centre for Science and Environment (CSE), Chipko Movement

Consumer rights: Consumer Guidance Society of India, CGSI

Human rights: PUCL (People's Union for Civil Liberties), PUDR

Anti-corruption: India Against Corruption (Anna Hazare movement), Common Cause

Women's rights: National Alliance of Women (NAWO), Self Employed Women's Association (SEWA)

Other Classifications

Institutional groups: Operate within the government structure — military, bureaucracy, judiciary lobbying for institutional interests

Anomic groups: Spontaneous, unorganised groups — mobs, demonstrations, riots

Associational groups: Formally organised, have specific purpose — trade unions, professional associations

Non-associational groups: Informal, based on kinship, ethnicity, caste — caste associations, religious groups

Caste and Communal Groups (India-specific)

Caste associations: Kshatriya Mahasabha, Maratha Mahasangh, Nair Service Society (NSS), Ezhava Mahajana Sabha

Religious bodies: VHP (Vishwa Hindu Parishad), Jamaat-e-Islami, Church associations

Tribal organisations: Adivasi Mahasabha, tribal councils in Northeast

Linguistic groups: Tamil Sangam, Kannada Rakshana Vedike

4. Methods and Techniques

Direct Methods

Lobbying: Direct contact with legislators, ministers, bureaucrats to influence policy

Representations: Memoranda, deputations to government departments

Testimony: Appearing before Parliamentary/State Legislature committees

Litigation: Filing PILs in courts — PUCL filed PILs for NOTA, Right to Food, etc.

Campaign contributions: Electoral funding to sympathetic candidates/parties

Indirect Methods

Propaganda and media campaigns: Building public opinion through advertising, social media, press conferences

Demonstrations and protests: Rallies, dharnas, marches — Anna Hazare's Jan Lokpal fast (2011)

Strikes and bandhs: Trade union strikes, bandhs — INTUC, BMS general strikes

Boycotts: Swadeshi movement, farmer boycotts of government schemes

Forming public opinion: Research papers, reports, op-eds influencing elite opinion

Lobbying in India

Not legally regulated in India (unlike the US Lobbying Disclosure Act)

Nira Radia Tapes controversy (2010) exposed corporate lobbying networks in government

CII, FICCI and other industry bodies hold regular meetings with PM, Finance Minister before budgets

Law Commission and Parliamentary Standing Committees receive submissions from various groups

Business/Industrial Groups

FICCI (1927) — oldest major business federation; advocates for industry

CII — technical industry association; significant policy influence

ASSOCHAM — Associated Chambers of Commerce and Industry

NASSCOM — IT industry body; lobbied for IT sector-friendly policies in 1990s–2000s

PharmExcil — pharmaceutical export promotion

Trade Unions (Major)

INTUC (Indian National Trade Union Congress) — affiliated to Congress

CITU (Centre of Indian Trade Unions) — affiliated to CPM

BMS (Bharatiya Mazdoor Sangh) — affiliated to RSS/BJP ideological sphere

HMS (Hind Mazdoor Sabha) — socialist-oriented

Farmers' Organisations

Bharatiya Kisan Union (BKU) — Mahendra Singh Tikait (UP); led major 1988 New Delhi dharna

6. Role in Democracy — Merits and Demerits

Merits of Pressure Groups

Articulate and aggregate interests not captured by political parties

Check on governmental arbitrariness — vigilance function

Provide expertise and information to policy makers (CII, NASSCOM, IMA)

Democratic participation beyond elections — continuous citizen engagement

Educate public on issues — CSE's environmental campaigns, PUCL on civil liberties

Counter-balancing power — multiple groups prevent monopoly of influence

Strengthen federalism — regional pressure groups articulate state-level concerns

Demerits of Pressure Groups

Sectional interests may override public/national interest

Wealthy groups have disproportionate influence (corporate lobbying vs. poor communities)

May destabilise government through strikes, bandhs, blockades

Corruption and nexus with politicians and bureaucrats

Undemocratic — not elected, not accountable to public

Communal and caste-based groups can promote divisive identity politics

Elitist capture — educated, organised groups dominate while unorganised poor are underrepresented

7. Pressure Groups and Policy Process

Points of Access in Policy Making

Legislature: Parliamentary Standing Committees, Select Committees — groups give testimony

Executive: Direct access to PMO, Finance Ministry, line ministries through industry associations

Bureaucracy: Department consultations — groups invited to draft policy documents

Judiciary: Public Interest Litigation (PIL) — groups approach courts directly

Media: Building public pressure through sustained campaigns

Corporatism vs. Pluralism

Pluralism (Dahl, Bentley, Truman): Power dispersed among competing groups; government acts as neutral arbiter; best developed in US context

Corporatism: Government and major organised groups (labour, capital) institutionalised partnership in policy-making; prominent in European democracies

India: closer to pluralism but with some corporatist elements (CII/FICCI representation on government committees)

Notable Pressure Group Successes in India

RTI Act 2005 — MKSS and civil society campaign drove the Right to Information Act

Lokpal Act 2013 — Anna Hazare/IAC movement forced Parliament to enact Lokpal legislation

NOTA 2013 — PUCL's PIL led to NOTA on EVMs

Farm Laws Repeal 2021 — farmers' protest movement forced repeal of three farm laws

Forest Rights Act 2006 — tribal rights organisations and NGOs lobbied for Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act

Chipko Movement — influenced India's forest conservation policy in 1980s
