Chapter 73 — Law Commission of India

భారత న్యాయ కమిషన్ | Non-Statutory Advisory Body | Law Reform

0. Origin & Nature

నేపథ్యం

Law Commission: non-statutory advisory body — NOT established by law or Constitution.

British India: First Law Commission 1834 (Charter Act 1833) — headed by Lord Macaulay.

Post-independence: First Law Commission 1955 — constituted by Central Government.

22 Law Commissions in post-independence India (as of latest count).

Each commission: constituted by a government resolution for 3-year term.

1. Composition

సంఘటన

Chairman: retired judge of SC or HC (usually).

Members: legal experts, retired judges, academics.

Full-time + part-time members.

Member-Secretary: handles day-to-day administration.

Reconstituted every 3 years (fresh government resolution).

2. Functions & Reports

విధులు

Research and review of existing laws — recommend reforms.

Examine specific legal issues referred by Government.

Study international laws for India's adoption.

Reports submitted to Ministry of Law and Justice.

Reports are advisory — not binding on government.

Both suo motu reports and on-reference reports.

3. British Period Law Commissions

British Period

1st Law Commission (1834): Charter Act 1833 — Lord Macaulay (Chairman) — drafted Indian Penal Code (IPC 1860).

2nd Law Commission (1853): drafted CrPC, CPC, Indian Evidence Act.

3rd Law Commission (1861): reviewed earlier drafts.

4th Law Commission (1879): drafted Transfer of Property Act 1882.

4. Post-Independence Law Commissions

Post-Independence

1st Law Commission (1955): M.C. Setalvad (Chairman) — comprehensive review of Indian laws.

14th Law Commission: examined legal reforms on specific issues.

21st Law Commission (2015-2018): Uniform Civil Code, electoral reforms, death penalty.

22nd Law Commission (2020-): ongoing — various law reform issues.

Key reports: Uniform Civil Code, Sedition law review, death penalty review, electoral reforms.

5. Key Reports

ముఖ్య Reports

Report on Uniform Civil Code: recommended but politically sensitive.

Report on electoral reforms: recommendations adopted by Parliament.

Report on death penalty: recommended abolition for most offences.

Report on sedition (Sec.124A IPC): recommended review/modification.

248th Report: Amendments to Criminal Laws (arrest, bail).

6. Nature & Criticism

Nature / Criticism

Non-statutory: no permanent status; reconstituted by government every 3 years.

Advisory only: government not bound to accept recommendations.

Many recommendations not implemented.

Criticism: delayed law reforms; reports gather dust; no enforcement mechanism.

Strength: independent legal expertise; comprehensive research; non-partisan recommendations.
