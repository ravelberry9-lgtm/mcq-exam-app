Ch44 — జాతీయ మానవ హక్కుల కమిషన్ / NHRC

PHRA 1993 | Protection of Human Rights Act | Statutory Body | Paris Principles

Background & PHRA 1993
PHRA 1993: Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993 — established NHRC (statutory, NOT constitutional)
Amended: PHRA Amendment Act 2006 and 2019 (significant changes to composition)
UN Resolution 1993: Paris Principles (1991, adopted by UN 1993) set standards for NHRIs (National Human Rights Institutions)
Established: October 12, 1993
HQ: New Delhi (Manav Adhikar Bhavan)
Nature: Statutory body under PHRA; NOT under Art.12 (not "State" for FR purposes by itself)
International accreditation: "A" status accreditation by GANHRI (Global Alliance of NHRIs)
Composition — Pre & Post 2019 Amendment
Chairperson: Retired Chief Justice of India (CJI) — post 2019: also retired CJI OR retired SC judge
Members: Post 2019 — up to 5 members (2 from SC judges + 3 from other fields including women's rights/minority/children's rights)
Pre-2019: Chairperson (retired CJI) + 4 members (2 retired SC judges + 2 from human rights experience)
Ex-officio Members: Chairpersons of NCSC, NCST, NCBC, NCPCR, NCW, NCM, NCMEI — total 7
Secretary General: Chief executive officer; runs the NHRC secretariat
Special Rapporteurs: For specific human rights issues/investigations
Appointment, Tenure & Removal
Appointment: By President on recommendation of a 6-member Committee
Committee: PM (Chair) + Speaker LS + Home Minister + Leader of Opposition in LS + Leader of Opposition in RS + Deputy Chairman RS
Tenure: 3 years OR until age 70 (whichever earlier); eligible for re-appointment once
Post-retirement: NOT eligible for further employment by Central or State Government
Removal: By President on grounds of misbehaviour or incapacity; after SC inquiry (similar to SC judge removal)
Salary: Charged on Consolidated Fund of India (CFI) — like constitutional bodies
Jurisdiction & Powers
Human Rights definition (PHRA): Rights relating to life, liberty, equality and dignity guaranteed by Constitution or embodied in international covenants and enforceable by courts
Inquiry jurisdiction: Can inquire into human rights violations by state actors (Central + State governments, their agencies)
Armed Forces: NHRC can only seek a report from Central Government; CANNOT directly investigate armed forces (Section 19 PHRA)
Time limit: Cannot inquire into matters more than 1 year old (from date of complaint) — unless exceptional circumstances
Pending cases: Cannot inquire into matters pending before State Human Rights Commission or other commissions
Functions & Powers — Detailed
Inquiry powers (Civil Court): Summon, receive evidence, compel production of documents, examine witnesses on oath
Visit: Can visit any institution under Central/State Government (prisons, detention centres) for inspection
Review: Can review existing laws, safeguards, treaties — recommend remedial measures
Recommendations: Can recommend compensation to victims; remedial action by government; prosecution of officials
NOT binding: NHRC recommendations are NOT binding on government; only advisory
Can approach SC/HC: Can approach Supreme Court or High Court for orders/writs in cases before it
Intervene: Can intervene in any HC proceedings involving human rights
Spread awareness: Promote human rights literacy, research, publications
State Human Rights Commissions (SHRCs)
Establishment: State governments can establish SHRCs under PHRA (Section 21)
Chairperson: Retired Chief Justice of a High Court
Members: One member who is/was a HC judge
Appointment: By Governor on recommendation of a committee (CM, Speaker, Home Minister, Leader of Opposition in Assembly)
Jurisdiction: Can inquire into violations of ONLY State subjects (Entry II — State List); Central subjects → NHRC
Transfer: NHRC can transfer cases to SHRC; SHRC cannot transfer to NHRC
Not all States: Not all states have established SHRCs; it is not mandatory
Limitations & Criticisms
No binding power: Recommendations not binding; government can ignore
Armed forces: Cannot directly investigate armed forces violations — major limitation
1-year bar: Cannot inquire into complaints older than 1 year (unless exceptional circumstances)
"Toothless tiger": Called so because no enforcement power
Pending before other bodies: Cannot investigate if case pending in SHRC or other commissions
Jurisdiction gap: Private parties/non-state actors not directly covered (only state violations)
Compensation: Can only recommend; cannot directly award and enforce compensation
Key Points — Quick Reference
First Chairperson: Justice Ranganath Misra (1993-96)
Paris Principles: NHRIs must be — independent, pluralistic, have broad mandate, adequate resources
International covenants covered: ICCPR + ICESCR (International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights + Economic, Social and Cultural Rights)
NHRC vs Courts: NHRC is quasi-judicial; courts are judicial; NHRC faster + less formal
2019 Amendment: Allowed women's rights/minority/children's rights experts as members; broadened chairperson eligibility
