Electoral Reforms

App Ch82 · Book Ch79 · Indian Polity (Lakshmikanth)

1. Overview and Need for Reforms

Why Electoral Reforms?

India's electoral process, though functional, has faced chronic problems of criminalisation of politics, money power, booth capturing, voter intimidation, and misuse of government machinery. Electoral reforms aim to strengthen democracy and ensure free, fair, and impartial elections.

Key Problem Areas

Criminalisation: candidates with criminal records contesting elections

Money power: massive illegal expenditure beyond statutory limits

Booth capturing, impersonation, and voter intimidation

Misuse of official machinery and media by ruling party

Defection, horse-trading, and political corruption

Opaque political party funding and foreign donations

Reform Bodies and Reports

Tarkunde Committee (1974) — non-official; recommended proportional representation and ECI independence

Goswami Committee (1990) — official Parliamentary committee; 22 recommendations on candidates, deposits, disqualification

Indrajit Gupta Committee (1998) — state funding of elections; recommended partial state funding

Law Commission Reports — 170th (1999) and 255th (2015) on electoral reforms

NCRWC (2002) — National Commission to Review Working of Constitution; electoral reform recommendations

Second ARC (2008) — ethics in governance including elections

2. Pre-1990 Reforms

Tarkunde Committee (1974)

Formed under V.M. Tarkunde by Citizens for Democracy. A non-governmental initiative. Recommended: lowering voting age from 21 to 18, proportional representation for RS, appointment of ECI by a committee (not just President), and a powerful independent ECI. Most recommendations not immediately implemented.

Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990)

Parliamentary committee on electoral reforms chaired by Law Minister Dinesh Goswami. Key recommendations:

Sponsored election expenditure from government funds

Prohibition of ministers using official vehicles/aircraft for campaigning

Computerisation of electoral rolls

Restriction on exit polls

Disqualification for defectors to be decided by President/Governor on ECI advice

Earlier Legislative Milestones

1961 — Conduct of Elections Rules framed under RPA 1951

1966 — Election Symbols (Reservation and Allotment) Order introduced

1968 — Election Symbols Order finalised; model code of conduct concept emerged

1988 — 61st Amendment reduced voting age from 21 to 18 (Art.326)

1989 — Voters' photo identity cards scheme announced (not immediately implemented)

3. Post-1990 Reforms — T.N. Seshan Era

T.N. Seshan (CEC 1990–1996)

T.N. Seshan transformed the Election Commission from a passive to an assertive institution. He enforced the Model Code of Conduct (MCC) strictly for the first time, deployed observers widely, postponed elections when conditions were not fair, and introduced Electors' Photo Identity Cards (EPICs).

Model Code of Conduct (MCC)

No statutory basis — evolved through convention since 1960 (Kerala elections). Seshan made it binding in practice. Covers: general conduct, meetings, processions, polling day, polling booths, observers, and the party in power. Comes into force from announcement of election schedule; ceases on result declaration. Violations can lead to postponement of elections by ECI.

Electors' Photo Identity Cards (EPIC)

Announced by Seshan in 1993; nationwide rollout from 1995

Not compulsory — 12 alternative identity documents accepted at polling booth

Aim: eliminate bogus voting and impersonation

Supreme Court upheld ECI's authority to issue EPICs without legislative backing

ECI Initiatives Post-Seshan

Paid news monitoring — ECI tracks paid news and charges as election expenditure

cVIGIL app — citizens report MCC violations in real time (launched 2018)

Voter Helpline 1950 — 24×7 grievance redressal

National Voter's Day — January 25 (since 2011; ECI founded January 25, 1950)

SVEEP — Systematic Voters' Education and Electoral Participation programme

4. Constitutional and Statutory Amendments

Key Constitutional Amendments for Elections

44th Amendment 1978 — Art.329A struck down (added by 39th Amdt 1975 to protect Indira Gandhi's election)

52nd Amendment 1985 — Anti-Defection Law (Tenth Schedule)

61st Amendment 1988 — Voting age 21 → 18 (Art.326)

73rd/74th Amendments 1992 — Panchayat/Municipality elections; State Election Commissions created

91st Amendment 2003 — Council of Ministers max 15% of House strength; strengthened anti-defection

RPA Amendments for Reforms

1996 amendment — Sec.8: candidates convicted of certain offences disqualified; SC's Lily Thomas 2013 extended to sitting members

2002 amendment — Mandatory disclosure of criminal antecedents, assets/liabilities, and educational qualifications by candidates (affidavit with nomination)

Sec.33B struck down by SC 2003 (ADR case) — Parliament cannot override SC's disclosure mandate

2003 amendment — Sec.77: free supply of voters' list and electoral rolls to candidates; expenses ceiling revised

Supreme Court Judgements on Electoral Reforms

ADR v. Union of India 2002 — candidates must disclose criminal record, assets, education in affidavits

Lily Thomas v. Union of India 2013 — convicted MPs/MLAs immediately lose seat; no protection pending appeal

PUCL v. Union of India 2013 — introduced NOTA (None of the Above) on EVMs

Abhiram Singh v. C.D. Commachen 2017 — appealing for votes on basis of religion, caste, language, community is corrupt practice under Sec.123(3)

Electoral Bonds case 2024 — SC unanimously struck down Electoral Bond Scheme as unconstitutional (violates Art.19(1)(a) right to information)

5. EVM, VVPAT, and Technology Reforms

Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs)

First used: 1982 — Kerala Parur constituency (70 booths); SC stayed the results (no legal provision then)

Legal basis: RPA 1951 amended in 1989 to allow EVMs

Limited use: 1998 (Rajasthan, MP, Delhi assembly elections)

Nationwide: 2004 Lok Sabha elections

Manufactured by: BEL (Bangalore) and ECIL (Hyderabad) — both PSUs

Consists of: Ballot Unit (BU) + Control Unit (CU) + VVPAT

VVPAT (Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail)

First used: 2013 Nagaland Noksen constituency (bye-election)

Nationwide: 2019 Lok Sabha elections

Slip visible for 7 seconds then drops into sealed box

2019 SC order: 5 EVMs per constituency to be matched with VVPAT

2024 SC (Writ Petition by ADR): dismissed demand for 100% VVPAT counting; upheld current EVM-VVPAT system

Other Technology Initiatives

Online voter registration — Form 6, 7, 8 online via Voter Portal (voterportal.eci.gov.in)

Electoral Roll Management System (ERMS) — integrated database

Suvidha portal — candidates' permission requests online

Results portal — real-time counting data (results.eci.gov.in)

National Electoral Roll Purification and Authentication Programme (NERPAP)

6. Campaign Finance and Money Power Reforms

Election Expenditure Limits

Lok Sabha: Rs.95 lakh (large states); Rs.75 lakh (small states/UTs) — revised 2022

State Assembly: Rs.40 lakh (large states); Rs.28 lakh (small states) — revised 2022

Sec.77 RPA 1951: candidate must maintain accounts; submit within 30 days of result

Sec.10A: ECI can disqualify for failure to lodge accounts (3 years)

Paid news: if not included in accounts, treated as corrupt practice

Political Party Funding

Sec.13A Income Tax Act — political parties exempt from income tax on donations

Anonymous donations: only up to Rs.20,000 per donor (cash); above Rs.2,000 must be by cheque/digital — 2017 amendment

FCRA: foreign donations to political parties banned

Electoral Bonds: Finance Act 2017; purchasable from SBI; anonymous donation to parties; SC struck down February 15, 2024

State funding of elections — Indrajit Gupta Committee recommended; not yet implemented fully

Corporate Donations

Companies Act 1956 (original): companies could donate up to 5% of average net profits of 3 years; director disclosure required

Companies Act 2013 Sec.182: 7.5% of average net profits; prior board approval; disclosed in profit and loss account

Finance Act 2017 removed the 7.5% cap on corporate donations — allowed unlimited via Electoral Bonds

SC's 2024 Electoral Bonds judgment: struck down unlimited cap removal, ordered SBI to disclose all bond data

7. Recent Reforms (2000s–2020s)

2000s Reforms

2003 — Mandatory affidavit disclosures by candidates (criminal record, assets, education)

2004 — Nationwide EVM deployment for Lok Sabha elections

2009 — Re-polling provisions strengthened; postal ballot extended to more categories

2010 — NRI voting rights; NRIs can enrol in constituency of their passport address

2010s Reforms

2013 — NOTA introduced on EVMs (PUCL judgment)

2013 — VVPAT first used (Nagaland)

2013 — Lily Thomas: convicted lawmakers disqualified immediately

2014 — 16-digit AADHAAR-Voter ID linkage initiative started (voluntary)

2018 — cVIGIL app launched for real-time MCC violation reporting

2019 — VVPAT nationwide; 5 VVPAT per constituency counted (SC order)

2020s Reforms

2021 — Election Laws (Amendment) Act: Aadhaar-Voter ID linking voluntary; 4 enrollment dates per year; service voter provisions updated

2022 — Expenditure limits revised upward (LS: Rs.95 lakh)

2023 — ECI reconstitution: Chief Election Commissioner and other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act 2023; selection committee: PM + Leader of Opposition + Cabinet Minister (Law Minister); removed CJI from panel

2024 — SC struck down Electoral Bond Scheme (ADR case, CJI Chandrachud bench)

2024 — SC upheld CEC/EC appointment law as constitutional (separate petition)

Pending/Proposed Reforms

One Nation One Election (simultaneous elections) — High Level Committee under Kovind (2024 report) recommended; implementation pending

State funding of elections — recommended by Indrajit Gupta Committee and Law Commission but not implemented

Proportional representation for Rajya Sabha — Tarkunde Committee recommendation; not implemented

Inner-party democracy — Law Commission recommended statutory provisions; not implemented

Right of recall — proposed but opposed; not implemented

Negative/rejection vote (beyond NOTA which has no effect) — proposed; not implemented
