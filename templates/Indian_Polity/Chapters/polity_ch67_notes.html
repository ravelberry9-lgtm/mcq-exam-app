Chapter 27 — Judicial Review (న్యాయసమీక్ష)

Power of courts to examine constitutionality of laws | Art.13, 32, 226, 246

0. Meaning & Origin

అర్థం మరియు మూలం

Judicial Review = Power of courts to examine laws/executive actions and declare them void if they violate the Constitution.

Origin: USA — Marbury v. Madison (1803) — Chief Justice John Marshall established judicial review.

India: Not explicitly mentioned in Constitution but implied in Art.13, 32, 136, 143, 226, 246.

Art.13: Laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights are void — basis of judicial review of legislation.

Indian judicial review: Narrower scope than USA (Parliament's supremacy in some areas); but broader after Basic Structure doctrine.

1. Constitutional Provisions

రాజ్యాంగ నిబంధనలు

Art.13: Pre-constitutional laws inconsistent with FRs void; State shall not make laws abridging FRs.

Art.32: SC can issue writs for enforcement of FRs — right to move SC itself is a FR.

Art.226: HC can issue writs for FRs and other legal rights.

Art.246: Distributes legislative powers — forms basis for reviewing whether laws are within competence.

Art.131: SC has original jurisdiction in Centre-State and inter-State disputes.

Art.136: SC's special leave to appeal — can review any court's decision.

Art.143: President can seek SC's advisory opinion (consultative jurisdiction).

2. Scope of Judicial Review in India

పరిధి

1. Constitutionality of legislative enactments (Central + State).

2. Constitutionality of executive actions.

3. Enforcement of Fundamental Rights.

4. Review of amendments to Constitution (after Kesavananda Bharati 1973 — Basic Structure doctrine).

NOT subject to judicial review: 9th Schedule laws (added pre-1973) — but 9th Schedule laws added after Kesavananda CAN be reviewed (I.R. Coelho case 2007).

Political questions (Speaker's decision on disqualification under 10th Schedule had limited review historically — now reviewable after Kihoto Hollohan).

3. Importance of Judicial Review

ప్రాముఖ్యత

1. Protects Fundamental Rights of citizens.

2. Maintains supremacy of Constitution.

3. Maintains federal balance between Centre and States.

4. Prevents abuse of power by legislature and executive.

5. Protects independence of judiciary.

6. Acts as check and balance in constitutional system.

4. Landmark Cases

ముఖ్యమైన కేసులు

Marbury v. Madison (1803, USA): First judicial review case — Marshall CJ established principle.

A.K. Gopalan (1950): SC upheld preventive detention law — narrow interpretation of FRs.

Shankari Prasad (1951): Constitutional amendments can abridge FRs — Parliament can amend Art.13.

Golaknath (1967): Parliament CANNOT amend FRs — reversed Shankari Prasad; 11-judge bench.

Kesavananda Bharati (1973): Basic Structure doctrine — Parliament can amend Constitution but cannot destroy basic structure; 13-judge bench.

Minerva Mills (1980): Reaffirmed Basic Structure; Art.31C (DPSP over FRs) limited.

Maneka Gandhi (1978): Expanded Art.21 — procedure must be fair, just and reasonable.

I.R. Coelho (2007): 9th Schedule laws added after Kesavananda (1973) can be judicially reviewed.

5. Limitations of Judicial Review

పరిమితులు

1. Parliament can amend Constitution (except Basic Structure) — can reverse SC judgements.

2. 9th Schedule: Laws added before 1973 not reviewable (partially).

3. SC cannot review all political questions.

4. Courts cannot question wisdom of policy — only constitutionality.

5. Budget and financial matters — limited review.

6. President's satisfaction (Art.352, 356) — courts can review but limited grounds.

6. Judicial Review vs Judicial Supremacy

తేడా

Judicial Review: Courts can examine and void unconstitutional laws/actions — does NOT mean courts are supreme.

India: No doctrine of Parliamentary Supremacy (like UK) nor absolute Judicial Supremacy (like some interpret USA).

India: Constitutional Supremacy — Parliament + SC both bound by Constitution.

Doctrine of Harmonious Construction: Courts try to reconcile apparently conflicting provisions.

Doctrine of Severability: If part of a law is unconstitutional, only that part is void; rest survives.

Doctrine of Eclipse: Pre-constitutional law that violates FR is not void but eclipsed — becomes effective if FR is amended to permit it.

Key Facts

Art.32: SC writs (FR = FR itself)

Art.226: HC writs

Kesavananda Bharati 1973: Basic Structure
