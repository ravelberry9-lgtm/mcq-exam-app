Introduction — What is Chemistry about? రసాయన శాస్త్రం పరిచయం
EN Chemistry is the branch of science that deals with the composition, structure, properties, and changes of matter. Everything around us — the air we breathe, the food we eat, the metals we use, the medicines we take — is made up of chemical substances. General Science questions for APPSC and TSPSC examinations draw heavily from topics such as the classification of matter, the properties of metals and non-metals, the Periodic Table, important alloys and their compositions, common chemical names and formulas, and the abundances of elements in the Earth's crust. A thorough understanding of these topics, combined with awareness of common exam traps, is essential for scoring well. This combined notes file covers all three chapters (Ch 01, Ch 02, and Ch 05) in a single, textbook-quality, bilingual reference document.

Classification of Matter పదార్థ వర్గీకరణ
EN All matter in the universe can be classified into three fundamental categories: elements, compounds, and mixtures. An element is the simplest form of matter, consisting of only one type of atom. It cannot be broken down further by ordinary chemical methods. There are 118 elements known to science, of which 92 occur naturally on Earth and the remaining 26 are synthetic (man-made). A compound is formed when two or more elements combine chemically in a fixed ratio by mass. The properties of a compound are entirely different from those of its constituent elements. A mixture is formed when two or more substances are physically combined in any proportion; the components retain their individual properties and can be separated by physical means.

EXAM TRAP — Air, Alloys and Sea Water are MIXTURES, not Compounds
Air is a mixture of nitrogen (78%), oxygen (21%), argon (0.9%), and carbon dioxide (0.04%) — its composition can vary with altitude and location. Brass (copper and zinc) and Stainless Steel (iron, chromium, nickel) are alloys, which are mixtures, not compounds. Sea water is a mixture of water and dissolved salts. A very common examination question asks: "Is air an element, compound, or mixture?" The correct answer is always mixture.
గాలి, మిశ్రమ లోహాలు (Alloys), సముద్రపు నీరు — ఇవన్నీ మిశ్రమాలు (Mixtures). సమ్మేళనాలు కాదు!
Homogeneous and Heterogeneous Mixtures / సజాతీయ మరియు విజాతీయ మిశ్రమాలు
EN Mixtures are further classified as homogeneous (uniform composition throughout — e.g., salt water, air, alloys) and heterogeneous (non-uniform composition — e.g., sand in water, oil in water). Solutions are homogeneous mixtures. A colloid is intermediate — particles are between 1–1000 nm and scatter light (Tyndall effect). Milk, fog, smoke, and blood are colloids.

Metals versus Non-Metals లోహాలు వర్సెస్ అలోహాలు
EN Metals are elements that are lustrous (shiny), malleable (can be beaten into sheets), ductile (can be drawn into wires), good conductors of heat and electricity, and sonorous (produce a ringing sound when struck). Chemically, metals react with acids to produce hydrogen gas and form basic oxides. Non-metals are generally brittle (when solid), poor conductors of electricity (with the notable exception of graphite), and form acidic or neutral oxides. Metalloids (or semimetals) such as silicon (Si), germanium (Ge), and arsenic (As) possess intermediate properties — they are semiconductors. The most ductile metals are gold, silver, and copper. The most malleable metal is gold. Cast iron is brittle because of its very high carbon content (2–4%).
TE లోహాలు మెరుపు (lustrous), పేపర్ వలె సాగే (malleable), తీగలుగా సాగే (ductile), ఉష్ణం మరియు విద్యుత్ వాహకాలు, మరియు చప్పుడు చేసే (sonorous) లక్షణాలు కలిగి ఉంటాయి. రసాయనంగా, లోహాలు ఆమ్లాలతో చర్య పొంది హైడ్రోజన్ వాయువును విడుదల చేస్తాయి మరియు ఆల్కలైన్ ఆక్సైడ్లు ఏర్పరుస్తాయి. అలోహాలు పెళుసైనవి, పేద విద్యుత్ వాహకాలు (గ్రాఫైట్ మినహా), మరియు ఆమ్ల లేదా తటస్థ ఆక్సైడ్లు ఏర్పరుస్తాయి. మెటలాయిడ్లు (Si, Ge, As) మధ్యంతర ధర్మాలు కలిగి ఉన్న అర్ధవాహకాలు.

Carbon and Its Allotropes కార్బన్ మరియు దాని రూపాంతరాలు
EN Carbon is unique among elements because it forms several allotropes — different physical forms of the same element with different structural arrangements and properties. The four major allotropes of carbon are diamond, graphite, fullerene, and carbon nanotubes. Diamond has a three-dimensional tetrahedral network structure in which each carbon atom is covalently bonded to four other carbon atoms. This makes diamond the hardest naturally occurring substance (Mohs hardness 10). Because all four valence electrons of each carbon atom are used in bonding, there are no free electrons, and diamond does not conduct electricity — it is an electrical insulator. Graphite has a layered hexagonal structure; each carbon atom bonds to three others in the same plane, leaving one free electron per atom that can move between layers and conduct electricity. Graphite is soft because the layers are held together only by weak van der Waals forces and can slide over each other easily — making it an excellent lubricant and the material in pencils.

EXAM TRAP — Diamond does NOT conduct electricity; Graphite DOES
Students often assume that since both diamond and graphite are forms of pure carbon, they should behave similarly. They do not. Diamond is a perfect electrical insulator because all four valence electrons of each carbon atom are engaged in bonding, leaving no free electrons. Graphite is a good electrical conductor because one electron per carbon atom remains free and can move through the layers. This is one of the most frequently tested distinctions in competitive exams.

Silicon — The Semiconductor Element సిలికాన్ — అర్ధవాహక మూలకం
EN Silicon (Si) is the second most abundant element in the Earth's crust, constituting approximately 28% by mass (oxygen is first at 46%). Silicon exists in the form of silicon dioxide (SiO₂) — commonly known as sand, quartz, and glass — and as silicate minerals in rocks. Pure silicon is a semiconductor and is the foundational material for modern electronics, including transistors, integrated circuits, and solar cells. Germanium (Ge) was the original semiconductor material used in early transistors before silicon took over. Silicon carbide (SiC), known as carborundum, has a hardness of 9–9.5 on the Mohs scale and is used as an industrial abrasive for cutting very hard substances.

Noble Gases — Group 18 నోబెల్ వాయువులు — 18వ గ్రూపు
EN The noble gases (also called inert gases or rare gases) occupy Group 18 — the rightmost column — of the Periodic Table. They are: Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar), Krypton (Kr), Xenon (Xe), and Radon (Rn). All noble gases have completely filled outermost electron shells — helium has 2 electrons (duplet), and all others have 8 electrons (octet) in the outermost shell. This complete configuration makes them chemically inert — they do not form bonds under ordinary conditions, do not react with other elements, and exist as monoatomic gases. Radon is radioactive. Helium is the second most abundant element in the universe (after hydrogen) and was first detected in the spectrum of the Sun's chromosphere in 1868, before being isolated on Earth.

Types of Iron and Steel — Carbon Content ఇనుము మరియు స్టీల్ రకాలు — కార్బన్ శాతం
EN Iron is the most widely used metal in industry and the most abundant metal in the whole Earth (including the iron core). The properties of iron and its alloys (steels) are determined primarily by the percentage of carbon they contain. As carbon content increases, iron becomes harder but also more brittle. Wrought iron (less than 0.1% carbon) is the purest and most malleable form of iron. Mild steel (0.1–0.3% C) is the most widely used grade for construction and general manufacturing. High-carbon steel (0.3–1.5% C) is used for cutting tools. Cast iron (2–4% C) is very hard but brittle and used for pipes and engine blocks. Pig iron is the impure product from a blast furnace containing carbon (2.5–4.5%), sulphur, phosphorus, silicon, and manganese, and is the raw material for all other iron products.

Galvanisation and Corrosion / గాల్వనీకరణ మరియు తుప్పు
EN Galvanisation is the process of coating iron or steel with a thin layer of zinc (Zn) to protect it from rusting. Zinc is more reactive than iron and acts as a sacrificial anode — even if the zinc layer is scratched and the iron is exposed, the zinc continues to corrode preferentially, protecting the iron underneath. This makes galvanised iron highly durable in outdoor and marine environments. Rust forms when iron is exposed to both oxygen and moisture simultaneously. The chemical composition of rust is hydrated iron(III) oxide: Fe₂O₃·xH₂O. Rusting requires both water AND oxygen; if either is absent, rusting does not occur.
TE గాల్వనీకరణ అనేది ఇనుము లేదా స్టీల్ పై జింక్ (Zn) పలుచని పొర పూసే ప్రక్రియ. జింక్ ఇనుము కంటే చురుకుగా ఉన్నందున, జింక్ ముందు తుప్పు పడుతుంది మరియు ఇనుమును రక్షిస్తుంది. తుప్పు ఏర్పడాలంటే నీరు మరియు ఆక్సిజన్ రెండూ అవసరం. తుప్పు = Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (జల ఐరన్ ఆక్సైడ్).
EXAM ALERT — Wrought Iron is PUREST; Pig Iron has MOST Carbon
A very common exam trap: "Which form of iron is purest?" Answer = Wrought Iron (less than 0.1% C). "Which has the most carbon?" Answer = Pig Iron (2.5–4.5% C). Pig iron is the most brittle and impure; wrought iron is the most malleable. Cast iron and pig iron are often confused but both have high carbon content and are brittle.

Bleaching Agents and Special Gases బ్లీచింగ్ ఏజెంట్లు మరియు ప్రత్యేక వాయువులు
EN A bleaching agent is a substance that removes colour from fabrics, paper, or other materials by oxidation or reduction. The common bleaching agents are sulphur dioxide (SO₂), chlorine (Cl₂), sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl), hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂), and bleaching powder Ca(OCl)Cl. Sulphur dioxide is a reducing bleaching agent used specifically for delicate fabrics like silk and wool — its bleaching action is reversible (the colour returns on exposure to air). Chlorine and bleaching powder are oxidising bleaching agents used for cotton fabrics — their action is irreversible. Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is NOT a bleaching agent — it is commonly listed as a wrong option in examinations. The formation of bleaching powder involves passing chlorine gas over slaked lime: Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → Ca(OCl)Cl + H₂O.

Greenhouse Gases and Special Atmospheric Facts / గ్రీన్‌హౌస్ వాయువులు
EN Greenhouse gases trap infrared radiation from the Earth's surface and cause global warming. The main greenhouse gases are: carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), nitrous oxide (N₂O), chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), and water vapour (H₂O). Hydrogen (H₂) is frequently listed as a wrong option — it is not a greenhouse gas. The atmosphere is composed of: Nitrogen 78%, Oxygen 21%, Argon 0.9%, CO₂ 0.04%. Nitrogen is the most abundant gas in the atmosphere. The most abundant element in the entire universe is Hydrogen.

The Periodic Table — Structure, Trends, and Groups ఆవర్తన పట్టిక — నిర్మాణం, ధోరణులు
EN Mendeleev's Periodic Table (1869) arranged elements in order of increasing atomic mass. Elements with similar chemical properties were placed in the same vertical column (group). Mendeleev left gaps for elements not yet discovered and predicted their properties. Eka-aluminium (predicted) was later identified as Gallium (Ga); eka-silicon was identified as Germanium (Ge). Moseley's Modern Periodic Law (1913) states that the properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic number (not mass). The modern Periodic Table has 7 periods (horizontal rows) and 18 groups (vertical columns). The total number of known elements is 118. The elements are divided into s-block (Groups 1–2), p-block (Groups 13–18), d-block (Groups 3–12, transition metals), and f-block (lanthanides and actinides, placed below the main table).

Periodic Trends / ఆవర్తన ధోరణులు
EN Going down a group: Atomic size increases (more electron shells are added); metallic character increases; ionisation energy decreases; reactivity of metals increases (most reactive alkali metal = Cs or Fr). Going across a period (left to right): Atomic size decreases (nuclear charge increases, pulling electrons closer); ionisation energy increases; electronegativity increases; metallic character decreases and non-metallic character increases.

Coal Types and Calorific Values బొగ్గు రకాలు మరియు ఉష్ణ విలువలు
EN Coal is a fossil fuel formed from the remains of ancient plants buried under sediment over millions of years. The grade of coal depends on its carbon content — a process called coalification. As coal matures over geological time, carbon content increases and the moisture content decreases, resulting in a higher calorific value. The four main grades of coal in increasing order of carbon content are: Peat, Lignite, Bituminous, and Anthracite. Firedamp is methane (CH₄) released from coal seams in underground mines. When mixed with air in a ratio of 5–15%, methane forms an explosive mixture — the cause of most coal mine explosions.

EXAM TRAP — Oxygen is Most Abundant in Crust; Iron is Most Abundant in Whole Earth
A very common question asks: "Which is the most abundant element in the Earth?" If the question says "Earth's crust" the answer is Oxygen. If the question says "whole Earth" or "Earth including its core", the answer is Iron (Fe). Students who confuse these two will lose marks. The most abundant metal specifically in the crust is Aluminium.

The Reactivity Series of Metals లోహాల చురుకుదనం శ్రేణి
EN The Reactivity Series (also called the Activity Series) arranges metals in decreasing order of their tendency to react — specifically, to lose electrons and form positive ions. A more reactive metal will displace a less reactive metal from its salt solution. For example, zinc displaces copper from copper sulphate solution because Zn is above Cu in the series. Gold and platinum, at the very bottom of the series, are called noble metals because they do not react with common acids, do not corrode, and do not tarnish in air. The position of hydrogen in the series is used as a reference: metals above hydrogen react with dilute acids to produce H₂ gas; metals below hydrogen (Cu, Hg, Ag, Au, Pt) do not react with dilute acids to give H₂.

EXAM ALERT — Displacement Reactions and the Reactivity Series
If asked "which metal is displaced by zinc from a solution of its salt?", think: Zinc is above Fe, Ni, Sn, Pb, Cu, Hg, Ag, Au in the reactivity series — so Zn can displace all of these. A classic exam question: "Copper vessel turns green when exposed to moist air" — this is due to formation of basic copper carbonate (malachite) CuCO₃·Cu(OH)₂. Gold does not react with single acids, only with Aqua Regia (3 parts HCl + 1 part HNO₃).

EXAM TRAP — Azurite is Copper Ore, NOT Aluminium Ore
Azurite (Cu₃(CO₃)₂(OH)₂) is a blue copper carbonate mineral — it is an ore of Copper, not Aluminium. The ores of Aluminium are Bauxite, Cryolite (Na₃AlF₆), and Feldspar. Similarly, Cassiterite (SnO₂) is the ore of Tin — not Iron. A frequent trap question: "Which of the following is NOT an ore of iron?" — then listing Haematite, Magnetite, Limonite, and Cassiterite; the answer is Cassiterite (tin ore).

EXAM ALERT — Pearls and Quartz
Pearls are composed mainly of Aragonite (CaCO₃) — a form of calcium carbonate — and a protein called Conchiolin. Aragonite is an orthorhombic polymorph of CaCO₃ (calcite is the trigonal form). Quartz crystals are chemically silicon dioxide (SiO₂), are piezoelectric, and are used in quartz clocks and watches.

Hydrogen Isotopes and Heavy Water / హైడ్రోజన్ ఐసోటోప్‌లు మరియు భార జలం
EN Hydrogen has three isotopes: Protium (¹H) — the ordinary hydrogen with one proton and no neutrons, mass number 1; Deuterium (²H or D) — one proton and one neutron, mass number 2; Tritium (³H or T) — one proton and two neutrons, mass number 3 (radioactive). Heavy water (D₂O) is formed from deuterium and oxygen. It is used as a moderator in nuclear reactors to slow down neutrons. Tritium oxide (T₂O) is called super-heavy water — it is not the same as heavy water.
TE హైడ్రోజన్ మూడు ఐసోటోప్‌లు: ప్రోటియం (¹H) — సాధారణ H, ద్రవ్యరాశి సంఖ్య 1; డ్యుటీరియం (²H) — ద్రవ్యరాశి 2; ట్రిటియం (³H) — ద్రవ్యరాశి 3, రేడియోధర్మి. భార జలం (D₂O) = అణు రియాక్టర్లలో మాడరేటర్. ట్రిటియం ఆక్సైడ్ (T₂O) = అతి భార జలం — భార జలం కాదు.
Annealing and Physical Properties of Glass / ఎన్నీలింగ్ మరియు గాజు ధర్మాలు
EN Annealing is the controlled process of slowly cooling hot glass (or metal) after it has been formed, to relieve internal stresses and prevent cracking or shattering. If glass is cooled rapidly after shaping, temperature gradients create internal stresses that make the glass brittle and prone to spontaneous fracture. Annealed glass is much stronger and more durable. Tempered glass is a different process — the glass is cooled rapidly by air jets, creating a compressive stress on the surface and making it approximately four times stronger than ordinary glass. Glass is brittle by nature (amorphous solid) and does not have a defined melting point — it softens gradually over a range of temperatures.
TE ఎన్నీలింగ్ వేడి గాజును లేదా లోహాన్ని నెమ్మదిగా చల్లబరిచే ప్రక్రియ — లోపలి ఒత్తిళ్లు తొలగుతాయి; గాజు పగలకుండా ఉంటుంది. వేగంగా చల్లబరిస్తే అంతర్గత ఒత్తిళ్లు ఏర్పడి గాజు పెళుసవుతుంది. టెంపర్డ్ గాజు (వేగంగా గాలి శీతలీకరణ) సాధారణ గాజు కంటే 4 రెట్లు బలంగా ఉంటుంది. గాజు నిరాకార ఘనం — నిర్ణీత ద్రవీభవన స్థానం లేదు.

  • Carbon allotropes: Diamond (hardest, insulator) | Graphite (conductor, lubricant) | Fullerene C₆₀ (drug delivery) | Carbon nanotubes (strongest).
  • Silicon: 28% of crust; SiO₂ = sand/glass; SiC = carborundum (abrasive); used in transistors with Ge.
  • Noble gases (Group 18): He (balloons), Ne (red signs), Ar (bulbs), Kr (lamps), Xe (flash), Rn (radioactive).
  • Alloys: Brass=Cu+Zn | Bronze=Cu+Sn | Bell Metal=Cu+Sn (high Sn%) | Gunmetal=Cu+Sn+Zn | German Silver=Cu+Zn+Ni (NO Ag!) | Duralumin=Al+Cu+Mg+Mn | SS=Fe+Cr+Ni | Nichrome=Ni+Cr | Solder=Pb+Sn | Amalgam=Hg+metal.
  • Iron types: Wrought (<0.1% C, purest) | Mild (0.1–0.3%) | Cast iron (2–4%, brittle) | Pig iron (2.5–4.5%, most C, most brittle). Galvanising = Zn coating on Fe.
  • Chemical names: Washing Soda=Na₂CO₃ | Baking Soda=NaHCO₃ | Caustic Soda=NaOH | Water Glass=Na₂SiO₃ | Heavy Water=D₂O | Muriatic Acid=HCl | PoP=2CaSO₄·H₂O | Gypsum=CaSO₄·2H₂O | Blue Vitriol=CuSO₄ | Green Vitriol=FeSO₄ | White Vitriol=ZnSO₄.
  • Bleaching: SO₂ (silk/wool, reversible), Cl₂, NaOCl, H₂O₂, bleaching powder. CO₂ is NOT a bleaching agent.
  • Periodic Table: Mendeleev 1869 (mass) → Moseley 1913 (atomic number). 7 periods, 18 groups. Group 1=alkali metals, 17=halogens, 18=noble gases. s/p/d/f blocks.
  • Coal: Peat (50–60%) < Lignite (60–75%) < Bituminous (75–90%) < Anthracite (90–95%, highest calorific value). Firedamp = CH₄ in mines.
  • Earth's crust: O(46%) > Si(28%) > Al(8%) > Fe(5%). Whole Earth: Fe first. Atmosphere: N₂(78%) > O₂(21%).
  • Gold: 24ct=100%, 22ct=91.67%, 18ct=75%. Superconductors: zero resistance + zero permeability (Meissner). High-Tc = ceramic oxides. LPG odorant = ethyl mercaptan.
  • Reactivity: K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Ni > Sn > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au > Pt. Gold dissolves only in Aqua Regia.
  • Ores: Bauxite=Al | Haematite=Fe | Chalcopyrite=Cu | Galena=Pb | Cinnabar=Hg | Monazite=Th | Pitchblende=U+Ra | Cassiterite=Sn.
  • Superlatives: Best conductor=Ag | Most malleable=Au | Hardest metal=Cr | Lightest metal=Li | Highest MP metal=W(3422°C) | Liquid metal=Hg | Liquid non-metal=Br₂.
