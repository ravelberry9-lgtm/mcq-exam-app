📚 Section 1: Basic Economic Concepts (Q 1–30)
1Who is known as the Father of Economics?
Answer: Adam Smith — He wrote "The Wealth of Nations" (1776) and is considered the founder of modern economics.
2Who wrote "The Wealth of Nations" and in which year?
Answer: Adam Smith wrote "An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations" in 1776.
3Who is known as the Father of Modern Macroeconomics?
Answer: John Maynard Keynes — He wrote "The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money" (1936).
4Which ancient Indian economist wrote "Arthashastra"?
Answer: Kautilya (also known as Chanakya or Vishnugupta) — adviser to Chandragupta Maurya. Arthashastra is one of the earliest works on economics and statecraft.
5Who defined Economics as "study of mankind in the ordinary business of life"?
Answer: Alfred Marshall — in his book "Principles of Economics" (1890). He is known for the welfare definition of economics.
6Who coined the terms "Micro Economics" and "Macro Economics"?
Answer: Ragnar Frisch (Norwegian economist) coined these terms in 1933.
7What is the basic economic problem?
Answer: Scarcity — Resources are limited (scarce) while human wants are unlimited. This forces us to make choices about how to allocate resources.
8What is Opportunity Cost?
Answer: The value of the next best alternative foregone when a choice is made. E.g., If you choose to study instead of working, the opportunity cost is the income you gave up.
9What is the difference between Micro and Macro Economics?
Answer: Micro Economics studies individual units — a firm, consumer, or industry (e.g., price of a product). Macro Economics studies the economy as a whole — GDP, inflation, unemployment.
10What does a point INSIDE the Production Possibility Frontier (PPF) indicate?
Answer: Inefficient production — Resources are not fully utilised; the economy is operating below its potential.
11What does a point ON the Production Possibility Frontier (PPF) indicate?
Answer: Efficient/Optimal production — All resources are fully and efficiently employed.
12What does a point OUTSIDE the PPF indicate?
Answer: Unattainable with current resources — Production at that point is not possible given existing technology and resources.
13What causes an OUTWARD SHIFT of the PPF?
Answer: Economic Growth — Caused by improvement in technology, increase in resources (capital, labour), better education, or discovery of new resources.
14What is Comparative Advantage?
Answer: A country has a Comparative Advantage in producing a good when it can produce it at a lower opportunity cost than another country — even if the other country is better at producing everything (David Ricardo, 1817).
15Who gave the theory of Comparative Advantage?
Answer: David Ricardo in 1817, in "On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation." It forms the basis for international free trade.
16What is a Mixed Economy? Give an example.
Answer: An economy where both public sector (government) and private sector coexist. India is a mixed economy — the government controls key sectors while private enterprise is also allowed.
17What is the difference between Positive Economics and Normative Economics?
Answer: Positive Economics deals with facts — "What IS" (e.g., India's GDP is $3.5 trillion). Normative Economics deals with value judgments — "What OUGHT TO BE" (e.g., government should reduce taxes).
18What is Human Capital?
Answer: The skills, knowledge, experience, and education embodied in workers that increase their productive capacity. Investment in education and health builds human capital.
19What is the difference between Economic Growth and Economic Development?
Answer: Economic Growth = Increase in GDP/national income (quantitative). Economic Development = Improvement in living standards, reduction in inequality, better health and education — overall well-being (qualitative + quantitative).
20What is Scarcity in Economics?
Answer: Scarcity means limited availability of resources relative to unlimited human wants. It is the fundamental economic problem that forces individuals and societies to make choices.
21What is a Command Economy?
Answer: An economy where the government/central authority makes all economic decisions — what to produce, how to produce, and for whom to produce. Example: Former USSR.
22What is a Market Economy (Capitalist Economy)?
Answer: An economy where economic decisions are made by private individuals through the price mechanism (demand and supply). Minimal government intervention. Example: USA.
23Who is Milton Friedman and what is he known for?
Answer: Milton Friedman — American economist, Nobel Prize 1976, known for Monetarism — the theory that controlling money supply is key to controlling inflation. Advocated free markets.
24Who is Amartya Sen and what is he known for?
Answer: Amartya Sen — Indian economist, Nobel Prize 1998, known for welfare economics, capability approach, and his work on famines and poverty. Co-created the HDI concept.
25What is Utility in Economics?
Answer: Utility is the satisfaction or pleasure derived from consuming a good or service. It is subjective and varies between individuals. Measured in "utils" (theoretical unit).
26What are the three central economic questions every economy must answer?
Answer: 1. What to produce? 2. How to produce? 3. For whom to produce? — These are the fundamental allocation problems every economic system must solve.
27What is Capital in Economics?
Answer: Man-made resources used in production — machinery, equipment, buildings, tools. It is one of the four factors of production (Land, Labour, Capital, Entrepreneurship).
28What is the difference between Microeconomics and Macroeconomics with examples?
Answer: Micro: Price of rice, profit of Tata Motors, wages in IT sector. Macro: India's GDP, national inflation rate, RBI's monetary policy, unemployment rate.
29Who wrote "Arthashastra" and what does it contain?
Answer: Kautilya (Chanakya) — It contains principles of governance, taxation, fiscal policy, trade, and economic administration. Often called the world's first economics textbook.
30What is Free Trade vs Protectionism?
Answer: Free Trade = No tariffs/barriers between countries; promotes comparative advantage. Protectionism = Tariffs, quotas, subsidies to protect domestic industries from foreign competition.
📊 Section 2: National Income — GDP, GNP, NNP (Q 31–70)
31What is GDP (Gross Domestic Product)?
Answer: GDP is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given year, regardless of who produces them (residents or foreigners).
32Write the Expenditure Method formula for GDP.
Answer: GDP = C + I + G + (X − M) where C = Private Consumption, I = Investment, G = Government Expenditure, X = Exports, M = Imports.
33What is GNP (Gross National Product)?
Answer: GNP = GDP + NFIA (Net Factor Income from Abroad). It measures the value of goods and services produced by a country's nationals — both inside and outside the country.
34What is NFIA?
Answer: Net Factor Income from Abroad = Income earned by residents abroad MINUS income earned by foreigners within the country. NFIA = Factor income received from abroad − Factor income paid abroad.
35What is NNP (Net National Product)?
Answer: NNP = GNP − Depreciation (Capital Consumption Allowance). It represents the actual net output after accounting for wear and tear of capital assets.
36What is NDP (Net Domestic Product)?
Answer: NDP = GDP − Depreciation. It is the net value of goods and services produced within a country after subtracting the value of capital consumption (depreciation).
37What is National Income (NI)?
Answer: National Income = NNP at Factor Cost = NNP at Market Price − Net Indirect Taxes (Indirect taxes − Subsidies). It measures income of all factors of production.
38What is Per Capita Income (PCI)?
Answer: Per Capita Income = National Income ÷ Total Population. It is the average income per person and is used as a measure of the standard of living.
39What is the base year for calculating India's GDP currently?
Answer: 2011-12 is the current base year for India's GDP calculation (revised from 2004-05 in January 2015).
40Which organization calculates India's National Income?
Answer: CSO (Central Statistics Office) — now renamed as MoSPI (Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation).
41What are the three methods of calculating National Income?
Answer: 1. Income Method (sum of factor incomes) 2. Output/Product Method (sum of value added) 3. Expenditure Method (sum of final expenditures). All three give the same result.
42What is the difference between Real GDP and Nominal GDP?
Answer: Nominal GDP = GDP measured at current year prices (includes inflation effect). Real GDP = GDP measured at base year prices (inflation removed). Real GDP is a better measure of actual economic growth.
43What is the GDP Deflator?
Answer: GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100. It measures the price level of all goods and services in an economy — a broad measure of inflation.
44What is Depreciation in National Income accounting?
Answer: Depreciation (Capital Consumption) = The reduction in value of capital assets due to wear and tear, obsolescence over time. It is subtracted from GNP to get NNP.
45What is Transfer Payment? Give examples.
Answer: Payments made without any corresponding production of goods/services. Examples: pensions, unemployment benefits, scholarships, subsidies. They are NOT included in GDP calculation.
46What is Double Counting in National Income? How is it avoided?
Answer: Counting the value of a product more than once at different stages of production. Avoided by using the Value Added Method — counting only the value added at each stage, not the total output.
47What is GDP at Market Price vs GDP at Factor Cost?
Answer: GDP at Market Price = actual price paid by consumers (includes indirect taxes, excludes subsidies). GDP at Factor Cost = income received by factors of production = GDP at Market Price − Net Indirect Taxes.
48Which sector contributes the most to India's GDP?
Answer: Tertiary sector (Services sector) — contributes approximately 55% of India's GDP. It includes banking, IT, trade, transport, communication.
49What is PPP (Purchasing Power Parity)?
Answer: PPP compares the purchasing power of different currencies — adjusting for price differences between countries. India's GDP rank by PPP is higher (3rd largest) than nominal GDP rank.
50What is the difference between Stock and Flow variables? Give examples.
Answer: Stock = measured at a point in time (e.g., wealth, capital, population). Flow = measured over a period of time (e.g., income, GDP, investment, savings). National Income is a flow variable.
51What is Green GDP?
Answer: Green GDP = GDP − Cost of environmental degradation. It adjusts GDP to account for environmental damage and depletion of natural resources. Reflects sustainable development.
52What is Disposable Personal Income?
Answer: Income available to individuals after paying personal taxes. Disposable Income = Personal Income − Personal Taxes. It is used for consumption or saving.
53What are Intermediate goods? Why are they excluded from GDP?
Answer: Intermediate goods are used as inputs in producing other goods (e.g., steel used to make cars, flour used to make bread). They are excluded from GDP to avoid double counting.
54If India's GDP is ₹200 lakh crore and population is 140 crore, what is Per Capita Income?
Answer: Per Capita Income = ₹200 lakh crore ÷ 140 crore = ₹1,42,857 approximately. (Simple formula: National Income ÷ Population).
55What is the difference between GNP and GDP?
Answer: GDP = production within borders (regardless of nationality). GNP = production by nationals (regardless of location). GNP = GDP + NFIA. If NFIA is positive, GNP > GDP.
56What is Net Exports in GDP formula?
Answer: Net Exports = Exports (X) − Imports (M). If X > M, it is a trade surplus and adds to GDP. If M > X, it is a trade deficit and reduces GDP.
57Which country has the world's largest GDP (nominal)?
Answer: United States of America (USA) has the world's largest nominal GDP. By PPP, China ranks 1st. India is approximately 5th largest by nominal GDP and 3rd by PPP.
58What is Value Added Method in National Income?
Answer: GDP = Sum of Value Added at each stage of production. Value Added = Output value − Intermediate input cost. This method avoids double counting.
59What is Income Method of National Income calculation?
Answer: NI = Rent + Wages + Interest + Profit (all factor incomes). It sums up all incomes earned by factors of production (land, labour, capital, enterprise) in a year.
60India's approximate GDP size (2024-25)?
Answer: India's GDP is approximately $3.5–3.7 trillion (nominal), making it the 5th largest economy. Target: become a $5 trillion economy. By PPP, India is 3rd largest.
61What is the relationship between GDP and NNP?
Answer: NNP = GDP + NFIA − Depreciation. Or: NNP = GNP − Depreciation. NNP is a more accurate measure of net output than GDP.
62What is a Recession? When does it officially occur?
Answer: Recession = two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth. It is characterized by falling output, rising unemployment, reduced consumer spending and business investment.
63What is the difference between Gross and Net in national income?
Answer: Gross = before subtracting depreciation. Net = after subtracting depreciation (capital consumption). So GDP (Gross) − Depreciation = NDP (Net).
64What is the Expenditure Method of calculating GDP?
Answer: GDP = C + I + G + (X−M) where C = household consumption, I = private investment (includes inventory changes), G = government spending, X-M = net exports.
65If NFIA is NEGATIVE, what does it mean for GNP vs GDP?
Answer: If NFIA is negative, GNP < GDP — meaning foreigners within the country earn more than nationals abroad. This is common in countries with large foreign investment.
66What is the full form of MoSPI?
Answer: Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation — the government body responsible for collection, compilation, and publication of national accounts data including GDP.
67What is the difference between Domestic and National in economics?
Answer: Domestic = within the geographical boundaries of the country (regardless of who produces). National = by the nationals of the country (regardless of where they produce).
68What are the four factors of production?
Answer: 1. Land (natural resources — earns Rent) 2. Labour (human effort — earns Wages) 3. Capital (man-made — earns Interest) 4. Entrepreneurship (organization — earns Profit).
69What is Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF)?
Answer: GFCF is investment in fixed assets — machinery, buildings, infrastructure — by businesses, governments, and households. It represents the I (Investment) component in GDP formula. India's GFCF rate is ~30% of GDP.
70What happens to GDP if government increases spending (G) in GDP formula?
Answer: GDP increases because G is a component of GDP. However, if financed by borrowing, it may crowd out private investment. This is the basis of Keynesian fiscal stimulus.
🌾 Section 3: Sectors of Economy & Planning (Q 71–120)
71What are the three sectors of the Indian Economy?
Answer: 1. Primary Sector (agriculture, mining, fishing) 2. Secondary Sector (manufacturing, industry) 3. Tertiary Sector (services — banking, IT, trade, transport).
72What percentage of GDP does India's Primary sector contribute?
Answer: Approximately 20% of GDP but provides about 44% of employment — showing low productivity in agriculture.
73When was India's First Five Year Plan launched?
Answer: 1951 — The First Five Year Plan (1951-56) focused on agriculture, irrigation, and power. Modeled on Harrod-Domar model.
74When did India's Five Year Plans end?
Answer: The 12th Five Year Plan (2012-17) was the last. NITI Aayog replaced the Planning Commission in 2015 and Five Year Plans were discontinued after 2017.
75What was the main focus of India's Second Five Year Plan?
Answer: Rapid industrialization and development of heavy industries (1956-61). Based on the Mahalanobis Model. Steel plants at Bhilai, Durgapur, and Rourkela were set up.
76Which Five Year Plan period is called the "Plan Holiday"?
Answer: 1966-69 — After the 3rd Five Year Plan, three annual plans were made instead of a regular 5-year plan due to wars (1962, 1965) and drought, foreign exchange crisis.
77When was NITI Aayog established?
Answer: 1 January 2015. It replaced the Planning Commission (established 1950). NITI = National Institution for Transforming India.
78Who is the Chairman of NITI Aayog?
Answer: The Prime Minister of India is the ex-officio Chairman of NITI Aayog.
79What is a key difference between Planning Commission and NITI Aayog?
Answer: Planning Commission could allocate funds to states; NITI Aayog CANNOT allocate funds. NITI Aayog is purely an advisory/think-tank body. Fund devolution is done by the Finance Commission.
80When was India's LPG reform introduced and what does it stand for?
Answer: 1991 — LPG = Liberalisation, Privatisation, Globalisation. Introduced by PM P.V. Narasimha Rao and Finance Minister Dr. Manmohan Singh during India's balance of payments crisis.
81What is a Kharif crop? Give examples.
Answer: Kharif crops are sown in monsoon (June-July) and harvested in autumn (September-October). Examples: Rice, Maize, Jowar, Bajra, Cotton, Sugarcane, Groundnut, Soybean.
82What is a Rabi crop? Give examples.
Answer: Rabi crops are sown in winter (October-November) and harvested in spring (March-April). Examples: Wheat, Barley, Mustard, Peas, Gram, Linseed.
83What is MSP (Minimum Support Price)?
Answer: MSP is the guaranteed minimum price the government pays farmers for their crops, ensuring they don't sell at a loss. The CACP (Commission for Agricultural Costs and Prices) recommends MSP.
84What is the Green Revolution?
Answer: Green Revolution (mid-1960s) = Introduction of High-Yielding Variety (HYV) seeds, modern irrigation, fertilizers and pesticides — leading to a dramatic increase in food grain production. India became self-sufficient in food.
85Who is known as the Father of Green Revolution in India?
Answer: M.S. Swaminathan (Mankombu Sambasivan Swaminathan) — Indian agricultural scientist. Norman Borlaug (Nobel 1970) is the father of Green Revolution globally.
86What is the White Revolution? Who is its father?
Answer: White Revolution (Operation Flood) = Large-scale increase in milk production through dairy cooperatives. Father: Dr. Verghese Kurien (Milkman of India). AMUL is the product of this revolution.
87What is the Blue Revolution?
Answer: Blue Revolution = Rapid growth in fisheries and aquaculture production. India is one of the largest fish producers in the world. Government promotes it through schemes like PM Matsya Sampada Yojana.
88When was NABARD established and what does it stand for?
Answer: NABARD = National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development. Established on 12 July 1982. It provides credit and development support for agriculture and rural development.
89What is Zamindari Abolition?
Answer: Abolition of the Zamindari (landlord) system after Independence — Zamindars were large landowners who collected rent from peasants under British rule. Land reforms gave ownership rights directly to farmers.
90What is Operation Flood?
Answer: Operation Flood (1970-1996) was India's national dairy development programme launched by NDDB (National Dairy Development Board) under Verghese Kurien. It made India the world's largest milk producer.
91What are HYV seeds?
Answer: High-Yielding Variety (HYV) seeds — specially bred seeds that produce much higher crop yields than traditional varieties. They require more water, fertilizers, and pesticides. Key to Green Revolution success.
92What is Disguised Unemployment? Where is it mainly found?
Answer: Disguised Unemployment = More people employed in a job than actually needed. If removed, output doesn't fall. Mainly found in the agricultural sector in India and other developing nations.
93What is Structural Unemployment?
Answer: Unemployment due to mismatch between workers' skills and job requirements — caused by technological change, shift in industries. E.g., workers displaced by automation.
94What is Frictional Unemployment?
Answer: Temporary unemployment when workers are between jobs — searching for a new job, transitioning from one sector to another. It is considered a natural part of a healthy economy.
95What is Cyclical Unemployment?
Answer: Unemployment caused by recession or downswing in the business cycle. As economic activity falls, demand for labour falls. It is the main type addressed by Keynesian economics.
96What is Seasonal Unemployment?
Answer: Unemployment occurring during certain seasons when demand for labour falls. Common in agriculture (between harvests), tourism, and construction (rainy season).
97What is APMC?
Answer: Agricultural Produce Market Committee — state-regulated mandis (markets) where agricultural produce is traded. APMC Acts governed how and where farmers could sell. e-NAM digitally links all APMCs.
98What were the 3 pillars of 1991 LPG reforms?
Answer: 1. Liberalisation — Removing industrial licensing, reducing regulations. 2. Privatisation — Encouraging private sector, disinvestment of PSUs. 3. Globalisation — Opening economy to foreign trade and investment.
99Who are the "Architects of 1991 reforms" in India?
Answer: PM P.V. Narasimha Rao (political will) and Finance Minister Dr. Manmohan Singh (economic blueprint) — they introduced the historic 1991 liberalization during India's worst BoP crisis.
100What is land ceiling in land reforms?
Answer: Land ceiling = maximum limit of land a person/family can hold. Land above this limit was taken by the government and redistributed to landless farmers. Part of post-independence land reform measures.
101What is SIDBI?
Answer: Small Industries Development Bank of India — established in 1990. It provides financial assistance, refinance, and development support to Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs).
102What is EXIM Bank?
Answer: Export-Import Bank of India — established in 1982. It finances, facilitates, and promotes India's foreign trade by providing loans and guarantees to exporters and importers.
103What is AMUL and what does it stand for?
Answer: AMUL = Anand Milk Union Limited — India's largest dairy cooperative, headquartered in Anand, Gujarat. Product of the White Revolution. "Taste of India" — run by Gujarat Cooperative Milk Marketing Federation (GCMMF).
104What is the Mahalanobis Model in Five Year Plans?
Answer: P.C. Mahalanobis (statistician) designed the model for the 2nd Five Year Plan — emphasizing heavy industrialisation (capital goods sector) as the engine of growth. Influenced planning for decades.
105What is the Zaid crop season?
Answer: Zaid crops are grown between Rabi and Kharif seasons (March to June — summer season). Examples: Watermelon, Cucumber, Muskmelon, Bitter gourd, Pumpkin.
106What is the CACP?
Answer: Commission for Agricultural Costs and Prices — a statutory body under the Ministry of Agriculture that recommends MSP (Minimum Support Price) for major crops before each season.
107What is the Governing Council of NITI Aayog?
Answer: The Governing Council includes the Chief Ministers of all states and Lieutenant Governors of Union Territories — this is a key difference from Planning Commission, promoting cooperative federalism.
108What is Disinvestment?
Answer: Disinvestment = government selling its stake in Public Sector Undertakings (PSUs). Full privatisation = selling majority stake. Managed by DIPAM (Department of Investment and Public Asset Management).
109What is the Primary sector's share in employment in India?
Answer: Approximately 44-47% of India's workforce is employed in the primary sector (agriculture and allied activities), even though it contributes only ~20% of GDP — indicating low agricultural productivity.
110What is a cooperative society?
Answer: A cooperative is a voluntary organisation owned and managed by its members for their mutual benefit. Examples: AMUL (dairy), IFFCO (fertilizers), cooperative banks. Governed by state cooperative acts.
111What were the Narsimham Committee recommendations?
Answer: Narsimham Committee (1991 and 1998) recommended banking sector reforms — reducing CRR/SLR, deregulating interest rates, setting up of private banks, strengthening bank supervision and reducing NPAs.
112What is Demographic Dividend?
Answer: Economic growth potential resulting from a large working-age population. India has ~65% of its population in the working age (15-64 years). Window: 2020-2050. But requires investment in education and skills.
113What is the Special Economic Zone (SEZ)?
Answer: SEZ = duty-free enclaves for manufacturing/exports. Businesses in SEZs get tax exemptions, simpler regulations to promote exports. SEZ Act 2005 governs SEZs in India. First SEZ: Kandla (1965).
114What is MSME? What are the current limits?
Answer: Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises. Micro: Investment ≤₹1cr & Turnover ≤₹5cr. Small: ≤₹10cr & ≤₹50cr. Medium: ≤₹50cr & ≤₹250cr. Regulated by Ministry of MSME.
115What is the NITI Aayog's 15-year Vision Document called?
Answer: Vision@2035 — a long-term strategic document. NITI Aayog also prepares 7-year Strategy (Saptarishi) and 3-year Action Agenda — replacing the old Five Year Plans.
116What is the National Food Security Act (NFSA)?
Answer: NFSA 2013 — provides subsidised food grain to approximately 67% of India's population. Rice at ₹3/kg, Wheat at ₹2/kg, Coarse grains at ₹1/kg under the Public Distribution System (PDS).
117What is e-NAM?
Answer: Electronic National Agriculture Market — launched April 2016. Online platform connecting APMC mandis across India. Implemented by SFAC. Enables farmers to sell to buyers anywhere online, eliminating middlemen.
118What is PMFBY?
Answer: Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana — launched 18 February 2016. Crop insurance scheme. Farmer pays only 2% premium for Kharif, 1.5% for Rabi, 5% for commercial crops. Rest shared by Centre and State.
119What is Food Inflation and why does it matter in India?
Answer: Food Inflation = rise in prices of food items. It matters more in India because food has a very high weight (~46%) in India's CPI basket. Rise in food prices heavily impacts poor households.
120What is a Public Distribution System (PDS)?
Answer: PDS distributes subsidized food and essential commodities to poor households through "ration shops" (Fair Price Shops). Managed by state governments. Beneficiaries identified as BPL/AAY families.
🏦 Section 4: Banking & RBI (Q 121–180)
121When was RBI (Reserve Bank of India) established?
Answer: 1 April 1935 (under RBI Act 1934). Nationalised on 1 January 1949. Headquartered in Mumbai. First Governor: Sir Osborne Smith; First Indian Governor: C.D. Deshmukh.
122What is the Repo Rate?
Answer: Repo Rate = the rate at which RBI lends money to commercial banks (short-term). When Repo Rate increases, borrowing becomes costlier → credit reduces → inflation falls. Current rate: approximately 6.5%.
123What is the Reverse Repo Rate?
Answer: Reverse Repo Rate = the rate at which RBI borrows money from commercial banks. When banks park excess funds with RBI. It is always lower than Repo Rate. Approximately 3.35% currently.
124What is CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio)?
Answer: CRR = percentage of a bank's total deposits that must be kept as cash reserves with RBI. If CRR is 4.5%, a bank with ₹100 crore deposits must keep ₹4.5 crore with RBI. No interest paid on CRR.
125What is SLR (Statutory Liquidity Ratio)?
Answer: SLR = percentage of deposits banks must maintain in liquid assets — cash, gold, or government-approved securities. Currently approximately 18%. Banks earn interest on SLR securities.
126What is the Bank Rate?
Answer: Bank Rate = rate at which RBI lends money to commercial banks for long-term purposes (without collateral). Currently approximately 6.75%. Used as a signal for long-term interest rates.
127What is MCLR?
Answer: Marginal Cost of Funds-based Lending Rate — the minimum interest rate below which banks cannot lend. Introduced by RBI in April 2016 to replace the base rate system. More transparent and market-linked.
128What happens when RBI INCREASES the Repo Rate?
Answer: Borrowing becomes costlier → banks raise lending rates → credit demand falls → money supply contracts → inflation decreases. RBI raises Repo Rate to control inflation (contractionary monetary policy).
129What happens when RBI DECREASES the CRR?
Answer: Banks have more funds to lend → money supply increases → economic activity stimulates → but risk of inflation rises. Lowering CRR is an expansionary monetary policy tool.
130When was SBI (State Bank of India) established?
Answer: SBI = 1 July 1955 — converted from Imperial Bank of India. It is India's largest public sector bank. Headquarters: Mumbai. SBI is also among the world's top 50 banks by assets.
131When were banks nationalised in India? How many in each round?
Answer: First nationalisation: 19 July 1969 — 14 banks nationalised by PM Indira Gandhi. Second nationalisation: 1980 — 6 more banks. Total: 20 banks. Reason: social objectives, priority sector lending.
132What is the MPC (Monetary Policy Committee)?
Answer: MPC is a 6-member committee that decides India's policy interest rates (Repo Rate). 3 members from RBI (including Governor as Chair) + 3 external members appointed by Government. Meets every 2 months.
133What is a Payment Bank?
Answer: Payment Banks can accept deposits (up to ₹2 lakh per customer) and provide remittance services but CANNOT give loans or issue credit cards. Examples: Paytm Payments Bank, India Post Payments Bank (IPPB).
134What is a Small Finance Bank?
Answer: Small Finance Banks provide basic banking services — deposits and loans — primarily to underserved sections like small farmers, micro industries, unorganised sector. Examples: AU Small Finance Bank, Ujjivan SFB.
135What is Priority Sector Lending (PSL)?
Answer: Banks must lend at least 40% of their Adjusted Net Bank Credit (ANBC) to priority sectors — Agriculture (18%), MSMEs, Education, Housing, Weaker Sections. Foreign banks: 40% with sub-targets.
136What is the difference between a Bank and an NBFC?
Answer: Banks: can accept demand deposits, part of payment & settlement system, regulated by RBI Act/Banking Regulation Act. NBFC: cannot accept demand deposits, not part of payment system, regulated under Companies Act + RBI directions.
137What is RTGS?
Answer: Real-Time Gross Settlement — Electronic funds transfer system where transactions are settled in real-time (immediately). Minimum amount: ₹2 lakh. Available 24x7 (since December 2020). Used for large-value transactions.
138What is NEFT?
Answer: National Electronic Funds Transfer — Transfers settled in batches (every 30 minutes). Available 24x7. No minimum amount. Suitable for small transactions. Operated by RBI through NPCI.
139What is UPI?
Answer: Unified Payments Interface — Instant mobile payment system developed by NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India). Launched 2016. Works 24x7. No bank details needed — just UPI ID or QR code.
140What is NPCI?
Answer: National Payments Corporation of India — an umbrella organisation for retail payments in India. Set up by RBI and IBA. Operates: UPI, RuPay, NEFT, IMPS, NACH, Bharat Bill Payment System.
141What is RuPay?
Answer: India's domestic card payment network (like Visa/Mastercard but Indian). Developed by NPCI. Lower transaction costs. Used in PMJDY (Jan Dhan) accounts. Name = Rupee + Payment.
142What is IMPS?
Answer: Immediate Payment Service — Real-time interbank electronic fund transfer available 24x7x365 (including holidays). Works through mobile, internet, ATM. No minimum/maximum fixed limit (set by bank). Operated by NPCI.
143What is the Lender of Last Resort?
Answer: A central bank acts as Lender of Last Resort — when commercial banks face a liquidity crisis and cannot borrow from anywhere else, the central bank (RBI in India) provides emergency funds to prevent bank failure.
144What is DICGC and how much deposit does it insure?
Answer: Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation — a subsidiary of RBI. Insures bank deposits up to ₹5 lakh per depositor per bank (increased from ₹1 lakh in 2020).
145What are Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)?
Answer: RRBs established in 1975 under the RRB Act to provide banking services to rural areas. Owned by: Central Government (50%) + State Government (15%) + Sponsoring Commercial Bank (35%).
146What is Open Market Operations (OMO)?
Answer: RBI buying or selling government securities in the open market to regulate money supply. RBI buys securities → money flows into economy (expansionary). RBI sells → money flows out (contractionary).
147What is Inflation Targeting in India?
Answer: RBI targets CPI inflation at 4% with a band of ±2% (i.e., 2-6%). This mandate was given by the government in 2016 under the amended RBI Act. The MPC is responsible for achieving this target.
148What is a Microfinance Institution (MFI)?
Answer: MFIs provide small loans (micro-credit) to poor and low-income borrowers who don't have access to formal banking. SHG-Bank linkage model promoted by NABARD. Grameen Bank model (Bangladesh, Muhammad Yunus).
149What is a Self Help Group (SHG)?
Answer: SHG = a small voluntary group (10-20 members, usually women) that pools savings and lends to members. Linked to banks for larger credit. India has over 1 crore SHGs. Key tool for rural women empowerment.
150What is PMJDY (Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana)?
Answer: Launched 28 August 2014 — Financial inclusion scheme to provide every household with a bank account, RuPay debit card, ₹1 lakh accident insurance, and ₹30,000 life insurance. Zero balance accounts.
151What is the difference between RTGS and NEFT?
Answer: RTGS: Real-time settlement, minimum ₹2 lakh, for large transfers. NEFT: Settled in batches (every 30 min), no minimum amount, for smaller transfers. Both available 24x7.
152What is a Crossed Cheque?
Answer: A cheque with two parallel lines drawn across it. A crossed cheque cannot be encashed at a counter — it can only be deposited into a bank account. Provides security against misuse if lost.
153What is the Money Multiplier formula?
Answer: Money Multiplier = 1 / CRR. If CRR = 4% (0.04), Money Multiplier = 1/0.04 = 25. Meaning ₹1 of new deposits creates ₹25 of total money supply through successive lending.
154What is SEBI and when was it established as a statutory body?
Answer: Securities and Exchange Board of India. Set up in 1988 as a non-statutory body; became a statutory body in 1992 under SEBI Act. Regulates and develops the securities market, protects investors.
155What is the difference between Monetary Policy and Fiscal Policy?
Answer: Monetary Policy: managed by RBI, controls money supply and interest rates. Fiscal Policy: managed by government (Finance Ministry), controls taxation and government expenditure (budget). Both together manage the economy.
156What is a Non-Performing Asset (NPA)?
Answer: NPA = a loan/advance where interest or principal repayment is overdue for more than 90 days. High NPAs weaken banks. RBI has stringent NPA recognition norms. Resolved through IBC 2016, SARFAESI Act.
157What is SARFAESI Act?
Answer: Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Securities Interest Act, 2002. Allows banks to recover NPAs by auctioning mortgaged assets without court intervention.
158What is the RBI's role as "Banker to the Government"?
Answer: RBI manages the government's accounts, receives revenues, makes payments, manages public debt, and issues government securities. It is the fiscal agent of both central and state governments.
159What is NACH?
Answer: National Automated Clearing House — operated by NPCI. Used for bulk transactions like salaries, pension, EMIs, dividends, subsidies. Electronic equivalent of paper-based clearing system.
160What is the IBC 2016?
Answer: Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016 — provides time-bound (330 days) resolution of insolvency cases. Companies: adjudicated by NCLT. Individuals: by DRT. Regulated by IBBI.
161What is IRDAI?
Answer: Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India — statutory body set up under IRDAI Act 1999 (operative from 2000). Regulates and promotes the insurance industry. Headquartered in Hyderabad.
162What is LIC?
Answer: Life Insurance Corporation of India — established 1 September 1956 by nationalizing 245 private insurance companies. India's largest insurance company and institutional investor. IPO in May 2022.
163What is Sensex and Nifty 50?
Answer: Sensex = BSE Sensitive Index — tracks top 30 companies on Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE, est. 1875). Nifty 50 = tracks top 50 companies on National Stock Exchange (NSE, est. 1992). Both are market barometers.
164What is a Bull Market vs a Bear Market?
Answer: Bull Market = stock prices are rising (optimism, investors buying). Bear Market = stock prices are falling (pessimism, investors selling). A bull "thrusts up" with its horns; a bear "swipes down" with its paws.
165What is the CBDC (Digital Rupee)?
Answer: Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) — Digital Rupee (e₹) issued by RBI. Pilot launched 2022. e₹-W (Wholesale, for banks) and e₹-R (Retail, for public). Legal tender, like physical rupee but digital.
166What is the difference between CBDC and Cryptocurrency?
Answer: CBDC: issued by central bank (RBI), legal tender, stable value, regulated. Cryptocurrency (Bitcoin, Ethereum): decentralised, not legal tender in India, highly volatile, unregulated by any government.
167What is Capital Market? Name its regulator.
Answer: Capital Market = market for long-term funds — equity shares, debentures, bonds (maturity > 1 year). Regulator: SEBI. Includes primary market (new issues) and secondary market (stock exchanges).
168What is Money Market? Name its regulator.
Answer: Money Market = market for short-term funds (maturity < 1 year). Instruments: T-Bills, Commercial Paper, Certificate of Deposit, Call Money. Regulator: RBI.
169What are Treasury Bills (T-Bills)?
Answer: T-Bills are short-term government securities issued by RBI on behalf of Government of India. Maturities: 91 days, 182 days, 364 days. Issued at discount, redeemed at face value. Safest money market instrument.
170What is Commercial Paper (CP)?
Answer: Commercial Paper = unsecured short-term promissory notes issued by corporates to raise short-term funds (working capital). Maturity: 7 days to 1 year. Minimum ₹5 lakh. Money market instrument.
171What is a Certificate of Deposit (CD)?
Answer: CD = negotiable money market instrument issued by banks. Like a fixed deposit but tradeable in the secondary market. Banks issue CDs (7 days to 1 year); Financial Institutions (1-3 years). Minimum ₹1 lakh.
172What is Call Money?
Answer: Call Money = overnight lending between banks. Maturity: 1 day. Notice Money = 2-14 days. The interest rate is called "Call Rate." Used by banks to manage short-term liquidity needs. Only banks and PDs participate.
173What is the role of Finance Commission?
Answer: Finance Commission (Article 280) — constituted by President every 5 years. Recommends devolution of central taxes to states and grants to states. 15th Finance Commission (2021-26) chaired by N.K. Singh.
174What is the CCI (Competition Commission of India)?
Answer: CCI regulates competition in Indian markets under the Competition Act 2002. It prevents abuse of dominant position, anti-competitive agreements (cartels), and regulates mergers and acquisitions.
175What is Quantitative Easing (QE)?
Answer: QE = Central bank purchases large amounts of financial assets (bonds) to inject money into the economy. Used when interest rates are near zero. Used by US Fed, ECB post-2008 crisis and COVID-19.
176What is the difference between Repo Rate and Bank Rate?
Answer: Repo Rate: Short-term lending by RBI to banks WITH collateral (government securities). Bank Rate: Long-term lending by RBI to banks WITHOUT collateral. Bank Rate is usually higher than Repo Rate.
177What is NABARD's headquarters?
Answer: NABARD is headquartered in Mumbai. It was established on 12 July 1982 based on recommendations of B. Sivaraman Committee. It refinances rural credit institutions and promotes agricultural development.
178What is EXIM Bank used for?
Answer: Export-Import Bank of India (est. 1982) provides financing for India's international trade — loans to exporters for pre/post-shipment, buyer's credit, Lines of Credit (LOC) to foreign governments.
179What is NPA resolution under IBC?
Answer: Under IBC 2016, companies with unresolved NPAs go to NCLT. Committee of Creditors (CoC) votes (66% majority) on resolution plan. If no plan in 330 days, company is liquidated. Creditors get paid in priority order.
180What is the IMF? Where is it headquartered?
Answer: International Monetary Fund — est. 1944 (Bretton Woods). Headquartered in Washington D.C., USA. 190 member countries. Provides loans to countries with balance of payments problems. Also conducts economic surveillance.
💼 Section 5: Budget, Fiscal Policy & Taxation (Q 181–240)
181What is the Union Budget? Under which Article?
Answer: Union Budget = Annual Financial Statement under Article 112 of the Constitution. It presents the government's estimated revenues and expenditures for the coming financial year (April 1 to March 31).
182On which date is the Union Budget presented since 2017?
Answer: 1st February (since 2017, changed by Arun Jaitley). Earlier it was the last working day of February. Railway Budget was also merged with Union Budget from 2017.
183What is the Economic Survey?
Answer: Annual report on India's economic performance — presented one day BEFORE the Union Budget. Prepared by the Chief Economic Adviser (CEA) under the Ministry of Finance. Reviews past year + gives policy outlook.
184What is Fiscal Deficit?
Answer: Fiscal Deficit = Total Government Expenditure − Total Government Receipts (excluding borrowings). It shows how much the government borrows to meet its expenses. A higher fiscal deficit means more borrowing.
185What is Revenue Deficit?
Answer: Revenue Deficit = Revenue Expenditure − Revenue Receipts. It indicates that the government's day-to-day income is insufficient to cover its day-to-day expenses. Bad sign — means govt is borrowing for consumption.
186What is Primary Deficit?
Answer: Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments. It shows the gap between government income and expenditure EXCLUDING interest payments on past debt. Measures current year fiscal management.
187What is FRBM Act and when was it enacted?
Answer: Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management Act, 2003 — requires the government to progressively reduce fiscal and revenue deficits. Sets targets for fiscal consolidation and debt management.
188What is GST?
Answer: Goods and Services Tax — a comprehensive indirect tax on supply of goods and services, implemented on 1 July 2017. Replaced multiple taxes (VAT, Service Tax, Excise Duty etc.). "One Nation One Tax."
189What are the GST rates in India?
Answer: GST has 5 rates: 0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, and 28%. Essential items (food grains, vegetables) = 0%. Luxury and sin goods (cars, tobacco) = 28%. IGST applies on interstate supply; CGST+SGST on intrastate.
190Who chairs the GST Council?
Answer: Union Finance Minister chairs the GST Council. Members include state Finance Ministers. Decisions taken by 3/4th majority. GST Council recommends rates, exemptions, and other GST-related matters.
191What is the difference between CGST, SGST, and IGST?
Answer: CGST (Central GST) = collected by Centre on intrastate sales. SGST (State GST) = collected by State on intrastate sales. IGST (Integrated GST) = collected by Centre on interstate sales, then distributed to states.
192What is a Direct Tax? Give examples.
Answer: Direct Tax = tax paid directly by the person on whom it is imposed — burden cannot be shifted. Administered by CBDT. Examples: Income Tax, Corporate Tax, Capital Gains Tax.
193What is an Indirect Tax? Give examples.
Answer: Indirect Tax = tax collected by intermediary and passed on to consumer — burden can be shifted. Administered by CBIC. Examples: GST, Customs Duty, Excise Duty.
194What is CBDT and CBIC?
Answer: CBDT = Central Board of Direct Taxes — administers Income Tax, Corporate Tax (under Finance Ministry). CBIC = Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs — administers GST, Customs, Excise.
195What is a Progressive Tax?
Answer: Progressive Tax = tax rate INCREASES as income increases. Higher income → higher tax rate. India's Income Tax is progressive (0%, 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%, 30% slabs). Reduces inequality.
196What is a Regressive Tax?
Answer: Regressive Tax = tax rate DECREASES as income increases. Poorer people pay a higher percentage of their income. GST is considered somewhat regressive because everyone pays the same rate regardless of income.
197What is Revenue Budget?
Answer: Revenue Budget includes day-to-day income and expenditure. Revenue Receipts = taxes + non-tax revenue. Revenue Expenditure = salaries, interest payments, subsidies, maintenance. No asset creation.
198What is Capital Budget?
Answer: Capital Budget deals with long-term assets and liabilities. Capital Receipts = borrowings, disinvestment, loans recovered. Capital Expenditure = infrastructure, defence equipment, loans given — creates assets.
199What is the Consolidated Fund of India (CFI)?
Answer: Article 266(1) — Most important government fund. All revenues (taxes, fees) and borrowings go here. ALL government expenditure must be charged to CFI. Parliamentary approval mandatory for withdrawal.
200What is the Contingency Fund of India?
Answer: Article 267 — Emergency fund of ₹500 crore at the disposal of the President. Used for urgent unforeseen expenditure. No Parliamentary approval needed for withdrawal. Later replenished by Parliament.
201What is the Public Account of India?
Answer: Article 266(2) — Money received by government as trustee — Provident Fund deposits, small savings, judicial deposits. Government acts as banker. No Parliamentary approval needed. Not government's own money.
202What is a Money Bill? Under which Article?
Answer: Money Bill (Article 110) — deals ONLY with taxation, appropriation, borrowing. Can be introduced ONLY in Lok Sabha. Rajya Sabha can only recommend changes (not reject). Speaker certifies as Money Bill.
203What is the Appropriation Bill?
Answer: Appropriation Bill (Article 114) authorises withdrawal from the Consolidated Fund of India to meet government expenditure. It is a Money Bill. Without the Appropriation Act, no money can be withdrawn from CFI.
204What is Vote on Account?
Answer: Advance grant for ~2 months of expenditure before the full budget is passed by Parliament. Used mainly during election years when full budget is delayed. Approved by Parliament. Usually 1/6th of total annual budget.
205What is Deficit Financing?
Answer: Government finances its deficit by printing new money or borrowing from RBI/public. Excessive deficit financing causes inflation. In India, government borrows through issuing bonds (G-secs) rather than printing money.
206What is the Finance Commission? Under which Article?
Answer: Finance Commission (Article 280) — constituted by President every 5 years. Recommends: share of central taxes for states, grants-in-aid to states, measures to augment state finances. Promotes fiscal federalism.
207What is Article 360?
Answer: Article 360 = Financial Emergency. Proclaimed by President when India's financial stability or credit is threatened. President can then direct states on financial matters, reduce salaries including judges. Never proclaimed so far in India.
208What is Customs Duty?
Answer: Tax levied on goods imported into or exported from India. Import duty protects domestic industries. Administered by CBIC. Basic Customs Duty (BCD) + other levies. Revenue for central government.
209What is Capital Gains Tax?
Answer: Tax on profit made from selling a capital asset (shares, property, gold). Short-term Capital Gains (STCG) — asset held < 1-3 years. Long-term Capital Gains (LTCG) — asset held longer. LTCG on equity above ₹1 lakh: 10%.
210What is Corporate Tax?
Answer: Tax levied on profits of companies. India's base corporate tax rate: 22% for domestic companies (reduced from 30% in 2019); 15% for new manufacturing companies. Administered by CBDT.
211What is a Balanced Budget, Surplus Budget, and Deficit Budget?
Answer: Balanced: Revenue = Expenditure. Surplus: Revenue > Expenditure (government saves). Deficit: Expenditure > Revenue (government borrows). India typically has a deficit budget to fund development expenditure.
212What is the Halwa Ceremony?
Answer: Traditional ritual marking the start of Budget printing in the Finance Ministry. Halwa is distributed to all staff involved in budget preparation who are then "locked in" until the Budget is presented — to prevent leaks.
213What is the Finance Bill?
Answer: Finance Bill is presented with the Union Budget. Part A contains Money Bill provisions (taxation changes). Part B may contain other financial proposals. It gives legal effect to budget taxation proposals for the year.
214What is Non-Tax Revenue? Give examples.
Answer: Revenue earned by government from non-tax sources: Interest received on loans given to states, dividends from PSUs, fees (passport, court), fines, spectrum auction proceeds, profits from RBI.
215What is the difference between Revenue Expenditure and Capital Expenditure?
Answer: Revenue Expenditure = does not create assets (salaries, subsidies, interest payments). Capital Expenditure = creates assets or reduces liabilities (roads, dams, defence equipment, loans given to states).
216What is the article for Financial Emergency?
Answer: Article 360. Under this, President can reduce salaries of all government servants including judges of Supreme Court and High Courts. Financial Emergency has never been declared in India's history.
217What are the three types of government accounts?
Answer: 1. Consolidated Fund of India (Art 266(1)) — needs Parliamentary approval. 2. Contingency Fund (Art 267) — President's discretion. 3. Public Account (Art 266(2)) — Executive authority.
218What is CBDT's full form and function?
Answer: Central Board of Direct Taxes — apex body under Ministry of Finance for administration of direct taxes (income tax, corporate tax, TDS). Issues circulars, forms, oversees tax collection.
219Who is the CAG and what is its role?
Answer: Comptroller and Auditor General (Article 148) — audits all government accounts including CFI, Contingency Fund, and Public Account. India's financial watchdog. Reports to Parliament. Independent constitutional authority.
220What are the Constitutional provisions related to trade and commerce?
Answer: Articles 301-307 — "Freedom of Trade, Commerce and Intercourse." Article 301 guarantees free flow of trade and commerce. Parliament can impose reasonable restrictions. Article 302 allows Parliament to impose trade restrictions.
221What is the difference between Plan and Non-Plan expenditure (old system)?
Answer: Plan Expenditure = spending on Five Year Plan targets (development). Non-Plan Expenditure = routine/non-developmental (defence, interest, salaries). This distinction was abolished from 2017-18 budget — replaced by Capital/Revenue classification.
222What is Article 112 of the Constitution?
Answer: Article 112 = Annual Financial Statement (Union Budget). The President shall in respect of every financial year cause to be laid before both Houses of Parliament a statement of estimated receipts and expenditure.
223What is the role of the Speaker in Money Bill?
Answer: The Speaker of Lok Sabha certifies a bill as a Money Bill. This certification is final and cannot be challenged in court. Once certified, Rajya Sabha can only suggest (not reject) amendments within 14 days.
224What is Goods and Services Tax Network (GSTN)?
Answer: GSTN = IT infrastructure for GST — a non-government, not-for-profit company that provides the IT backbone for GST registration, returns, and payments for taxpayers and governments.
225What is Ad Valorem Tax?
Answer: Ad Valorem Tax = tax based on the VALUE of the good (Latin: "according to value"). GST is an ad valorem tax. Opposite is Specific Tax — fixed amount per unit regardless of value (e.g., ₹10 per litre).
226What is revenue from disinvestment classified as in the budget?
Answer: Capital Receipt — because selling government assets (shares in PSUs) reduces the government's ownership stake. Disinvestment proceeds go into the National Investment Fund (NIF).
227What is Public Debt?
Answer: Total borrowings of the central government — internal (from domestic market: G-secs, T-bills, small savings) and external (from foreign governments, IMF, World Bank). India's public debt is predominantly internal.
228What is a Gilt-Edged Security?
Answer: Government securities (G-Secs/Gilts) — bonds issued by the central government. Called "gilt-edged" because they carry no default risk (government guarantees). Counted as SLR-eligible securities for banks.
229What is the role of IMF in financial crises?
Answer: IMF provides emergency loans to countries facing Balance of Payments (BoP) crises, with conditions attached (structural reforms). India took IMF loan in 1991 BoP crisis and pledged gold as collateral.
230What is the World Bank? What does it do?
Answer: World Bank (est. 1944, HQ Washington DC) — provides long-term loans and grants to developing countries for development projects (education, health, infrastructure). Two arms: IBRD (middle-income) + IDA (poorest countries).
231What is ADB?
Answer: Asian Development Bank — est. 1966, HQ Manila, Philippines. 68 member countries. Provides loans/technical assistance for development in Asia-Pacific region. India is one of the largest borrowers.
232What is the WTO?
Answer: World Trade Organization — est. 1 January 1995, replacing GATT (1948). HQ: Geneva, Switzerland. 164 member countries. Governs international trade rules, resolves trade disputes.
233What is GATT?
Answer: General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade — est. 1948, Geneva. Predecessor to WTO. Multilateral agreement aimed at reducing tariffs and trade barriers. Replaced by WTO in 1995 after Uruguay Round.
234What is Most Favoured Nation (MFN) clause?
Answer: WTO principle requiring countries to treat all trading partners equally — if you give one country a trade advantage, you must give the same to all WTO members. Core principle of non-discrimination in trade.
235What is India's largest export and import?
Answer: Largest export: Petroleum products, Software/IT services, Gems & Jewellery, Pharmaceuticals. Largest import: Crude Oil (biggest import), Gold, Electronics, Machinery. India's trade balance is typically negative.
236What is India's approximate Forex Reserve?
Answer: India's foreign exchange reserves are approximately $650–700 billion — making India the 4th largest Forex reserve holder in the world (after China, Japan, Switzerland).
237What is SDR?
Answer: Special Drawing Rights — an international reserve asset created by IMF. Not a currency but a claim on IMF members' currencies. Its value is based on a basket of 5 currencies: USD, Euro, Yuan, Yen, GBP.
238India's rank in Remittances received? Approximate amount?
Answer: India is the world's largest recipient of remittances — approximately $125 billion annually. Major sources: USA, UAE, UK, Saudi Arabia, Qatar. Remittances exceed India's FDI inflows.
239What is Current Account Deficit (CAD)?
Answer: CAD = imports of goods, services, and transfers exceed exports. India typically has a CAD because it imports more than it exports (especially crude oil and gold). Financed by capital account surplus (FDI, FII).
240What is the difference between FDI and FII?
Answer: FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) = long-term investment in physical assets (factories, businesses) — stable. FII/FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investment) = investment in financial assets (stocks, bonds) — short-term, "hot money," volatile.
📉 Section 6: Inflation, Poverty & Employment (Q 241–280)
241What is Inflation?
Answer: Inflation = sustained, general rise in price levels over time. Money loses purchasing power — same amount buys fewer goods. Measured by CPI (Consumer Price Index) in India for policy purposes.
242What is Deflation?
Answer: Deflation = sustained fall in the general price level. Opposite of inflation. Can be dangerous — consumers delay purchases expecting further price falls → demand collapses → recession. Japan experienced prolonged deflation.
243What is Disinflation?
Answer: Disinflation = decrease in the RATE of inflation (inflation is still positive but falling). E.g., inflation falling from 8% to 4% is disinflation. Different from deflation (where prices actually fall).
244What is Stagflation?
Answer: Stagflation = high inflation + high unemployment + slow/stagnant growth simultaneously. Worst combination — standard policies don't work well. Occurred in 1970s (oil shocks). Breaks the Phillips Curve relationship.
245What is Hyperinflation? Give an example.
Answer: Hyperinflation = extremely rapid inflation, usually >50% per month. Money becomes worthless. Famous examples: Germany (1923) — price doubled every 3-4 days; Zimbabwe (2008) — 89.7 sextillion % annual inflation.
246What is Demand-Pull Inflation?
Answer: Inflation caused by excess demand over supply — "too much money chasing too few goods." Common during economic booms. Government spending, easy credit can trigger demand-pull inflation.
247What is Cost-Push Inflation?
Answer: Inflation caused by rising production costs — supply-side shock. Rising oil prices, wages, raw material costs push prices up. 1973 OPEC oil crisis caused cost-push inflation globally.
248What are the 5 types of inflation by rate?
Answer: 1. Creeping (1-3%) — mild, manageable. 2. Walking (3-10%) — moderate, needs attention. 3. Galloping (10-50%) — serious, damaging. 4. Hyperinflation (>50%/month) — catastrophic. 5. Stagflation — inflation + stagnation.
249What is CPI (Consumer Price Index)?
Answer: CPI measures changes in the retail price of a basket of goods and services consumed by households. Base year: 2011-12. Used by RBI as the official inflation measure for monetary policy. Compiled by MoSPI.
250What is WPI (Wholesale Price Index)?
Answer: WPI measures prices at the wholesale level (before retail). Tracks inflation in manufacturing and trade. Base year: 2011-12. Compiled by Office of Economic Adviser under DPIIT. Does NOT include services.
251What is the difference between CPI and WPI?
Answer: CPI: Retail prices, includes services, consumer basket, RBI uses for policy. WPI: Wholesale prices, no services, producer/trader level. WPI often leads CPI — wholesale price rise eventually reaches consumer.
252What is the Tendulkar Committee on Poverty?
Answer: Suresh Tendulkar Committee (2009) — revised India's poverty line. Rural: ₹816/month, Urban: ₹1000/month per person. Based on consumption of calories, protein, clothing, fuel, light, conveyance.
253What is the Rangarajan Committee on Poverty?
Answer: C. Rangarajan Committee (2014) gave higher poverty line — Rural: ₹972/month, Urban: ₹1407/month. Also included nutrition, clothing, rent, education, health in the consumption basket.
254What is MNREGA?
Answer: Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act — enacted 2005. Guarantees 100 days of wage employment per year to every rural household willing to do unskilled manual work. Implemented since Feb 2006.
255What is the Phillips Curve?
Answer: Phillips Curve shows an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment in the short run — when inflation is high, unemployment is low and vice versa. Proposed by A.W. Phillips in 1958 using UK data (1861-1957).
256Why does the Phillips Curve break down in the long run?
Answer: In the long run, the Phillips Curve is vertical at NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment). Stagflation in 1970s (high inflation + high unemployment) proved the short-run trade-off doesn't hold long-term.
257What is NAIRU?
Answer: Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment — the unemployment rate at which inflation is stable (neither rising nor falling). Also called "natural rate of unemployment." Includes frictional and structural unemployment.
258What is the Lorenz Curve?
Answer: Lorenz Curve shows income distribution inequality. X-axis = cumulative % of population; Y-axis = cumulative % of income. Diagonal (45°) = perfect equality. Greater the bow/distance from diagonal = greater inequality.
259What is the Gini Coefficient?
Answer: Gini Coefficient = area between Lorenz Curve and diagonal / total area below diagonal. Range: 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality). India's Gini ≈ 0.35. Higher value = more unequal.
260What is Absolute Poverty vs Relative Poverty?
Answer: Absolute Poverty = income/consumption below a fixed minimum threshold (poverty line). Relative Poverty = income significantly below the average income of the society. India measures absolute poverty; developed countries use relative.
261What is the Human Poverty Index (HPI)?
Answer: UNDP's measure of poverty beyond income. HPI-1 for developing countries (life expectancy, literacy, access to water/health). HPI-2 for OECD countries. Replaced by MPI (Multidimensional Poverty Index) in 2010.
262What is MPI (Multidimensional Poverty Index)?
Answer: MPI measures poverty across 3 dimensions and 10 indicators: Health (nutrition, child mortality), Education (years of schooling, school attendance), Living Standards (cooking fuel, sanitation, water, electricity, housing, assets). Published by UNDP/OPHI.
263What is the Fisher's Quantity Theory of Money?
Answer: MV = PT — where M = Money Supply, V = Velocity of Money, P = Price Level, T = Volume of Transactions. Proposed by Irving Fisher. If M increases while V and T are constant, P (prices/inflation) increases proportionally.
264What is Velocity of Money?
Answer: Velocity of Money = number of times a unit of money changes hands in a given period. If ₹100 is used for 5 transactions, velocity = 5. Higher velocity → money circulates faster → greater economic activity.
265What is the Crowding Out Effect?
Answer: When government borrows heavily, interest rates rise → private investment falls (crowded out). Government "crowds out" private investment. Reduces effectiveness of fiscal stimulus. Opposite = Crowding In Effect.
266What is the Paradox of Thrift?
Answer: Keynes' insight: While individual saving is good, if EVERYONE saves simultaneously, total spending falls → GDP falls → income falls → everyone ends up saving less. Aggregate saving paradoxically decreases.
267What is BPL (Below Poverty Line)?
Answer: BPL = people living below the officially defined poverty line. They receive government benefits under food security, housing, health schemes. Identification methods have changed over years (Lakdawala → Tendulkar → Rangarajan).
268What is AAY (Antyodaya Anna Yojana)?
Answer: AAY = scheme for the "poorest of the poor" — most vulnerable households below BPL. Launched 2000. These households get 35 kg of food grain per month at ₹3/kg (rice) and ₹2/kg (wheat) under NFSA.
269What percentage of India's population is covered under NFSA?
Answer: National Food Security Act 2013 covers approximately 67% of India's population (75% rural + 50% urban). Entitled to subsidised food grain from PDS — 5 kg per person per month at ₹1-3/kg.
270What is Structural Adjustment Programme (SAP)?
Answer: IMF/World Bank conditions attached to loans — typically require: privatisation, deregulation, fiscal austerity, trade liberalisation, currency devaluation. India implemented SAP elements after 1991 BoP crisis.
271What is the base year for CPI and WPI in India?
Answer: Both CPI and WPI use 2011-12 as the base year in India currently. Index = 100 in base year. Rise in index shows inflation; fall shows deflation.
272What is the Poverty Trap?
Answer: A cycle where poverty itself causes conditions that perpetuate poverty — poor people can't save → can't invest → remain poor. Breaking the poverty trap requires government intervention (education, health, credit access).
273What is MNREGA's significance?
Answer: MNREGA is a demand-driven scheme — if applicants don't get work within 15 days, they are entitled to unemployment allowance. It creates durable rural assets (roads, ponds, plantations) while providing employment.
274What is the difference between Creeping and Walking inflation?
Answer: Creeping Inflation (1-3%) = mild and generally considered healthy for economic growth — encourages investment. Walking Inflation (3-10%) = moderate, warning sign, central bank begins policy action.
275Who decides India's official poverty line?
Answer: NITI Aayog (earlier Planning Commission) adopts the poverty line methodology. The Tendulkar methodology (2009) is the most recently implemented. The Rangarajan Committee (2014) recommendations are also referenced.
276What is Demand-Side vs Supply-Side economics?
Answer: Demand-Side (Keynesian): Boost government spending to stimulate demand → economic recovery. Supply-Side (Reaganomics/Trickle-down): Cut taxes for businesses and rich → they invest more → benefits trickle down to poor.
277What causes Inflation according to Monetarists (Milton Friedman)?
Answer: "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" — Milton Friedman. Excessive growth in money supply causes inflation. Monetarists advocate controlling money supply as the primary anti-inflation tool.
278What is Core Inflation vs Headline Inflation?
Answer: Headline Inflation = total CPI including food and fuel (volatile). Core Inflation = CPI excluding food and fuel — shows underlying inflation trend. RBI monitors both but targets headline CPI.
279What is the Employment Elasticity?
Answer: Employment Elasticity = % change in employment / % change in GDP. Shows how much job growth occurs per unit of economic growth. Low employment elasticity means "jobless growth" — GDP grows but jobs don't increase proportionally.
280What is the difference between Unemployment Rate and Labour Force Participation Rate (LFPR)?
Answer: Unemployment Rate = unemployed ÷ labour force. LFPR = labour force ÷ working-age population. If LFPR falls (discouraged workers stop looking), unemployment rate may fall even without new jobs. India's LFPR is around 40-42%.
🔬 Section 7: Micro Economics (Q 281–350)
281State the Law of Demand.
Answer: "Other things being equal (ceteris paribus), as the price of a good rises, its quantity demanded falls, and vice versa." Inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded. Demand curve slopes downward (left to right).
282What are the exceptions to the Law of Demand?
Answer: 1. Giffen Goods (inferior goods where demand rises with price). 2. Veblen Goods (prestige goods — demand rises with price). 3. Speculation (buying more as price rises, expecting further rise). 4. Emergencies.
283What are Giffen Goods?
Answer: Giffen Goods are inferior goods where demand INCREASES when price rises — violating the Law of Demand. Named after Sir Robert Giffen. Classic example: staple food (bread, rice) — when price rises, poor people can't afford better food and buy more of it.
284What are Veblen Goods?
Answer: Veblen Goods are luxury/prestige goods where higher price INCREASES demand — because higher price signals exclusivity and status. Named after Thorstein Veblen. Examples: luxury watches, designer bags, luxury cars.
285State the Law of Supply.
Answer: "Other things being equal, as the price of a good rises, its quantity supplied also rises, and vice versa." Direct/positive relationship between price and quantity supplied. Supply curve slopes upward (left to right).
286What is Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)?
Answer: PED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in price. Measures how responsive demand is to price changes. PED > 1 = Elastic. PED < 1 = Inelastic. PED = 1 = Unitary elastic. PED = 0 = Perfectly inelastic.
287Give examples of goods with Inelastic Demand.
Answer: Inelastic demand (PED < 1): Essential goods — Salt, medicines, insulin, tobacco, petrol. Even if price rises, consumers must buy them. Government taxes inelastic goods to generate revenue (salt, tobacco).
288Give examples of goods with Elastic Demand.
Answer: Elastic demand (PED > 1): Luxury goods, goods with many substitutes — cars, air travel, branded clothes, chocolates. Small price increase → significant fall in demand.
289What are Substitute Goods? Give examples.
Answer: Substitute Goods = goods that can replace each other. Rise in price of one increases demand for the other. Examples: Tea and Coffee, Pepsi and Coca-Cola, Butter and Margarine, Rice and Wheat. Positive cross-elasticity.
290What are Complementary Goods? Give examples.
Answer: Complementary Goods = goods used together. Rise in price of one decreases demand for both. Examples: Car and Petrol, Pen and Ink, Bread and Butter, Tea and Sugar. Negative cross-elasticity of demand.
291What is a Normal Good?
Answer: Normal Good = demand increases as income increases. Positive income elasticity. As people get richer, they buy more. Examples: Branded clothes, cars, restaurant meals, electronics. Income elasticity > 0.
292What is an Inferior Good?
Answer: Inferior Good = demand DECREASES as income increases. Negative income elasticity. When income rises, consumers switch to better alternatives. Examples: cheap local bus, generic brands, coarse grains. Income elasticity < 0.
293What is a Public Good?
Answer: Public Good = Non-excludable (cannot prevent anyone from using) + Non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce availability for others). Examples: National defence, street lights, public parks, clean air, lighthouse. Market fails to provide these → government must.
294What is Consumer Surplus?
Answer: Consumer Surplus = Willingness to Pay − Actual Price Paid. The "extra benefit" consumers receive. Example: Willing to pay ₹100 for a book, but pay ₹70 → Consumer Surplus = ₹30. Area above price line and below demand curve.
295State the Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility.
Answer: "As a consumer consumes more and more units of a good, the additional satisfaction (marginal utility) from each successive unit diminishes." E.g., 1st glass of water = very satisfying; 5th glass = much less satisfying.
296What is Marginal Utility (MU)?
Answer: MU = additional utility gained from consuming one more unit of a good. Formula: MU = ΔTU / ΔQ (change in total utility / change in quantity). When MU becomes zero, TU is maximum.
297What is Perfect Competition?
Answer: Market structure where: Many buyers and sellers, homogeneous product, free entry/exit, perfect information, no single firm controls price. Each firm is a price-taker (P = MR = AR = MC in equilibrium). Example: Agricultural markets.
298What is Monopoly?
Answer: Single seller, unique product with no close substitutes, high barriers to entry. Monopolist is a price-maker (MR < AR). Can earn supernormal profits long-term. Example: Govt-owned railways, utilities.
299What is Monopolistic Competition?
Answer: Many sellers selling differentiated (similar but not identical) products. Each firm has some price-making power. Free entry/exit. Examples: Toothpaste brands, soap brands, restaurants. Short-run profits, zero long-run profits.
300What is Oligopoly?
Answer: Few large sellers dominating the market with high barriers to entry. Firms are interdependent — each firm's decision affects others. Examples: Telecom (Jio, Airtel, Vi), aviation sector, automobile industry.
301What is a Cartel?
Answer: Cartel = formal agreement among oligopolistic firms to fix prices, limit production, and divide markets to maximise joint profits. Example: OPEC (oil cartel). Anti-competitive — regulated by CCI in India.
302In Perfect Competition, what is the relationship between P, MR, AR?
Answer: In Perfect Competition: P = MR = AR (Price = Marginal Revenue = Average Revenue). The demand curve is horizontal (perfectly elastic) for an individual firm — it is a price-taker.
303In Monopoly, what is the relationship between P, MR, AR?
Answer: In Monopoly: MR < AR (=P). The monopolist faces a downward-sloping demand curve. To sell more, it must lower price for all units. So MR falls faster than AR. MR can become negative.
304What is the profit-maximizing condition?
Answer: MR = MC (Marginal Revenue = Marginal Cost) — at this point, producing one more unit adds equal cost and revenue, so profit is maximized. Valid for all market structures (PC, Monopoly, Oligopoly, Monopolistic Competition).
305What is Break-even Point?
Answer: Break-even Point = where Total Revenue = Total Cost (TR = TC). The firm earns zero profit (normal profit). Below break-even = loss; above = profit. In Per-unit terms: Price = Average Total Cost (P = ATC).
306What is the Law of Variable Proportions?
Answer: "As we add more of a variable input (labour) to fixed inputs (capital/land), output first rises at increasing rate, then at decreasing rate, and eventually falls." Three stages: Increasing returns → Diminishing returns → Negative returns.
307What is Market Failure?
Answer: Market Failure = when free markets fail to allocate resources efficiently. Five causes: 1. Externalities 2. Public goods 3. Information asymmetry 4. Monopoly 5. Inequality. Government intervenes to correct market failures.
308What is a Positive Externality? Give example.
Answer: Positive Externality = third-party benefit NOT reflected in market price. Examples: Education (benefits society beyond the individual), vaccination (protects herd immunity), beekeeping (pollinates nearby crops). Government subsidises to encourage.
309What is a Negative Externality? Give example.
Answer: Negative Externality = third-party cost NOT reflected in market price. Examples: Factory pollution (cost borne by society), smoking (harms others), traffic congestion. Government imposes Pigouvian tax to correct.
310What is Pigouvian Tax?
Answer: A tax equal to the external cost (negative externality) imposed on polluters — to make them pay the full social cost. Named after A.C. Pigou. Examples: Carbon tax, tobacco tax, plastic bag tax.
311What are Economies of Scale?
Answer: Economies of Scale = cost advantages gained as production increases — average cost falls as output grows. Caused by: specialisation, bulk purchasing, spreading fixed costs. Large firms benefit. Opposite = Diseconomies of Scale.
312What is Fixed Cost and Variable Cost?
Answer: Fixed Cost = does not change with output level (rent, machinery, salaries of permanent staff). Variable Cost = changes with output (raw materials, wages of daily workers, electricity). TC = FC + VC.
313What is Marginal Cost (MC)?
Answer: MC = additional cost of producing one more unit. MC = ΔTC / ΔQ. MC curve is U-shaped — falls initially then rises. When MC < AC, AC falls. When MC > AC, AC rises. MC intersects AC at its minimum.
314What is Returns to Scale?
Answer: Returns to Scale = how output responds when ALL inputs are increased proportionately. IRS (Increasing Returns to Scale): output increases more than proportionately. CRS: proportionately. DRS (Decreasing): less than proportionately.
315What is Price Discrimination in Monopoly?
Answer: Price Discrimination = charging different prices to different consumers for the same product. Examples: Railway fares (AC vs sleeper), airline dynamic pricing, cinema morning vs evening shows, medicines (different countries).
316What is a Natural Monopoly?
Answer: Natural Monopoly = a market where one firm can serve all consumers at lower cost than multiple firms due to very high fixed costs and low marginal costs. Examples: Railways, water supply, electricity grid. Usually government-owned.
317What is Deadweight Loss?
Answer: Deadweight Loss = loss of economic efficiency when market equilibrium is not achieved — caused by monopoly pricing, taxes, price floors/ceilings. It represents transactions that don't occur that would have been beneficial.
318What is Information Asymmetry?
Answer: When one party in a transaction has more information than the other. Leads to market failure. Examples: Used car market (seller knows car's history better — "lemons problem"), insurance market (moral hazard, adverse selection).
319What is the Equi-Marginal Utility principle?
Answer: A consumer achieves maximum satisfaction when MU/Price is equal for all goods consumed. MUx/Px = MUy/Py = MUz/Pz = MU of money. Consumer redistributes spending to equalise marginal utility per rupee.
320What is Market Equilibrium?
Answer: Market Equilibrium = point where Quantity Demanded = Quantity Supplied. At equilibrium price (market-clearing price), there is no surplus or shortage. If price is above equilibrium → surplus; below → shortage.
321What causes a shift in the Demand Curve?
Answer: Demand curve shifts (NOT moves along) when non-price factors change: Income change, tastes/preferences change, price of substitutes/complements changes, population change, expectations change. Rightward shift = increase in demand.
322What is Monopsony?
Answer: Monopsony = a market with a single BUYER. The buyer has market power to set prices below competitive level. Example: A single employer in a town (buyer of labour), government as sole buyer of defence equipment.
323What is Adverse Selection?
Answer: Adverse Selection = a problem arising from information asymmetry BEFORE a transaction — bad risks are more likely to seek insurance/credit. E.g., unhealthy people more likely to buy health insurance → insurer gets adverse pool of customers.
324What is Moral Hazard?
Answer: Moral Hazard = when one party takes more risks because they don't bear the full consequences (information asymmetry after transaction). E.g., insured person drives more recklessly; banks take excessive risks when expecting government bailout.
325What is Total Revenue (TR), Average Revenue (AR), and Marginal Revenue (MR)?
Answer: TR = Price × Quantity. AR = TR/Q = Price. MR = ΔTR/ΔQ (additional revenue from one more unit). In PC: MR = AR = P. In Monopoly: MR < AR < P because price must be reduced to sell more.
326What are the conditions for Perfect Competition?
Answer: 1. Large number of buyers and sellers. 2. Homogeneous product. 3. Free entry and exit. 4. Perfect information. 5. No transport costs. 6. No government intervention. 7. Firms are price-takers.
327What is Indifference Curve?
Answer: Indifference Curve = shows all combinations of two goods that give a consumer equal satisfaction. Properties: Downward sloping, convex to origin, cannot intersect, higher IC = higher satisfaction. MRS (Marginal Rate of Substitution) = slope.
328What is the CCI (Competition Commission of India)?
Answer: CCI was established under the Competition Act 2002. It prevents anti-competitive practices (cartels, predatory pricing), regulates mergers that may reduce competition, and ensures markets work competitively for consumer benefit.
329What is Producer Surplus?
Answer: Producer Surplus = Actual Price Received − Minimum Price willing to accept. The extra benefit producers receive beyond their supply price. Area below price line and above supply curve. Together with Consumer Surplus = Total Social Welfare.
330What is the Budget Line (Budget Constraint)?
Answer: Budget Line shows all combinations of two goods a consumer can buy with a given income at given prices. Consumer's equilibrium = where highest Indifference Curve is tangent to Budget Line (MRS = Price ratio).
331What is Cross Elasticity of Demand?
Answer: Cross Elasticity = % change in demand for Good A / % change in price of Good B. Positive = Substitutes. Negative = Complements. Zero = Unrelated goods. Used to determine market boundaries in competition law.
332What is Income Elasticity of Demand?
Answer: Income Elasticity = % change in quantity demanded / % change in income. Positive = Normal good. Negative = Inferior good. >1 = Luxury good. <1 = Necessity. 0 = income doesn't affect demand.
333What is the difference between the short run and long run in economics?
Answer: Short Run = at least one input is fixed (cannot be changed — e.g., factory size). Long Run = ALL inputs can be varied. In long run, firms can enter/exit markets, build new factories, change all cost structures.
334What is the concept of Externality in one sentence?
Answer: Externality = a cost or benefit imposed on a third party (not involved in the transaction) that is not reflected in the market price. Negative externalities = market overproduces. Positive externalities = market underproduces.
335What is a Merit Good?
Answer: Merit Good = a good that society believes people should have regardless of ability to pay. Often underconsumed in free markets. Examples: Education, healthcare, housing. Government provides/subsidises merit goods.
336What is the distinction between Demand and Quantity Demanded?
Answer: Demand = the entire relationship (schedule/curve) between price and quantity. Quantity Demanded = specific amount demanded at a particular price. Price change → movement along curve (change in QD). Income/other changes → shift of curve (change in Demand).
337What is Asymmetric Information? Who proposed it?
Answer: Asymmetric Information = when parties to a transaction have different information. George Akerlof's "Market for Lemons" paper (1970, Nobel 2001) showed how information asymmetry can destroy markets. Led to signalling theory, adverse selection concepts.
338What is Price Ceiling and Price Floor?
Answer: Price Ceiling = Maximum price set below equilibrium (e.g., rent control, MSP for consumers) → causes shortage. Price Floor = Minimum price set above equilibrium (e.g., MSP for farmers, minimum wage) → causes surplus.
339What is Subsidy and how does it affect supply?
Answer: Subsidy = government payment to producers to reduce their costs → supply curve shifts rightward → price falls, quantity increases. Examples: Fertiliser subsidy, food subsidy, LPG subsidy. Opposite effect of tax.
340What is the long-run equilibrium in Perfect Competition?
Answer: In the long run, perfect competition results in Zero Economic Profit (Normal Profit only). If firms earn profit → new firms enter → price falls to cost level → zero profit. If loss → firms exit → price rises to cost level. P = MR = MC = AC = AR.
341What is a Luxury Good? Give examples.
Answer: Luxury Good = income elasticity > 1 — demand rises more than proportionately with income. Examples: International holidays, luxury cars, jewellery, fine dining, premium gadgets. Poor people don't demand them; rich do.
342What is the significance of MC = AC (Minimum of AC)?
Answer: When MC = AC, the Average Cost is at its minimum point (bottom of U-shape). When MC < AC → AC is falling. When MC > AC → AC is rising. MC always intersects AC at its lowest point. Important for finding efficient scale of production.
343What are the barriers to entry in Monopoly?
Answer: 1. Government license/patent (legal monopoly). 2. Control over key resources (e.g., OPEC controls oil). 3. Very high start-up costs (natural monopoly). 4. Network effects. 5. Economies of scale making new entry unprofitable.
344What is the difference between Micro and Macro Equilibrium?
Answer: Micro Equilibrium = equilibrium of a single market (demand = supply for one good). Macro Equilibrium = equilibrium of the entire economy (aggregate demand = aggregate supply, full employment).
345What is Allocative Efficiency?
Answer: Allocative Efficiency = resources are allocated to produce goods that consumers most value. Achieved when P = MC (price equals marginal cost). Perfect competition achieves allocative efficiency; monopoly does not (P > MC).
346What is Productive Efficiency?
Answer: Productive Efficiency = production at lowest possible cost — operating at minimum point of AC curve. Achieved in long-run perfect competition (P = minimum AC). Means no waste of resources.
347What is the difference between Elastic and Inelastic Supply?
Answer: Elastic Supply: Quantity supplied is very responsive to price changes (PES > 1) — manufactured goods. Inelastic Supply: Quantity supplied responds little to price (PES < 1) — agricultural goods (limited by seasons/land).
348What are the five causes of Market Failure?
Answer: 1. Externalities (costs/benefits not priced) 2. Public Goods (non-excludable, non-rival) 3. Information Asymmetry (unequal info) 4. Monopoly Power (no competition) 5. Inequality (market distributes income unequally). Government corrects each.
349What is Perfect Price Elasticity of Supply?
Answer: Perfect Elastic Supply (PES = ∞): Suppliers will supply any amount at the prevailing price — horizontal supply curve. Perfect Inelastic Supply (PES = 0): Fixed supply regardless of price — vertical supply curve. Example: Fixed supply of land.
350What is the concept of Opportunity Cost in PPF?
Answer: On the PPF, opportunity cost of producing more of Good A = amount of Good B that must be given up. The slope of PPF = opportunity cost. If PPF is concave (bowed out), opportunity cost increases as production shifts — Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost.
📊 Section 8: Macro Economics (Q 351–420)
351What is M0 (Reserve Money / High-Powered Money)?
Answer: M0 = Currency in circulation + Bankers' deposits with RBI + Other deposits with RBI. M0 is the monetary base — the foundation on which banks create money through lending. Also called "High-Powered Money."
352What is M1?
Answer: M1 = Currency with public + Demand deposits with banks + Other deposits with RBI. M1 is the most liquid measure of money — represents money available for immediate transactions. "Narrow Money."
353What is M3 (Broad Money)?
Answer: M3 = M1 + Time deposits with banks. The most commonly used measure of money supply in India. Also called "Aggregate Monetary Resources (AMR)" or Broad Money. Used by RBI for monetary policy.
354What is the Money Multiplier?
Answer: Money Multiplier = 1 / CRR. If CRR = 4% → Multiplier = 25. Meaning ₹100 of new deposit creates ₹2,500 of total money in the banking system through successive rounds of deposits and lending.
355What is MPC (Marginal Propensity to Consume)?
Answer: MPC = Change in Consumption / Change in Income. If income rises by ₹100 and consumption rises by ₹80, MPC = 0.8. MPC is between 0 and 1. Higher MPC in low-income groups.
356What is MPS (Marginal Propensity to Save)?
Answer: MPS = Change in Saving / Change in Income = 1 − MPC. If MPC = 0.8, then MPS = 0.2 (20% of additional income is saved). MPC + MPS = 1 always.
357What is the Fiscal Multiplier (Keynesian Multiplier)?
Answer: Fiscal Multiplier = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPS. If MPC = 0.8, Multiplier = 1/(1-0.8) = 1/0.2 = 5. Meaning ₹1 of government spending generates ₹5 of total GDP increase through successive spending rounds.
358What is the EXAM TRAP — Money Multiplier vs Fiscal Multiplier?
Answer: Money Multiplier = 1/CRR (banking concept — how deposits multiply in banking system). Fiscal/Keynesian Multiplier = 1/(1−MPC) (economics concept — how government spending multiplies in economy). They are completely different!
359What is Keynesian Economics?
Answer: Keynesian economics advocates government intervention to stabilise the economy — especially during recessions. Key idea: AD = C + I + G + (X-M). Government increases G to boost AD during recession. Wage and price rigidity means markets don't self-correct quickly.
360What is the Liquidity Trap?
Answer: Liquidity Trap = when interest rates are near zero and people hoard cash instead of spending/investing. Monetary policy becomes ineffective — reducing interest rates further doesn't stimulate economy. Fiscal policy (government spending) is the solution. Keynes identified this.
361What are Business Cycles?
Answer: Business Cycles = recurring fluctuations in economic activity around its long-run trend. Four phases: 1. Expansion (Recovery) → 2. Peak (Boom) → 3. Contraction (Recession) → 4. Trough (Depression) → back to expansion.
362What is HDI (Human Development Index)?
Answer: HDI = Geometric Mean of 3 indices: 1. Life Expectancy Index (health) 2. Education Index (mean + expected years of schooling) 3. GNI per capita Index (income). Published by UNDP annually. India's rank ≈ 134.
363Who developed the concept of HDI?
Answer: Mahbub ul Haq (Pakistani economist) developed the HDI concept, with intellectual support from Amartya Sen (India). First published in 1990 UNDP Human Development Report.
364What is India's HDI category?
Answer: India is in the Medium Human Development category (HDI score approximately 0.644, rank ~134 out of 193 countries). Norway is consistently ranked 1st (Very High HDI). India aims to improve health, education, and income indicators.
365What is Aggregate Demand (AD)?
Answer: AD = C + I + G + (X − M) — Total demand for goods and services in an economy. Keynes' key equation. AD curve slopes downward (higher price level → lower real output demanded). Shifts right = economic expansion.
366What is Aggregate Supply (AS)?
Answer: AS = total quantity of goods and services produced in the economy at different price levels. Short-run AS slopes upward. Long-run AS is vertical at potential GDP (full employment). Macro equilibrium = AD = AS.
367What is the difference between Expansionary and Contractionary monetary policy?
Answer: Expansionary: RBI decreases Repo Rate/CRR → cheaper credit → more money in economy → stimulates growth (used during recession). Contractionary: RBI increases Repo Rate/CRR → costly credit → less money → controls inflation (used during high inflation).
368What is the difference between Expansionary and Contractionary fiscal policy?
Answer: Expansionary Fiscal Policy: Increase G or reduce taxes → stimulates demand (during recession). Contractionary Fiscal Policy: Reduce G or increase taxes → reduces demand (during high inflation). India typically uses deficit budgets (expansionary).
369What is Potential GDP?
Answer: Potential GDP = maximum sustainable output an economy can produce when all resources are fully employed (at NAIRU). Actual GDP fluctuates around Potential GDP. GDP Gap = Potential GDP − Actual GDP.
370What is the Crowding Out Effect?
Answer: Government borrowing raises interest rates → private sector investment is "crowded out" (reduced) because businesses face higher borrowing costs. Reduces effectiveness of fiscal stimulus. Strong crowding out = multiplier effect diminishes.
371What is M2 money supply?
Answer: M2 = M1 + Savings deposits with Post Office Savings Banks. Slightly broader than M1 but narrower than M3. Intermediate measure of money supply used for tracking liquidity in the economy.
372What is M4 money supply?
Answer: M4 = M3 + All deposits with Post Office Savings Banks (excluding NSCs — National Savings Certificates). The broadest measure of money supply in India. Least liquid of all money supply measures.
373What is Demographic Dividend? Is it automatic?
Answer: Demographic Dividend = economic growth potential from a large working-age population (more producers, fewer dependents). NOT automatic — requires investment in education, healthcare, skill development to convert population into productive workers.
374India's working-age population percentage and demographic window?
Answer: India has approximately 65% of population in working age (15-64 years). Demographic dividend window: 2020-2050. India surpassed China as world's most populous country in 2023.
375What is the Paradox of Thrift? Who proposed it?
Answer: Proposed by John Maynard Keynes. "What is individually rational (saving) can be collectively harmful." If everyone saves simultaneously, total spending falls → firms produce less → income falls → saving falls. Individual virtue becomes collective vice.
376What is the difference between Recession and Depression?
Answer: Recession = two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth — mild, temporary. Depression = prolonged, severe recession lasting years with very high unemployment, massive fall in output. The Great Depression (1929-1939) is the classic example.
377What is Okun's Law?
Answer: Okun's Law = empirical relationship between GDP growth and unemployment. For every 1% rise in unemployment above natural rate, GDP falls by approximately 2% below potential. Shows trade-off between output and unemployment.
378What is the Keynesian "sticky wages and prices" concept?
Answer: Keynes argued wages and prices are "sticky" (downward rigid) — don't fall easily during recessions. So markets don't self-correct quickly to restore full employment. Government intervention (fiscal stimulus) is needed to restore equilibrium.
379What is the Natural Rate of Unemployment?
Answer: Natural Rate of Unemployment = Frictional + Structural unemployment. It's the unemployment rate when economy is at full employment (no cyclical unemployment). Also called NAIRU. Cannot be eliminated by demand management policies.
380What is Secular Stagnation?
Answer: Secular Stagnation = a prolonged period of slow economic growth, low interest rates, and insufficient demand. Term coined by Alvin Hansen (1938), revived by Lawrence Summers (2013). Caused by excess savings, aging population, declining investment opportunities.
381What is India's inflation target set for RBI?
Answer: RBI's inflation target = 4% CPI, with a tolerance band of ±2% (i.e., 2% to 6%). If RBI fails to achieve this for 3 consecutive quarters, it must explain to the government. This framework was adopted in 2016.
382What is the Sensex?
Answer: Sensex (Sensitive Index) = tracks the top 30 companies on the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE). BSE established in 1875 — Asia's oldest stock exchange. Base year: 1978-79 = 100. A rising Sensex indicates market optimism.
383What is Nifty 50?
Answer: Nifty 50 = benchmark index of National Stock Exchange (NSE) tracking top 50 companies. NSE established in 1992. Base year: November 1995 = 1000. Nifty = National + Fifty. More diversified than Sensex.
384What is the difference between Primary Market and Secondary Market?
Answer: Primary Market = new securities are issued for the first time (IPO — Initial Public Offering). Company raises fresh capital. Secondary Market = existing securities are traded between investors (Stock Exchanges — BSE, NSE). Company gets no new money.
385What is IPO (Initial Public Offering)?
Answer: IPO = when a private company offers shares to the public for the first time to raise capital from the stock market. The company gets listed on a stock exchange after IPO. Example: LIC IPO (2022) — India's largest ever IPO.
386What is NAV (Net Asset Value) in Mutual Funds?
Answer: NAV = (Total value of fund's assets − Liabilities) / Number of units outstanding. It is the per-unit price of a mutual fund. Calculated daily. If NAV rises, your investment has grown. Used to buy/sell mutual fund units.
387What is the Capital Account in Balance of Payments?
Answer: Capital Account in BoP records flows of financial assets — FDI, FPI/FII, External Commercial Borrowings (ECBs), banking capital. India typically runs a capital account surplus that finances the current account deficit.
388What are SDGs related to economics? (key ones)
Answer: SDG 1 (No Poverty), SDG 2 (Zero Hunger), SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth), SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation, Infrastructure), SDG 10 (Reduced Inequality), SDG 17 (Partnerships for the Goals).
389How many SDGs are there and when were they adopted?
Answer: 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) with 169 targets. Adopted by UN in September 2015. Deadline: 2030. Replaced MDGs (Millennium Development Goals — 8 goals, 2000-2015).
390What is the Census 2011 total population and sex ratio?
Answer: Total population: 121.09 crore (1.21 billion). Sex Ratio: 943 females per 1000 males. Child sex ratio (0-6 years): 914 (alarming decline). Literacy: 74.04%. Density: 382 persons/sq km.
391Which state has the highest and lowest literacy as per Census 2011?
Answer: Highest literacy: Kerala (93.91%). Lowest literacy: Bihar (63.82%). India's overall literacy: 74.04% (Male 82.14%, Female 65.46%). Female literacy gap is a key development challenge.
392Which state has highest population density as per Census 2011?
Answer: Bihar (1106 persons/sq km) has the highest population density among states. Among UTs, Delhi has the highest density. Lowest density state: Arunachal Pradesh (17/sq km).
393What is the difference between GDP and GNP for India?
Answer: For India, GNP is slightly higher than GDP because India receives significant remittances from Indians working abroad (positive NFIA). However, the gap has narrowed as foreign investment income paid abroad has also grown.
394What is the Solow Growth Model?
Answer: Robert Solow's neoclassical growth model — long-run economic growth is driven by technological progress (not just capital/labour accumulation). Capital accumulation faces diminishing returns; only tech progress causes sustained per capita growth. Nobel 1987.
395What is the India-specific meaning of "Hindu Rate of Growth"?
Answer: "Hindu Rate of Growth" = India's sluggish GDP growth of approximately 3.5% per annum (1950-1980). Term coined by economist Raj Krishna. Compared unfavourably with high-growth East Asian economies. Changed after 1991 reforms.
396What is an Economic Bubble?
Answer: Economic Bubble = rapid price escalation of an asset beyond its intrinsic value, driven by speculation. When bubble bursts → prices collapse → financial crisis. Famous examples: Dot-com bubble (2000), US Housing Bubble (2008 financial crisis).
397What is the Twin Deficit problem?
Answer: Twin Deficit = simultaneous Fiscal Deficit (government spending > revenue) + Current Account Deficit (imports > exports). India faces twin deficits in some years. Both deficits together put pressure on currency and interest rates.
398What is the difference between Economic Growth and Inclusive Growth?
Answer: Economic Growth = rise in GDP. Inclusive Growth = growth that benefits all sections of society — reduces poverty, inequality, creates employment, ensures social equity. India's planning shifted focus towards inclusive growth in recent decades.
399What is the Sustainable Development vs Economic Growth trade-off?
Answer: Rapid economic growth often degrades the environment (pollution, resource depletion). Sustainable development = "meeting current needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet their needs" (Brundtland Commission, 1987).
400What is the difference between Economic and Social indicators of development?
Answer: Economic indicators: GDP, Per Capita Income, Savings Rate, Investment Rate. Social indicators: Life Expectancy, Literacy Rate, Infant Mortality Rate, HDI, Gender Development Index (GDI). Development requires improvement in both.
401What is the NITI Aayog SDG Index?
Answer: NITI Aayog publishes an annual SDG India Index that tracks India's states/UTs on progress towards all 17 SDGs. States are ranked — "Front Runner" (65-99), "Performer" (50-64), "Aspirant" (0-49). Kerala consistently ranks high.
402What is the Gig Economy?
Answer: Gig Economy = labor market of short-term, freelance, or contract work rather than permanent jobs. Enabled by digital platforms (Uber, Zomato, Upwork). Workers are "gig workers." Growing rapidly in India — Ola/Uber drivers, Swiggy/Zomato delivery persons.
403What is India's Foreign Exchange Reserve composition?
Answer: India's forex reserves consist of: Foreign Currency Assets (largest component, ~90%), Gold reserves, SDRs (Special Drawing Rights), and Reserve Tranche Position with IMF. Total ≈ $650-700 billion.
404What is an Exchange Rate? Types?
Answer: Exchange Rate = price of one currency in terms of another. Types: Fixed (govt pegs value) vs Floating/Flexible (market determines). India has a managed float — RBI intervenes to prevent excessive volatility.
405What is Currency Depreciation vs Devaluation?
Answer: Depreciation = fall in currency value due to market forces (floating exchange rate). Devaluation = deliberate reduction in currency value by government/central bank (fixed exchange rate system). Both make exports cheaper and imports costlier.
406What is the effect of Rupee depreciation on India?
Answer: Rupee depreciation: Exports become cheaper (good for exporters); Imports become costlier (bad — especially crude oil, which increases India's import bill and inflation). NRIs sending remittances gain more in rupees. Inflation rises.
407What is the India-China comparison on Population (2023)?
Answer: India surpassed China in 2023 to become the world's most populous country. India: approximately 1.43 billion; China: approximately 1.42 billion. UN Population Fund confirmed this. India's population is still growing; China's is declining.
408What is the World Bank's "Ease of Doing Business" ranking for India?
Answer: India improved significantly in Ease of Doing Business — rose from rank 142 (2014) to rank 63 (2019). Key improvements: Starting a business, getting electricity, resolving insolvency (IBC 2016), paying taxes. WB discontinued this index in 2021.
409What is PMGSY?
Answer: Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana — launched in 2000. Aims to provide all-weather road connectivity to unconnected habitations in rural areas. Funded by Central Government. Critical for rural development and market access for farmers.
410What is Ayushman Bharat?
Answer: Ayushman Bharat (PM-JAY) — launched 2018. World's largest health insurance scheme. Provides health cover of ₹5 lakh per family per year for hospitalisation. Covers over 50 crore beneficiaries (poor and vulnerable families).
411What is Make in India?
Answer: Make in India — launched September 2014. Initiative to attract investment, foster innovation, enhance skill development, and build best-in-class manufacturing infrastructure. Aims to increase manufacturing's share in GDP from ~17% to 25%.
412What is Digital India?
Answer: Digital India — launched July 2015. Programme to transform India into a digitally empowered society. Key pillars: Digital infrastructure, Digital services, Digital literacy. Includes e-governance, DigiLocker, UMANG app, UPI ecosystem.
413What is Atmanirbhar Bharat?
Answer: Atmanirbhar Bharat (Self-Reliant India) — announced May 2020 (COVID-19 pandemic). Stimulus package of ₹20 lakh crore. Focus on: Local manufacturing, MSMEs, agriculture reform, labour reforms, defence manufacturing. "Vocal for Local."
414What is PM-KISAN?
Answer: Pradhan Mantri Kisan Samman Nidhi — launched 2019. Provides ₹6,000 per year (₹2,000 every 4 months) as direct income support to small and marginal farmer families. Transferred directly to bank accounts (DBT). Covers 11+ crore farmers.
415What is MUDRA scheme?
Answer: Micro Units Development and Refinance Agency (MUDRA) — launched 2015. Provides loans to micro enterprises (non-corporate, non-farm). Three categories: Shishu (up to ₹50,000), Kishore (₹50,000-5 lakh), Tarun (₹5-10 lakh).
416What is Startup India?
Answer: Startup India — launched January 2016. Initiative to build a strong ecosystem for startups. Benefits: Tax exemptions (3 years), self-certification, fast-track patent, ₹10,000 crore fund of funds. India has the 3rd largest startup ecosystem globally (unicorns 100+).
417What is the difference between MUDRA Shishu, Kishore, and Tarun?
Answer: Shishu = loans up to ₹50,000 (very small businesses, street vendors). Kishore = ₹50,001 to ₹5 lakh (established micro businesses). Tarun = ₹5 lakh to ₹10 lakh (growing enterprises needing expansion capital).
418What is India's GDP growth target (Vision 2047)?
Answer: India aims to become a Developed Nation (Viksit Bharat) by 2047 — the 100th year of Independence. Target: GDP of $30+ trillion. Requires sustained growth rate of 8-9% per annum. NITI Aayog's Vision @2047 document outlines roadmap.
419What is India's National Infrastructure Pipeline (NIP)?
Answer: NIP = ₹111 lakh crore investment in infrastructure over 2019-2025. PM Gati Shakti — National Master Plan (2021) coordinates multi-modal infrastructure (roads, railways, ports, airports, waterways, logistics). Aims to reduce logistics cost to 8% of GDP.
420What is the Green Revolution's impact on Indian economy?
Answer: Green Revolution transformed India from a food-deficit to a food-surplus nation (1960s-1970s). Wheat production tripled; rice production doubled. Punjab, Haryana, UP benefited most. Side effects: soil degradation, water depletion, regional inequality.

421What is Andhra Pradesh known as in terms of agriculture?
Answer: Andhra Pradesh is known as the "Rice Bowl of India" — it is one of the largest producers of rice in the country. It also leads in tobacco, chilli, and cotton production. Krishna-Godavari delta is highly fertile.
422What is Andhra Pradesh's new capital city?
Answer: Amaravati is designated as the state capital of Andhra Pradesh (after bifurcation from Telangana in 2014). Vizag (Visakhapatnam) serves as the executive capital and Kurnool as the judicial capital under the three-capital proposal.
423What are the major rivers/dams in Andhra Pradesh for irrigation?
Answer: Nagarjuna Sagar Dam (on Krishna river — world's largest masonry dam) and Srisailam Dam (largest hydro power project in AP) are key. Godavari and Krishna are the two major rivers. Polavaram project (national project) on Godavari.
424What is APIIC?
Answer: Andhra Pradesh Industrial Infrastructure Corporation — government agency that develops and manages industrial parks, infrastructure, and promotes industrial investment in Andhra Pradesh. Plays key role in AP's industrialisation.
425What are AP's major exports?
Answer: AP's major exports include: Rice, Tobacco, Chilli, Pharmaceuticals, Granite, Aquaculture products (shrimp/prawn). Vizag port is the main gateway. AP is India's largest aquaculture state.
426What is the WTO principle of National Treatment?
Answer: National Treatment = once goods/services enter a member country's market, they must be treated the same as domestically produced goods — no discrimination. Core WTO non-discrimination principle (along with MFN).
427What is a Trade Deficit? Is it always bad?
Answer: Trade Deficit = Imports > Exports. Not always bad — if imports are capital goods/technology for development, it can boost future production. India's trade deficit reflects import of oil (energy), gold, and electronics.
428What is Balance of Payments (BoP)?
Answer: BoP = systematic record of all economic transactions between a country and the rest of the world in a year. Two accounts: Current Account (goods, services, income, transfers) + Capital Account (financial flows). BoP always balances.
429What is the Current Account of BoP?
Answer: Current Account records trade in goods (visible trade), services (invisible trade), income (remittances, dividends), and transfers. India's Current Account Deficit (CAD) is typically 1-3% of GDP, mainly due to high oil imports.
430What is Dumping in International Trade?
Answer: Dumping = exporting goods at a price BELOW the domestic market price or below cost of production. Harms domestic industries in importing country. WTO allows Anti-Dumping Duty to protect against dumping. China has been accused of dumping steel.
431What are the three dimensions of HDI?
Answer: 1. Long and Healthy Life (Life Expectancy at Birth). 2. Knowledge (Mean Years of Schooling + Expected Years of Schooling). 3. Decent Standard of Living (GNI per Capita in PPP $). HDI = Geometric Mean of these three indices.
432What is the formula for HDI?
Answer: HDI = ∛(Life Expectancy Index × Education Index × GNI Index) — the cube root (geometric mean) of the three dimension indices. Each index is normalised between 0 and 1 using min-max values.
433What is India's approximate HDI score and rank?
Answer: India's HDI score is approximately 0.644, ranked around 134 out of 193 countries (Medium Human Development category). Kerala is India's best-performing state on all HDI dimensions. India's GDP rank is better than HDI rank.
434What is the difference between Money Market and Capital Market?
Answer: Money Market: Short-term (<1 year), regulated by RBI, instruments: T-Bills, CP, CD, Call Money — low risk. Capital Market: Long-term (>1 year), regulated by SEBI, instruments: Equity, Bonds, Debentures, MFs — higher risk.
435What is the full form of IBBI?
Answer: Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India — established under IBC 2016. Regulatory body for insolvency professionals, insolvency professional agencies, and information utilities. Ensures proper functioning of insolvency resolution process.
436What is NCLT?
Answer: National Company Law Tribunal — adjudicating authority for insolvency cases under IBC 2016 for companies and Limited Liability Partnerships. Also handles mergers, acquisitions, and oppression/mismanagement cases under Companies Act.
437What is the time limit for resolution under IBC?
Answer: 180 days initially, extendable to 270 days with majority of CoC (Committee of Creditors), and further to 330 days including litigation time with NCLT approval. After 330 days without resolution → liquidation.
438What is Article 110 of the Constitution?
Answer: Article 110 defines a Money Bill. A bill is a Money Bill if it deals ONLY with: taxation, borrowing, appropriation, consolidated/contingency funds, declaring expenditure charged to CFI, receipt of money into CFI. Speaker's certification is final.
439What is Article 266(1) of the Constitution?
Answer: Article 266(1) establishes the Consolidated Fund of India (CFI). All government revenues, loans, and repayments go into CFI. No money can be withdrawn from CFI without authorisation by Parliament (Appropriation Act).
440What is Article 267 of the Constitution?
Answer: Article 267 establishes the Contingency Fund of India. This fund (₹500 crore) is at the disposal of the President for urgent, unforeseen expenditure. No Parliamentary approval required. Replenished later by Parliament.
441Has Article 360 (Financial Emergency) ever been invoked in India?
Answer: No — Article 360 (Financial Emergency) has NEVER been proclaimed in India since independence. National Emergency (Art 352) has been proclaimed 3 times (1962, 1971, 1975); President's Rule (Art 356) numerous times; but Financial Emergency — never.
442What can the President do under Article 360 (Financial Emergency)?
Answer: Under Article 360, the President can: Direct states to observe financial propriety; reduce salaries of all government servants including judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts; reserve all money bills of state legislatures for Presidential consideration.
443What are the 17 SDGs in order?
Answer: 1-No Poverty, 2-Zero Hunger, 3-Good Health, 4-Quality Education, 5-Gender Equality, 6-Clean Water, 7-Affordable Energy, 8-Decent Work, 9-Industry/Innovation, 10-Reduced Inequality, 11-Sustainable Cities, 12-Responsible Consumption, 13-Climate Action, 14-Life Below Water, 15-Life on Land, 16-Peace/Justice, 17-Partnerships.
444What replaced the MDGs (Millennium Development Goals)?
Answer: SDGs (Sustainable Development Goals) replaced MDGs in 2015. MDGs (2000-2015) had 8 goals focused mainly on developing countries. SDGs (2015-2030) have 17 goals applicable to ALL countries (developed and developing).
445What is the Census 2011 literacy rate?
Answer: India's overall literacy rate as per Census 2011: 74.04%. Male literacy: 82.14%. Female literacy: 65.46%. Highest: Kerala (93.91%). Lowest: Bihar (63.82%). Census 2021 postponed due to COVID-19.
446What is Census 2011 Child Sex Ratio and why is it alarming?
Answer: Child Sex Ratio (0-6 years): 914 girls per 1000 boys — down from 927 in 2001. Alarming because it indicates female foeticide/infanticide and gender discrimination even before birth. Government launched Beti Bachao Beti Padhao to address this.
447What is the most populous and least populous state as per Census 2011?
Answer: Most populous: Uttar Pradesh (19.98 crore = ~200 million). Least populous: Sikkim (about 6 lakh). Among UTs, Lakshadweep is least populous. Among UTs, Delhi is most populous.
448What is PMFBY's premium structure?
Answer: Under PMFBY: Farmer pays Kharif crops: 2%, Rabi crops: 1.5%, Annual/Commercial/Horticulture crops: 5% of sum insured as premium. The difference between actual premium and farmer's share is subsidised equally by Centre and State governments.
449What does e-NAM connect?
Answer: e-NAM connects APMC mandis across India into a unified national online marketplace. Farmers can get price discovery from buyers in distant locations. Implemented by SFAC (Small Farmers' Agribusiness Consortium). Over 1000 mandis connected.
450What is the NITI Aayog composition?
Answer: Chairman: PM. Vice Chairman: Appointed by PM. CEO: Day-to-day operations. Governing Council: All Chief Ministers + LG of UTs. Regional Councils: For specific issues. Full-time Members: Economists/experts appointed by PM.
451What is the difference between NITI Aayog and Planning Commission on fund allocation?
Answer: Planning Commission could allocate funds directly to states — NITI Aayog CANNOT. Fund devolution to states is done by the Finance Commission (Article 280). NITI Aayog is purely a policy think-tank and advisory body.
452What is the T-Bill and its maturities?
Answer: Treasury Bill = short-term government security issued by RBI on behalf of Government of India. Maturities: 91 days (most popular), 182 days, 364 days. Issued at discount, redeemed at face value. Zero coupon. Safest instrument.
453What is the CBDC and who issues it in India?
Answer: Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) = Digital Rupee (e₹) issued by RBI. Pilot began November 2022 (Wholesale) and December 2022 (Retail). It is legal tender — same as physical rupee but in digital form. Not the same as UPI or cryptocurrency.
454What is the difference between e₹-W and e₹-R?
Answer: e₹-W (Wholesale CBDC): For interbank settlements and financial institutions. e₹-R (Retail CBDC): For the general public — like a digital version of physical cash. Works through digital wallets maintained by banks.
455What is the IBC's priority order in liquidation?
Answer: Order: 1. Insolvency resolution costs → 2. Secured creditors + workers' dues (2 years) → 3. Unsecured creditors → 4. Government dues → 5. Remaining secured creditors → 6. Equity shareholders (last — highest risk).
456What is the full form of SFAC?
Answer: Small Farmers' Agribusiness Consortium — implements e-NAM (Electronic National Agriculture Market). It provides agribusiness development services including market linkages, contract farming, and value chain development for small farmers.
457What is the Halwa Ceremony significance?
Answer: Halwa ceremony marks the start of Union Budget printing in the Finance Ministry. Staff involved are "locked in" — no phone/communication allowed until Budget Day to prevent leaks. Halwa (sweet) distributed to all staff as tradition.
458What is Adam Smith's "Invisible Hand"?
Answer: Adam Smith's "Invisible Hand" = the idea that individuals pursuing self-interest in free markets unintentionally promote social good. Markets coordinate economic activity efficiently without government intervention. Price mechanism acts as the "invisible hand."
459What is Keynes' "Animal Spirits"?
Answer: Keynes used "Animal Spirits" to describe the instinctive, emotional, and non-rational forces that drive investment decisions — optimism/pessimism, confidence/fear. When animal spirits are high → investment booms; low → recession.
460What is the significance of MPC + MPS = 1?
Answer: MPC + MPS = 1 always because additional income is either consumed or saved — there's no third option. If MPC = 0.75, then MPS = 0.25. This identity holds at both individual and aggregate level.
461What is the Multiplier effect with an example?
Answer: If MPC = 0.8, Multiplier = 1/(1-0.8) = 5. Government spends ₹100 crore on roads → workers earn ₹100cr → spend ₹80cr → recipients spend ₹64cr → and so on. Total GDP increase = ₹100 × 5 = ₹500 crore.
462What is the Repo Rate's impact chain?
Answer: RBI raises Repo Rate → Commercial banks raise lending rates → Home/car/business loans become costlier → Borrowing falls → Spending falls → Demand falls → Inflation falls. Takes 6-18 months to fully transmit through economy.
463What is the difference between Fiscal Policy and Monetary Policy tools?
Answer: Monetary Policy tools (RBI): Repo Rate, Reverse Repo, CRR, SLR, OMO, Bank Rate. Fiscal Policy tools (Government/Finance Ministry): Government expenditure (G), Taxation (T), Subsidies, Disinvestment, Deficit Financing.
464What is the difference between Debenture and Share?
Answer: Debenture: Debt instrument (loan to company), fixed interest, no ownership, priority in liquidation, lower risk. Share (Equity): Ownership stake, variable dividend, voting rights, last in liquidation, higher risk/return.
465What is GINI coefficient of 0 vs 1?
Answer: Gini = 0 means perfect equality (everyone has exactly the same income). Gini = 1 means perfect inequality (one person has all income, everyone else has nothing). India's Gini ≈ 0.35 (moderate inequality).
466What is the Goods and Services Tax Council?
Answer: GST Council (Article 279A) — a constitutional body comprising Union Finance Minister (Chair) + State Finance Ministers. Recommends GST rates, exemptions, threshold limits. Decisions by 3/4th weighted majority. States have 2/3rd voting weight.
467What does Disinflation mean?
Answer: Disinflation = reduction in the rate of inflation — inflation is still positive but declining. E.g., inflation falls from 10% to 5% to 3%. Different from Deflation (prices actually falling). RBI aims for disinflation toward its 4% target.
468What is the significance of India's 1991 BoP crisis?
Answer: India's foreign exchange reserves fell to just 2-3 weeks of import cover in 1991. India pledged gold to IMF for emergency loan. Led to PM Narasimha Rao + FM Manmohan Singh's historic LPG reforms — opening up the Indian economy.
469What is the Fisher Equation of Exchange?
Answer: MV = PT (Irving Fisher). M = Money supply, V = Velocity of money, P = Price level, T = Volume of transactions. Classical economists assumed V and T are constant → increase in M directly causes proportional increase in P (inflation).
470What is the short-run Phillips Curve trade-off?
Answer: Short-run Phillips Curve: Inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. To reduce unemployment → increase money supply → inflation rises. To reduce inflation → tighten money → unemployment rises. This trade-off holds only in short run.
471What is Lorenz Curve and what does the diagonal represent?
Answer: Lorenz Curve plots cumulative income share (Y-axis) against cumulative population share (X-axis). The 45° diagonal = perfect equality (each % of population earns the same % of income). The further the Lorenz Curve bows away from diagonal, the greater the inequality.
472What is the full form of NABARD and when was it established?
Answer: National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development — established 12 July 1982 based on B. Sivaraman Committee recommendations. It refinances banks for rural/agricultural credit, promotes rural development, supervises RRBs and cooperative banks.
473What is the difference between a Scheduled Bank and a Non-Scheduled Bank?
Answer: Scheduled Banks = included in the Second Schedule of RBI Act (meet certain criteria — paid-up capital/reserves ≥ ₹5 lakh). Eligible for RBI refinancing. Non-Scheduled Banks = local area banks not meeting criteria. Very rare today.
474What is the Crowding-In Effect?
Answer: Crowding-In Effect = when government spending stimulates private investment (opposite of Crowding Out). Happens when government builds infrastructure → private firms invest in related industries. Common in developing economies with infrastructure deficits.
475What is the significance of the Narasimham Committee?
Answer: M. Narasimham Committees (1991 and 1998) recommended banking sector reforms: Reduce CRR/SLR, deregulate interest rates, allow private/foreign banks, set up ARCs (Asset Reconstruction Companies) for NPAs, strengthen bank supervision through CAMELS framework.
476What is the difference between Bilateral and Multilateral Trade?
Answer: Bilateral Trade = trade agreement between two countries (FTA — Free Trade Agreement). Multilateral Trade = trade agreement among many countries under WTO framework. India has bilateral FTAs with ASEAN, UAE, Australia, Japan, South Korea.
477What is India's export basket composition?
Answer: India's top exports: Petroleum products (refined), Software/IT services, Gems and Jewellery, Pharmaceuticals (generic drugs), Engineering goods, Textiles. India is world's largest exporter of generic medicines — "Pharmacy of the World."
478What is India's import basket composition?
Answer: India's top imports: Crude Oil and Petroleum products (largest), Gold, Electronics (mobiles, computers), Machinery, Coal, Edible oils, Fertilisers, Chemicals. India imports ~85% of its crude oil requirements.
479What is the significance of India's IT/Software sector?
Answer: India's IT sector contributes approximately 8-9% of GDP. India is the world's largest exporter of IT services. Cities: Bengaluru (Silicon Valley of India), Hyderabad, Pune, Chennai, Delhi-NCR. Major companies: TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL.
480What is a Free Trade Agreement (FTA)?
Answer: FTA = agreement between two or more countries to reduce/eliminate tariffs and trade barriers between them. India has FTAs with ASEAN, South Korea, Japan, Singapore, UAE, Australia. India-UK FTA under negotiation. Boosts bilateral trade.
481What is the Public Distribution System (PDS) and how does it work?
Answer: PDS = network of Fair Price Shops (FPS) distributing subsidised food and essential commodities to BPL families. Central government procures food grains (via FCI) → stores at state level → distributes through state FPS. NFSA governs it.
482What is the FCI (Food Corporation of India)?
Answer: Food Corporation of India — est. 1965. Procures food grains from farmers at MSP, stores them in warehouses, and distributes to states for PDS. Manages India's food security buffer stock. HQ: New Delhi.
483What is the difference between Repo Rate and Reverse Repo Rate?
Answer: Repo Rate: RBI lends to banks at this rate (banks borrow from RBI). Higher → costlier loans → controls inflation. Reverse Repo Rate: Banks lend to RBI at this rate (banks park excess funds with RBI). Always lower than Repo. Higher → banks prefer parking over lending → money supply contracts.
484What is the Goods and Services Tax (GST) threshold limit?
Answer: GST threshold: ₹40 lakh annual turnover for goods suppliers (₹20 lakh for most service providers; ₹10 lakh for special category states). Below threshold, businesses need not register for GST. Composition scheme: up to ₹1.5 crore.
485What is the difference between National Income and GDP?
Answer: National Income (NI) = NNP at Factor Cost = NNP at Market Price − Net Indirect Taxes. It is a narrower concept than GDP — accounts for depreciation, factor income flows, and indirect taxes. GDP is at market prices including taxes.
486What is the India-Pakistan comparison on HDI?
Answer: India (HDI ~0.644, rank ~134) ranks ahead of Pakistan (HDI ~0.54, rank ~161) but both are in Medium/Low Human Development category. Both rank poorly compared to developed nations. India performs better on education and income dimensions.
487What is Minimum Wage vs Living Wage?
Answer: Minimum Wage = legally mandated minimum rate of pay (set by government). Living Wage = wage sufficient to cover basic needs (food, housing, healthcare, education) — higher concept than minimum wage. India has a national floor-level minimum wage.
488What is the Code on Wages 2019?
Answer: Code on Wages 2019 consolidated 4 labour laws (Minimum Wages Act, Payment of Wages Act, Equal Remuneration Act, Payment of Bonus Act) into one code. Part of 4 Labour Codes reform (wages, industrial relations, social security, occupational safety).
489What is the difference between Money Supply (M) and Velocity (V) in inflation?
Answer: In MV=PT: Increase in M (money supply) → more money chasing same goods → demand-pull inflation. Increase in V (velocity) → same money circulates faster → similar inflationary effect without printing more money. Both M and V increases cause inflation.
490What is the difference between ADB and IMF?
Answer: ADB (Asian Development Bank, est. 1966, Manila): provides long-term development loans for Asia-Pacific projects (infrastructure, education). IMF (est. 1944, Washington DC): provides short-term balance of payments support and monetary stability worldwide.
491What is a Unicorn company?
Answer: Unicorn = a privately held startup company valued at $1 billion or more. India has 100+ unicorns — 3rd largest in world. Examples: Flipkart, Ola, Byju's, Zomato, Paytm, PhonePe, CRED. India's startup ecosystem is growing rapidly.
492What is the Green Economy?
Answer: Green Economy = economy that results in improved human well-being and social equity while significantly reducing environmental risks and ecological scarcities. Key concepts: carbon credits, green bonds, ESG investing, renewable energy. India's target: Net Zero by 2070.
493What is India's rank as a Remittance recipient?
Answer: India is the world's largest recipient of remittances — approximately $125 billion annually. Major sources: USA, UAE, UK, Saudi Arabia, Qatar. Indian diaspora (NRIs/PIOs) is one of the world's largest. Remittances exceed India's FDI inflows.
494What is the National Pension System (NPS)?
Answer: NPS = defined contribution pension scheme launched in 2004 for central government employees, extended to all citizens in 2009. Regulated by PFRDA (Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority). Replaces old defined benefit pension for new employees.
495What is the difference between Kharif and Rabi in terms of water requirements?
Answer: Kharif crops (Rice, Cotton) require heavy rainfall/irrigation — sown in monsoon (June-July). Rabi crops (Wheat, Barley, Mustard) require cool weather + moderate water — sown in winter (Oct-Nov). Zaid crops need irrigation in summer.
496What is the India Stack?
Answer: India Stack = India's unified digital infrastructure: Aadhaar (identity), UPI (payments), DigiLocker (documents), eSign (digital signatures), Unified Health Interface. It enables paperless, cashless, and presence-less service delivery. Model being adopted globally.
497What is the Amrit Kaal period?
Answer: Amrit Kaal = India's journey from 2022 to 2047 (25 years to India@100 — centenary of independence). Vision: Developed India by 2047 (Viksit Bharat). Focus on: inclusive growth, strong infrastructure, green development, human capital, innovation.
498What is the difference between Revenue Deficit and Fiscal Deficit?
Answer: Revenue Deficit = Revenue Exp − Revenue Receipts (day-to-day operations shortfall). Fiscal Deficit = Total Exp − Total Receipts (excl. borrowings) (total borrowing requirement). Fiscal Deficit > Revenue Deficit always. Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit − Interest payments.
499What is India's approximate FDI inflow annually?
Answer: India receives approximately $70-85 billion in FDI annually (in recent years). Top sources: Mauritius, Singapore, USA, Netherlands, Japan. Top sectors: Services, Computer software/hardware, Telecom. India is among world's top 5 FDI destinations.
500What is the key difference between a Money Bill and an Ordinary Bill in terms of Rajya Sabha's role?
Answer: Money Bill (Art 110): Rajya Sabha can only RECOMMEND changes (not amend or reject) within 14 days. Lok Sabha may or may not accept recommendations. President cannot withhold assent. Ordinary Bill: Both Houses have equal power — Rajya Sabha can amend or reject. President can return once. This makes Money Bill process much faster.
