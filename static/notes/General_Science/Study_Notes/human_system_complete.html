Introduction: Life Processes and Body Systems జీవన ప్రక్రియలు మరియు శరీర వ్యవస్థలు
EN All living organisms perform certain basic functions that are essential for maintaining life. These functions are collectively called life processes. According to NCERT Class 10 Science, the essential life processes include nutrition, respiration, transportation, excretion, control and coordination, and reproduction. In a single-celled organism like Amoeba, all these functions are carried out by the single cell itself. However, the human body is a complex multicellular organism made up of trillions of cells. No single cell can perform all the required functions for the entire body. Therefore, in the human body, groups of cells that are similar in structure and function come together to form tissues, tissues form organs, and organs working together form organ systems. Each organ system is specialised to carry out one or more specific life processes efficiently. The study of these systems helps us understand how the human body maintains a stable internal environment — a process called homeostasis.

Nutrition and Digestion — The Human Digestive System పోషణ మరియు జీర్ణం — మానవ జీర్ణ వ్యవస్థ
EN What is Nutrition? Nutrition is the process by which an organism obtains food and uses it for growth, energy, and maintenance of body functions. Humans are heterotrophs — they cannot make their own food and must obtain it from external sources. Food provides the body with carbohydrates (energy), proteins (growth and repair), fats (energy storage and insulation), vitamins and minerals (regulation), and water (medium for chemical reactions). The process by which complex food molecules are broken down into simpler, absorbable forms is called digestion. The organ system responsible for digestion is the digestive system, which consists of the alimentary canal and accessory digestive organs.
TE పోషణ అంటే ఏమిటి? పోషణ అనేది ఒక జీవి ఆహారాన్ని పొంది దాన్ని పెరుగుదల, శక్తి మరియు శరీర విధులకు ఉపయోగించే ప్రక్రియ. మానవులు హెటెరోట్రోఫ్లు — వారు స్వంతంగా ఆహారం తయారు చేసుకోలేరు, బయటి వనరుల నుండి పొందాలి. ఆహారం శరీరానికి కార్బోహైడ్రేట్లు (శక్తి), ప్రొటీన్లు (పెరుగుదల మరియు మరమ్మతు), కొవ్వులు (శక్తి నిల్వ), విటమిన్లు మరియు ఖనిజాలు (నియంత్రణ) మరియు నీరు అందిస్తుంది. సంక్లిష్టమైన ఆహార అణువులను సరళమైన, శోషించదగిన రూపాలుగా విచ్ఛిన్నం చేసే ప్రక్రియను జీర్ణం అంటారు. జీర్ణ వ్యవస్థ ఆహారనాళం మరియు అనుబంధ జీర్ణ అవయవాలను కలిగి ఉంటుంది.
The Alimentary Canal / ఆహారనాళం
EN The alimentary canal is a continuous muscular tube that runs from the mouth to the anus, approximately 9 metres long in an adult. Food passes through it in the following order: Mouth (Oral cavity) → Oesophagus (food pipe) → Stomach → Small Intestine → Large Intestine → Rectum → Anus. The accessory digestive organs — Salivary Glands, Liver, Gallbladder, and Pancreas — are not part of the alimentary canal itself, but they produce secretions that are delivered into the canal through ducts and are essential for digestion.

Important Facts — Accessory Organs
The Liver is the largest gland in the human body (weighing about 1.5 kg). It produces bile, which is stored in the gallbladder and released into the duodenum. Bile does not contain any digestive enzymes — it only emulsifies fats by breaking them into smaller droplets so that lipase can act on them effectively. The Pancreas is both an exocrine gland (produces digestive enzymes) and an endocrine gland (produces insulin and glucagon).

Respiration — Human Respiratory System శ్వాసక్రియ — మానవ శ్వాస వ్యవస్థ
EN What is Respiration? Respiration is often confused with breathing, but they are two distinct processes. Breathing (pulmonary ventilation) refers to the physical act of inhaling oxygen and exhaling carbon dioxide — it is simply the gaseous exchange between the lungs and the atmosphere. Cellular respiration, on the other hand, is the biochemical process that occurs inside every cell, in which food (glucose) is broken down to release energy in the form of ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate). ATP is called the energy currency of the cell because it stores and supplies energy for all biological processes. All living cells carry out cellular respiration continuously, whether or not the organism is physically active.
TE శ్వాసక్రియ అంటే ఏమిటి? శ్వాసక్రియను తరచుగా శ్వాస పీల్చడంతో గందరగోళపరుస్తారు, కానీ ఇవి రెండు వేర్వేరు ప్రక్రియలు. శ్వాస పీల్చడం అంటే ఊపిరితిత్తులు మరియు వాయుమండలం మధ్య గ్యాస్ మార్పిడి — ఆక్సిజన్ పీల్చడం మరియు కార్బన్ డయాక్సైడ్ వదలడం. కణజాల శ్వాసక్రియ అనేది ప్రతి కణంలో జరిగే జీవరసాయన ప్రక్రియ, దీనిలో గ్లూకోజ్ విచ్ఛిన్నమై ATP రూపంలో శక్తి విడుదలవుతుంది. ATP ను కణం యొక్క శక్తి కరెన్సీ అంటారు. అన్ని జీవ కణాలు నిరంతరం కణజాల శ్వాసక్రియ చేస్తాయి.

EN Pathway of Respiration at the Cellular Level (NCERT Class 10): The first step of cellular respiration is glycolysis, which occurs in the cytoplasm. One glucose molecule (6 carbon) is broken down into two molecules of pyruvate (3 carbon each), producing 2 ATP. In aerobic respiration, pyruvate enters the mitochondria and is completely oxidised to CO₂ and water through the Krebs cycle and oxidative phosphorylation, yielding 38 ATP total. In anaerobic respiration in muscles, pyruvate is converted to lactic acid — this is why muscles ache after intense exercise (lactic acid accumulation). In yeast, pyruvate is converted to ethanol and CO₂ — this is the basis of fermentation used in bread and alcohol production.

Pathway of Air / వాయుమార్గం
EN When we breathe in, air follows this pathway: Nostrils (nose) — Nasal cavity (warms, humidifies, and filters air) — Pharynx (throat) — Larynx (voice box; contains vocal cords) — Trachea (windpipe; supported by C-shaped cartilage rings that prevent collapse) — Bronchi (two, one entering each lung) — Bronchioles (smaller branches inside the lung) — Alveoli (tiny air sacs, the functional units of the lung, site of gas exchange). An adult human has approximately 300 to 700 million alveoli, providing an enormous surface area of about 70 square metres for gas exchange.
TE శ్వాస పీల్చేటప్పుడు వాయువు ఈ మార్గంలో ప్రయాణిస్తుంది: నాసారంధ్రాలు — నాసికా కుహరం (వాయువును వేడి చేసి, తేమగా చేసి, వడపోస్తుంది) — గొంతు — స్వరపేటిక (స్వర తంతులు ఇక్కడ ఉంటాయి) — శ్వాసనాళం (C-ఆకార మృదులాస్థి వలయాలతో రక్షితం) — శ్వాసన శాఖలు (రెండు, ఒక్కొక్కటి ఒక ఊపిరితిత్తిలో ప్రవేశిస్తుంది) — బ్రాంకియోల్స్ — అల్వియోలి (వాయు మార్పిడి జరిగే చిన్న వాయు సంచులు). పెద్దవారికి దాదాపు 30–70 కోట్ల అల్వియోలి ఉంటాయి, వాయు మార్పిడికి దాదాపు 70 చ.మీ. వైశాల్యం అందిస్తాయి.
EN Mechanism of Breathing — Inhalation: During inhalation, the diaphragm (dome-shaped muscle at the base of the chest) contracts and flattens downward. Simultaneously, the external intercostal muscles contract, pulling the ribs upward and outward. Both actions increase the volume of the thoracic cavity, which decreases the air pressure inside the lungs below atmospheric pressure. Air therefore flows into the lungs from the atmosphere (high pressure to low pressure).
TE శ్వాస తీసుకునే విధానం — ఇన్‌హలేషన్: శ్వాస తీసుకునేటప్పుడు డయాఫ్రమ్ సంకోచించి చదునుగా అవుతుంది. ఒకే సమయంలో పక్క కండరాలు పక్కటెముకలను పైకి మరియు బయటికి లాగుతాయి. ఈ రెండు చర్యలు ఉరస్సు కుహరపు పరిమాణాన్ని పెంచుతాయి, ఫలితంగా లోపల వాయు పీడనం తగ్గుతుంది. వాతావరణ గాలి అధిక పీడనం నుండి తక్కువ పీడనం వైపు ఊపిరితిత్తులలోకి ప్రవేశిస్తుంది.
EN Mechanism of Breathing — Exhalation: During exhalation, the diaphragm relaxes and returns to its dome shape, the ribs move downward and inward, and the volume of the thoracic cavity decreases. This increases the pressure inside the lungs above atmospheric pressure, pushing air out. Normal exhalation is a passive process requiring no muscular effort. Forced exhalation involves internal intercostal muscles and abdominal muscles.

EN Gas Exchange at Alveoli and Role of Haemoglobin: The alveoli have walls that are only one cell thick, surrounded by a dense network of blood capillaries. Oxygen from the inhaled air dissolves in the thin film of moisture on the alveolar surface and diffuses through the alveolar and capillary walls into the blood. Here, oxygen binds with haemoglobin (a red iron-containing protein in RBCs) to form oxyhaemoglobin and is transported to all body cells. At the tissues, oxygen is released and haemoglobin picks up CO₂ produced by cellular respiration. CO₂ is transported back to the lungs in three forms: dissolved in plasma (7%), as bicarbonate ions in plasma (70%), and as carbaminohaemoglobin in RBCs (23%). At the alveoli, CO₂ diffuses out of the blood into the alveolar air and is expelled during exhalation.

ATP — Energy Currency / ATP — శక్తి కరెన్సీ
Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP) is produced during cellular respiration and stores energy in its high-energy phosphate bonds. When a cell needs energy for any work — muscle contraction, active transport, protein synthesis — ATP is hydrolysed to ADP (Adenosine Diphosphate) + Pi (inorganic phosphate), releasing energy. Aerobic respiration produces 38 ATP per glucose; anaerobic produces only 2 ATP per glucose. This is why aerobic exercise (running, swimming) is far more energetically efficient than anaerobic bursts (sprinting).
ATP (అడెనోసిన్ ట్రైఫాస్ఫేట్) కణజాల శ్వాసక్రియలో ఉత్పత్తి అవుతుంది మరియు శక్తిని నిల్వ చేస్తుంది. వాయు శ్వాసక్రియ గ్లూకోజ్ కు 38 ATP ఉత్పత్తి చేస్తుంది; అవాయు శ్వాసక్రియ కేవలం 2 ATP మాత్రమే ఉత్పత్తి చేస్తుంది.
Transportation — The Circulatory System రవాణా — రక్త ప్రసరణ వ్యవస్థ
EN The circulatory system is responsible for transporting oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste products throughout the body. It consists of the heart (the pump), blood vessels (arteries, veins, and capillaries — the pipes), and blood (the transport medium). The human heart is roughly the size of a closed fist and is located in the thoracic cavity (chest), slightly towards the left side, between the two lungs, in a region called the mediastinum. It is enclosed in a double-layered protective membrane called the pericardium, and the fluid between the layers (pericardial fluid) reduces friction during heartbeats.
TE రక్త ప్రసరణ వ్యవస్థ శరీరమంతా ఆక్సిజన్, పోషకాలు, హార్మోన్లు మరియు వ్యర్థ పదార్థాలను రవాణా చేస్తుంది. ఇది గుండె (పంపు), రక్తనాళాలు (ధమనులు, సిరలు మరియు కేశనాళాలు) మరియు రక్తం (రవాణా మాధ్యమం) తో కూడి ఉంటుంది. మానవ గుండె మూసిన పిడికిలి పరిమాణంలో ఉంటుంది మరియు ఉరస్సు కుహరంలో, రెండు ఊపిరితిత్తుల మధ్య, కొంచెం ఎడమ వైపుగా ఉంటుంది. పెరికార్డియమ్ అనే రక్షణ పొర గుండెను కప్పి ఉంచుతుంది.
Heart Structure — Four Chambers / గుండె నిర్మాణం — నాలుగు గదులు
EN The human heart has four chambers: the Right Atrium (upper right, receives deoxygenated blood from the body via the vena cava), Right Ventricle (lower right, pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery), Left Atrium (upper left, receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via pulmonary veins), and Left Ventricle (lower left, pumps oxygenated blood to the entire body via the aorta). The wall of the left ventricle is the thickest of all four chambers because it must generate sufficient pressure to push blood throughout the entire body against resistance. Valves between chambers (tricuspid on the right, bicuspid/mitral on the left, and semilunar valves at the aorta and pulmonary artery) prevent backflow of blood and ensure unidirectional flow.

Double Circulation / ద్వంద్వ రక్త ప్రసరణ
EN In humans, blood passes through the heart twice in one complete circuit — this is called double circulation and it is a key feature of all mammals and birds. It consists of two loops. The Pulmonary Circulation is the loop from the heart to the lungs and back: deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle travels through the pulmonary artery to the lungs, where CO₂ is released and O₂ is absorbed, and oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium via the pulmonary veins. The Systemic Circulation is the loop from the heart to the rest of the body: oxygenated blood from the left ventricle is pumped through the aorta to all organs and tissues, where oxygen and nutrients are delivered and CO₂ and waste are collected, and deoxygenated blood returns to the right atrium through the superior and inferior vena cava. Double circulation keeps oxygenated and deoxygenated blood completely separate, making the system more efficient.

Important Exception — Pulmonary Vessels
Students often assume that all arteries carry oxygenated blood and all veins carry deoxygenated blood. This is incorrect. The rule is: arteries carry blood AWAY from the heart, and veins carry blood TOWARDS the heart — regardless of oxygen content. The Pulmonary Artery carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs (it is an artery because it goes away from the heart, but carries deoxygenated blood). The Pulmonary Vein carries oxygenated blood from the lungs to the left atrium (it is a vein because it goes towards the heart, but carries oxygenated blood). This is a very frequent exam trap.

ABO Blood Groups / ABO రక్తపు గ్రూపులు
EN The ABO blood group system was discovered by Karl Landsteiner in 1901, for which he received the Nobel Prize in 1930. Blood groups are determined by the presence or absence of antigens (glycoproteins) on the surface of RBCs and antibodies in the plasma. Antigen A and Antigen B are the two antigens. Group A has Antigen A on RBCs and Anti-B antibody in plasma. Group B has Antigen B on RBCs and Anti-A antibody in plasma. Group AB has both Antigen A and B on RBCs and no antibodies in plasma — this makes AB the Universal Recipient because it will not reject any donated blood. Group O has no antigens on RBCs but has both Anti-A and Anti-B antibodies in plasma — this makes O the Universal Donor because its blood will not trigger rejection in any recipient. During blood transfusion, the donor's RBC antigens must not clash with the recipient's plasma antibodies, as this would cause agglutination (clumping) and haemolysis, which can be fatal.

Blood Clotting / రక్తం గడ్డకట్టడం
EN Blood clotting (coagulation) is a protective mechanism that stops bleeding from an injured blood vessel. The process requires Platelets, thirteen clotting factors (numbered I through XIII, with Factor VI excluded from the list, leaving thirteen functional factors), Calcium ions (Ca²⁺), and Vitamin K. When a blood vessel is injured, platelets aggregate at the wound site and release chemicals that trigger the coagulation cascade. The key steps are: (1) Prothrombin (Factor II, an inactive precursor) is converted to active Thrombin (with the help of thromboplastin and calcium). (2) Thrombin then converts soluble Fibrinogen (Factor I, dissolved in plasma) into insoluble Fibrin threads. (3) Fibrin threads form a mesh that traps RBCs and platelets, creating a clot (thrombus) that seals the wound. Heparin, produced by mast cells and the liver, is a natural anticoagulant that prevents unwanted clotting inside blood vessels. Vitamin K deficiency causes clotting failure. Haemophilia A is caused by deficiency of Factor VIII and is an X-linked recessive genetic disorder, predominantly affecting males.

Rh Factor and Blood Compatibility / Rh కారకం మరియు రక్త అనుకూలత
EN The Rh factor is an antigen found on the surface of red blood cells. People who have this antigen are called Rh-positive (Rh+), and those who lack it are called Rh-negative (Rh−). The Rh factor is detected through an immunological reaction involving antigen-antibody agglutination. Blood group inheritance is controlled by genes located on chromosome 9, where the alleles I¹ and I² are codominant and i is recessive. Rh incompatibility: When an Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive baby, the mother's immune system may produce antibodies against the Rh antigen after the first delivery. In a subsequent pregnancy with an Rh-positive baby, these maternal antibodies cross the placenta and destroy the foetal RBCs, causing a condition called Erythroblastosis Fetalis (Haemolytic Disease of the Newborn). Prevention: Anti-D immunoglobulin (Rh immunoglobulin) is given to the Rh-negative mother within 72 hours of delivery to prevent sensitisation.

CO Poisoning and Haemoglobin Compounds / CO విషప్రభావం మరియు హిమోగ్లోబిన్ సమ్మేళనాలు
EN When Carbon monoxide (CO) combines with Haemoglobin, it forms Carboxyhemoglobin (HbCO). This reaction is irreversible because CO has approximately 200 times greater affinity for Haemoglobin than oxygen does. This prevents oxygen from binding to Haemoglobin, leading to CO poisoning, tissue hypoxia, and can cause death. CO is a colourless, odourless gas produced by incomplete combustion of fuels. When Carbon dioxide (CO&sub2;) combines with Haemoglobin, it forms Carbaminohemoglobin. This reaction is completely reversible and is a normal part of CO&sub2; transport in the blood from tissues to the lungs.

Warfarin — Anticoagulant Drug / వార్ఫారిన్ — రక్తగడ్డకట్టు నిరోధక మందు
EN Warfarin is a Vitamin K antagonist drug used as an anticoagulant (blood thinner) in clinical medicine. It works by blocking Vitamin K, which is essential for the synthesis of clotting factors (Prothrombin/Factor II, VII, IX, and X). By blocking Vitamin K, Warfarin prevents the liver from making these clotting factors, thus preventing dangerous blood clots in conditions like deep vein thrombosis and atrial fibrillation.

Urochrome and the Colour of Urine / యూరోక్రోమ్ మరియు మూత్రం రంగు
EN The yellow colour of urine is caused by a pigment called Urochrome, also known as Urobilin. This pigment is produced through the breakdown of Haemoglobin from old RBCs. The breakdown pathway is: Haemoglobin → Bilirubin (in spleen/liver) → Urobilinogen (in intestine) → Urochrome (excreted in urine). This is why the destruction of old RBCs by the Spleen has a direct connection to the colour of urine.

SA Node and the Cardiac Cycle / SA నోడ్ మరియు హృదయ చక్రం
EN The Sinoatrial (SA) node, located in the wall of the right atrium, is the natural pacemaker of the heart. It spontaneously generates electrical impulses at a rate of 70 to 72 beats per minute, initiating each heartbeat. The impulse travels from the SA node to the Atrioventricular (AV) node, then along the Bundle of His and the Purkinje fibres to cause ventricular contraction. One complete cardiac cycle (one heartbeat) takes 0.8 seconds at rest. This cycle consists of atrial systole (0.1 seconds), ventricular systole (0.3 seconds), and diastole — the relaxation phase (0.4 seconds). At a rate of 75 beats per minute, the heart beats approximately 100,000 times per day.

Excretion — The Excretory System విసర్జన — విసర్జన వ్యవస్థ
EN What is Excretion? Excretion is the process of removing harmful metabolic waste products from the body. These wastes are produced as by-products of cellular metabolism. The major nitrogenous waste in humans is urea, which is produced in the liver by the breakdown of amino acids (deamination). Other wastes include CO₂ (removed via lungs), excess water and mineral salts (removed via kidneys and skin), and bilirubin (bile pigment from haemoglobin breakdown, excreted via faeces). The primary organs of excretion are the kidneys, which continuously filter blood and produce urine. The kidneys also regulate water balance, blood pressure (by controlling blood volume), and blood pH.

Nephron — Structural and Functional Unit / నెఫ్రాన్
EN The nephron is the microscopic structural and functional unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains about one million (10 lakh) nephrons. Each nephron consists of a Malpighian body (comprising the Bowman's capsule and the glomerulus), and a renal tubule (comprising the proximal convoluted tubule, the loop of Henle, and the distal convoluted tubule, which drains into the collecting duct). The glomerulus is a tight knot of blood capillaries enclosed within the cup-shaped Bowman's capsule. Blood enters the glomerulus through the afferent arteriole at high pressure and leaves through the efferent arteriole (narrower, maintaining pressure). This pressure difference drives the filtration of blood.

GFR and Urine Volume Facts
The Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) in a healthy adult is approximately 125 mL per minute. This means approximately 180 litres of blood plasma is filtered by the kidneys every day. However, through tubular reabsorption, about 178.5 litres are reabsorbed back into the blood, and only 1.5 to 2 litres are excreted as urine per day. If the GFR falls below normal (as in kidney disease), waste products accumulate in the blood — a condition called uraemia.

Control and Coordination — The Nervous System నియంత్రణ మరియు సమన్వయం — నాడీ వ్యవస్థ
EN The nervous system is the body's communication and control network. It enables the body to detect changes in the internal and external environment (stimuli), process information, and produce appropriate responses. In complex organisms like humans, this needs to happen rapidly — the nervous system achieves this by using electrical signals (nerve impulses) that travel at speeds up to 100 metres per second. The nervous system works in coordination with the endocrine system to maintain homeostasis. While nervous control is rapid but short-lived, hormonal control (via the endocrine system) is slower but more prolonged.

Neuron — The Structural Unit of Nervous System / న్యూరాన్ — నాడీ వ్యవస్థ యొక్క నిర్మాణ యూనిట్
EN The neuron (nerve cell) is the structural and functional unit of the nervous system. It is the longest cell in the human body (some can be over 1 metre long). A typical neuron has three parts: (1) Cell body (Cyton / Soma) — contains the nucleus and carries out most of the cell's metabolic work; (2) Dendrites — short branched extensions from the cell body that receive nerve signals from other neurons or sense organs and carry them towards the cell body; (3) Axon — a single long projection that carries nerve impulses away from the cell body to other neurons, muscles, or glands. The axon is often covered by a white fatty insulating layer called the myelin sheath (produced by Schwann cells), which speeds up impulse conduction. The gaps in the myelin sheath are called Nodes of Ranvier. The axon terminates in branched axon terminals (synaptic knobs) that communicate with the next neuron. Neurons are classified as Sensory neurons (carry impulses from sense organs to CNS), Motor neurons (carry impulses from CNS to muscles and glands), and Relay/Interneurons (connect sensory and motor neurons, found in the CNS).

EN Synapse: A synapse is the microscopic junction between the axon terminal of one neuron and the dendrite or cell body of the next neuron. The two neurons do not actually touch — there is a tiny gap called the synaptic cleft. When a nerve impulse reaches the axon terminal, it triggers the release of chemical molecules called neurotransmitters (such as acetylcholine or dopamine) from synaptic vesicles. These chemicals cross the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the next neuron, generating a new nerve impulse in it. This chemical transmission allows signals to travel from one neuron to the next and also allows the nervous system to filter and modify signals.

Reflex Arc / రిఫ్లెక్స్ ఆర్క్
EN A reflex is an automatic, rapid, involuntary response to a stimulus that does not require conscious thought. Reflexes protect the body from harm — for example, pulling the hand away from a hot object before the pain is consciously perceived. The pathway of a reflex response is called a reflex arc. The components and their sequence are: Receptor (sense organ detects stimulus) — Sensory (afferent) neuron (carries impulse to spinal cord) — Relay neuron in spinal cord (processes and relays signal; this is where integration occurs, NOT in the brain) — Motor (efferent) neuron (carries response impulse to the effector) — Effector (muscle contracts or gland secretes). The crucial point is that in a reflex arc, the signal does not travel to the brain for processing — it is handled entirely at the level of the spinal cord. This saves critical time in emergency situations. The brain does receive information about the reflex happening, but only after the response has already occurred.

Biological Death and Neuron Regeneration
Brain cells (neurons) begin to die approximately 4 to 6 minutes after cardiac arrest (when the heart stops pumping oxygenated blood to the brain). This is why CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation) must be started immediately when cardiac arrest occurs. Unlike most other cells in the body, mature neurons in the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) cannot regenerate or divide — once they are lost, they are permanently gone. This is why spinal cord injuries often result in permanent paralysis. However, the peripheral nervous system has some limited capacity for regeneration.

Alzheimer's Disease / అల్జీమర్స్ వ్యాధి
EN Alzheimer's disease is caused by the progressive degeneration of brain cells (neurons). As neurons die, the affected person experiences memory loss, confusion, and eventually severe dementia. The disease is associated with the formation of amyloid plaques (deposits of a protein called beta-amyloid) and tau tangles (twisted protein fibres inside neurons) in the brain. Biological death refers to the irreversible death of brain tissue. It occurs 4 to 6 minutes after clinical death (when the heart stops) because brain cells cannot survive without oxygen and cannot regenerate once dead. This is why immediate CPR (cardiopulmonary resuscitation) is critical to prevent biological death.

Chemical Coordination — The Endocrine System రసాయన సమన్వయం — అంతర్గ్రంథి వ్యవస్థ
EN The endocrine system is the body's chemical communication network. It consists of endocrine (ductless) glands that secrete chemical messengers called hormones directly into the bloodstream (without using ducts). Hormones travel through the blood to specific target organs or target cells that have matching receptors. When the hormone binds to its receptor, it causes a specific physiological effect. Hormones can be proteins or peptides (like insulin), steroids (like cortisol), or amino acid derivatives (like thyroxine and adrenaline). Hormonal control is slower to act than nervous control (because hormones must travel through the blood), but their effects last much longer and can influence entire body systems simultaneously.

EN Feedback Mechanism in Hormone Regulation: Hormone levels in the blood are regulated by a negative feedback mechanism. This works as a self-correcting system. For example, when blood thyroid hormone (T4) levels rise above normal, this signal is detected by the hypothalamus and pituitary gland. The hypothalamus reduces its secretion of TRH (Thyrotropin-Releasing Hormone), and the pituitary reduces its secretion of TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone). With less TSH, the thyroid gland produces less T4, and blood T4 levels fall back to normal. Conversely, when T4 levels are too low, the hypothalamus and pituitary increase TRH and TSH production to stimulate more T4 release. This negative feedback loop keeps hormone levels within a narrow, healthy range. Similarly, insulin and glucagon regulate each other and blood glucose through opposing feedback loops.

Sense Organs — The Eye జ్ఞానేంద్రియాలు — కన్ను
EN The eye is the organ of vision and one of the most complex organs in the human body. It is housed in the bony eye socket (orbit) of the skull and is protected by the eyelids, eyelashes, and lacrimal (tear) glands. The eye works like a camera: it uses a lens system to focus incoming light on a photosensitive surface (retina), which converts light signals into nerve signals that are sent to the brain via the optic nerve. The brain's visual cortex (located in the occipital lobe of the cerebrum) interprets these signals as vision.

Accommodation — How the Eye Focuses / వసతి
EN Accommodation is the ability of the eye to change the focal length of its lens to focus on objects at different distances. When focusing on a near object: the ciliary muscles contract, the suspensory ligaments loosen, and the lens becomes more convex (thicker), increasing its refracting power to bend light more strongly. When focusing on a distant object: the ciliary muscles relax, the suspensory ligaments tighten and flatten the lens, decreasing its refracting power. The eye can maintain clear vision for objects between about 25 cm (near point) and infinity (far point). With age, the lens loses its elasticity and the ability to accommodate decreases — this is called Presbyopia.

Eye Camera Analogy / కన్ను కెమెరా పోలిక
The human eye and a camera share a striking structural analogy. The Retina functions like the film or digital sensor (records the image). The Lens functions like the camera lens (focuses the image). The Iris and Pupil together function like the camera aperture (controls the amount of light admitted). The Cornea provides most of the fixed focusing power, like the front element of a camera lens. The Sclera (white of the eye) is the protective outer casing, like the camera body. However, unlike a camera, the eye continuously adapts in real time.

The Skeletal and Muscular System అస్థి వ్యవస్థ మరియు కండర వ్యవస్థ
EN The skeletal system is the internal framework of the body. An adult human has 206 bones. A newborn baby has about 270 to 300 bones, many of which fuse together as the child grows. The skeletal system performs five major functions: (1) Support — provides structural framework for the body; (2) Protection — protects vital organs (skull protects brain, ribcage protects heart and lungs, vertebral column protects spinal cord); (3) Movement — acts as levers for muscles to pull against; (4) Haematopoiesis — red bone marrow in certain bones (sternum, vertebrae, ribs, pelvis) produces blood cells (RBCs, WBCs, and platelets); (5) Mineral storage — stores calcium and phosphorus, releasing them when needed for metabolic functions.

They are strong but slightly elastic. Tendons connect muscles to bones and transmit the force of muscle contraction to produce movement. They are very strong, inelastic fibrous cords. Cartilage is a smooth, firm connective tissue that covers the ends of bones at joints (articular cartilage), reducing friction and absorbing shock. It also forms structures like the ear pinna, nose tip, intervertebral discs, and knee menisci. Unlike bone, cartilage has no blood vessels (avascular), so it heals slowly after injury.

EN Muscle Contraction Mechanism (Sliding Filament Theory): When a nerve signal (action potential) reaches a muscle fibre via a motor neuron, it triggers the release of calcium ions (Ca²⁺) from the sarcoplasmic reticulum inside the muscle cell. Calcium ions bind to troponin, which causes tropomyosin to move, exposing active sites on the actin (thin) filaments. Myosin (thick) filament heads bind to these sites and undergo a power stroke, pulling the actin filaments towards the centre of the sarcomere. This causes the muscle fibre to shorten — the muscle contracts. When the nerve signal stops, Ca²⁺ is pumped back, tropomyosin covers the actin sites again, and the muscle relaxes.

Ca²⁺ ట్రోపోనిన్ తో బంధించబడి, యాక్టిన్ తంతువులపై క్రియాశీల స్థానాలను బహిర్గతం చేస్తుంది. మయోసిన్ తంతువు తలలు ఈ స్థానాలకు అనుసంధానించబడి యాక్టిన్ ను వెళ్ళదీస్తాయి — కండరం సంకోచిస్తుంది. నాడీ సంకేతం ఆగినప్పుడు Ca²⁺ తిరిగి పంప్ చేయబడి కండరం సడలుతుంది.
Keratin and the Functions of Skin / కెరాటిన్ మరియు చర్మం విధులు
EN The outer layer of the skin (epidermis) is made waterproof and tough by a protein called Keratin. Keratin is a fibrous structural protein that fills the cells of the outermost layer of the epidermis (stratum corneum). It prevents water loss from the body, protects against mechanical damage, and acts as a barrier against pathogens. Keratin is also the main structural protein in hair and nails. Perspiration (sweating): The skin contains sweat glands that produce sweat. The primary scientific function of sweating is thermoregulation — as sweat evaporates from the skin surface, it cools the body. Sweat also removes small amounts of waste products such as urea and salts. The skin can tolerate temperatures up to approximately 60°C before blisters begin to form.
TE చర్మం యొక్క బాహ్య పొర (epidermis) కెరాటిన్ అనే ప్రోటీన్ వలన నీటికి అభేద్యంగా ఉంటుంది. కెరాటిన్ శరీరం నుండి నీటి నష్టాన్ని నిరోధిస్తుంది మరియు సూక్ష్మజీవులకు అడ్డుగా పనిచేస్తుంది. ఇది జుట్టు మరియు గోళ్ళలో కూడా ఉంటుంది. చెమట యొక్క ప్రధాన శాస్త్రీయ పాత్ర శరీర ఉష్ణోగ్రత నియంత్రణ (thermoregulation). చెమట ఆవిరైనప్పుడు శరీరం చల్లబడుతుంది. చర్మం 60°C వరకు పొక్కులు లేకుండా తట్టుకుంటుంది.
Histamine, Allergic Reactions, and Immunity / హిస్టామిన్, అలెర్జీ ప్రతిచర్యలు మరియు రోగనిరోధకత
EN Histamine is a chemical mediator released by mast cells and basophils during an allergic reaction. When the immune system encounters an allergen (a substance it has been sensitised to), these cells release histamine into surrounding tissues. Histamine causes the classic symptoms of an allergic reaction: itching, sneezing, runny nose, watery eyes, and redness. Antihistamine drugs (such as cetirizine, loratadine, and diphenhydramine) work by blocking histamine receptors and relieving these symptoms. Antigen: Any foreign substance (bacteria, virus, pollen, dust) that enters the body and triggers an immune response is called an antigen. The immune system produces antibodies (produced by B-lymphocytes/plasma cells) that are specific to each antigen, marking it for destruction.
TE హిస్టామిన్ అనేది అలెర్జీ ప్రతిచర్యలో మాస్ట్ కణాలు మరియు బాసోఫిల్స్ విడుదల చేసే రసాయన మధ్యవర్తి. ఇది దురద, తుమ్ము, ముక్కు కారడం, కళ్ళలో నీళ్ళు వంటి లక్షణాలు కలిగిస్తుంది. యాంటీహిస్టామిన్ మందులు (సెటిరిజైన్, లోరటాడిన్) హిస్టామిన్ గ్రాహకాలను నిరోధించి ఈ లక్షణాలను తగ్గిస్తాయి. యాంటిజెన్ అనేది శరీరంలో ప్రవేశించి రోగనిరోధక ప్రతిస్పందనను ప్రేరేపించే విదేశీ పదార్థం. B-లింఫోసైట్లు (ప్లాస్మా కణాలు) యాంటీబాడీలను ఉత్పత్తి చేస్తాయి.
Vestigial Organs / వెస్టిజియల్ అవయవాలు
EN Vestigial organs are body structures that have lost most or all of their original function through evolution. They are evidence of common ancestry between species. Key vestigial organs in humans include: the Appendix — a small finger-shaped pouch attached to the large intestine; in herbivorous ancestors it helped digest cellulose; the Wisdom teeth (3rd Molars) — ancestral humans needed larger teeth for chewing tough raw food; as human jaws became smaller through evolution, these teeth became surplus; the Coccyx (Tailbone) — the remnant of an ancestral tail, made of 3–5 fused vertebrae at the base of the spine; the Plica semilunaris — a small pink fold at the inner corner of the eye, a remnant of the nictitating membrane (third eyelid) seen in birds and reptiles; and Arrector pili muscles — tiny muscles attached to hair follicles that cause goosebumps (piloerection) when cold or frightened. Note: The Diaphragm is NOT vestigial — it is an essential dome-shaped muscle between the chest and abdomen, critical for breathing.
TE వెస్టిజియల్ అవయవాలు పరిణామ క్రమంలో తమ మూల విధిని కోల్పోయిన శరీర నిర్మాణాలు. మానవులలో ముఖ్యమైన వెస్టిజియల్ అవయవాలు: అపెండిక్స్ — పెద్ద ప్రేగుకు అతుక్కుని ఉండే చిన్న వేలు ఆకారపు సంచి; పూర్వీకులు సెల్యులోజ్ జీర్ణించుకోవడానికి దీన్ని ఉపయోగించేవారు. అక్కల్ పళ్ళు (3rd Molars) — మన పూర్వీకుల పెద్ద దవడకు అనుకూలంగా ఉండేవి. కాక్సిక్స్ (పురి ఎముక) — పూర్వీకుల తోకకు మిగిలిన భాగం. ప్లికా సెమిలూనారిస్ — కన్ను లోపలి మూలలో చిన్న పాపిటి మడత, పక్షులలో చూసే మూడవ కనురెప్ప యొక్క అవశేషం. డయాఫ్రామ్ వెస్టిజియల్ కాదు — ఇది శ్వాసకు అవసరమైన ముఖ్యమైన కండరం.
EN Hinge Joint — Detailed Explanation: The hinge joint is found at the elbow (where the Humerus meets the Radius and Ulna) and the knee (where the Femur meets the Tibia). It allows movement in one plane only — like the hinge of a door, it can bend (flexion) and straighten (extension) but cannot rotate or move sideways. This makes it mechanically very strong and stable in one direction. The ankle joint also has hinge-like characteristics.

Vector-Borne Diseases వాహక జంతు వ్యాధులు
Malaria / మలేరియా
EN Malaria is caused by the protozoan parasite Plasmodium (species: P. vivax, P. falciparum, P. malariae, P. ovale). It is transmitted to humans through the bite of the female Anopheles mosquito. The Plasmodium parasite enters the liver, multiplies, and then infects red blood cells. Inside RBCs, the parasites (called merozoites) multiply until the RBCs burst, releasing toxins and causing the characteristic fever cycle that repeats every 48 to 72 hours. Treatment includes chloroquine and artemisinin-based combination therapy.
TE మలేరియా ప్లాస్మోడియం అనే పరాన్నజీవి వలన వస్తుంది మరియు ఆడ అనాఫెలిస్ దోమ కాటు ద్వారా వ్యాపిస్తుంది. పరాన్నజీవి ముందు కాలేయంలో గుణకారమై, తర్వాత RBC లలో ప్రవేశిస్తుంది. RBC లలో పరాన్నజీవులు (మెరోజోయిట్లు) గుణకారమై RBC లను పగలగొట్టినప్పుడు విషపదార్థాలు విడుదలై జ్వరం వస్తుంది — ఈ చక్రం 48–72 గంటలకు ఒకసారి పునరావృతమవుతుంది.
Filariasis (Elephantiasis) / ఫైలేరియా (ఏనుగు వ్యాధి)
EN Filariasis (Elephantiasis) is caused by a parasitic roundworm called Wuchereria bancrofti. It lives and reproduces inside the lymph vessels of the human body, blocking the normal flow of lymph fluid. This leads to massive swelling of the affected limbs or other body parts, a condition known as Elephantiasis. It is transmitted through the bite of the Culex mosquito.
TE ఫైలేరియా వుచెరేరియా బాన్‌క్రాఫ్టి అనే గుండ్రని పురుగు వలన వస్తుంది. ఇది శోషరస నాళాలలో నివసించి, శోషరస ప్రవాహాన్ని అడ్డుకుంటుంది — ఫలితంగా అవయవాలు వాచిపోతాయి (ఏనుగు వ్యాధి). ఇది క్యూలెక్స్ దోమ కాటు ద్వారా వ్యాపిస్తుంది.

Reproduction, Genetics, and Human Development / జనన క్రియ, జన్యు శాస్త్రం మరియు మానవ అభివృద్ధి
Sex Determination and Chromosomal Disorders / లింగ నిర్ణయం మరియు క్రోమోజోమ్ రుగ్మతలు
EN Every human cell contains 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs. Of these, 22 pairs are autosomes (non-sex chromosomes) and 1 pair are sex chromosomes. Males have sex chromosomes XY (46,XY) while females have XX (46,XX). The sex of an offspring is determined by the sperm of the father, not the egg of the mother. If an X-carrying sperm fertilises the egg, the offspring will be a girl (XX). If a Y-carrying sperm fertilises the egg, the offspring will be a boy (XY). Therefore, the father determines the sex of the child. Chromosomal disorders: Klinefelter syndrome (XXY) results in a male with small testes and some female features. Turner syndrome (XO, only one X chromosome) results in a female with underdeveloped ovaries and short stature.
TE ప్రతి మానవ కణంలో 46 క్రోమోజోమ్లు ఉంటాయి — 22 జతలు ఆటోసోమ్లు మరియు 1 జత లింగ క్రోమోజోమ్లు. పురుషుడిలో XY, స్త్రీలో XX ఉంటాయి. పిల్లల లింగ నిర్ణయం తండ్రి శుక్రకణం ద్వారా జరుగుతుంది — X శుక్రకణం = అమ్మాయి, Y శుక్రకణం = అబ్బాయి. క్లైన్‌ఫెల్టర్ సిండ్రోమ్ (XXY) = పురుషుడు; టర్నర్ సిండ్రోమ్ (XO) = స్త్రీ (అండాశయాలు అభివృద్ధి చెందవు).
DNA Structure and Base Pairing / DNA నిర్మాణం మరియు క్షార జత
EN DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) is the molecule that carries the genetic information of all living organisms. It has the structure of a double helix — two strands wound around each other in opposite directions (antiparallel), as described by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1953. The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary nitrogenous bases: Adenine (A) always pairs with Thymine (T) through 2 hydrogen bonds, and Guanine (G) always pairs with Cytosine (C) through 3 hydrogen bonds. This base pairing rule is called complementarity and is essential for DNA replication and protein synthesis.
TE DNA (డీఆక్సీరైబోన్యూక్లిక్ యాసిడ్) అనేది జీవుల జన్యు సమాచారాన్ని మోసే అణువు. ఇది డబుల్ హెలిక్స్ నిర్మాణాన్ని కలిగి ఉంటుంది — రెండు గొలుసులు ఒకదానికొకటి విరుద్ధ దిశలలో చుట్టుకుంటాయి. వాట్సన్-క్రిక్ నమూనా 1953 లో ప్రతిపాదించబడింది. A-T మధ్య 2 హైడ్రోజన్ బంధాలు, G-C మధ్య 3 హైడ్రోజన్ బంధాలు ఏర్పడతాయి. ఈ బేస్ పేరింగ్ నియమాన్నే పూరకత (complementarity) అంటారు.
Amnion, Placenta, and Foetal Development / ఉల్బం, మావి మరియు పిండ అభివృద్ధి
EN The Amnion is the innermost of the extra-embryonic membranes that surround the developing embryo. It forms a fluid-filled sac called the amniotic cavity, which contains amniotic fluid. This fluid serves two critical functions: it protects the embryo from desiccation (drying out) and from mechanical shocks and jolts. The other membranes are the Chorion (outermost), Allantois, and Yolk sac. The Placenta is a disc-shaped organ that connects the developing foetus to the uterine wall. It enables the exchange of oxygen, nutrients, and hormones from the mother to the foetus, and the removal of carbon dioxide and metabolic waste from the foetus to the mother. The placenta also produces the hormone Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (HCG), which is the hormone detected in pregnancy tests. The development of the foetus can be monitored safely using Ultrasound (Sonography), which uses high-frequency sound waves above 20,000 Hz and does not use ionising radiation.
TE ఉల్బం (Amnion) పిండాన్ని చుట్టుముట్టే అత్యంత లోపలి పొర. ఇది అమ్నియోటిక్ ద్రవంతో నిండిన సంచిని ఏర్పరుస్తుంది, ఇది పిండాన్ని ఎండిపోకుండా మరియు దెబ్బల నుండి రక్షిస్తుంది. మావి (Placenta) తల్లి మరియు పిండాన్ని కలిపే అవయవం. ఇది ఆక్సిజన్, పోషకాలు మరియు హార్మోన్లను పిండానికి అందిస్తుంది మరియు CO₂ మరియు వ్యర్థాలను తొలగిస్తుంది. మావి HCG హార్మోన్‌ను ఉత్పత్తి చేస్తుంది, ఇది గర్భ పరీక్షలలో గుర్తించబడుతుంది. అల్ట్రాసౌండ్ (సోనోగ్రఫీ) 20,000 Hz కంటే అధిక పౌనఃపున్య శబ్దతరంగాలను ఉపయోగించి పిండ అభివృద్ధిని పర్యవేక్షిస్తుంది; ఇది రేడియేషన్ ఉపయోగించదు.
Menstrual Cycle and Reproduction / ఋతుచక్రం మరియు జనన క్రియ
EN In females, the reproductive cycle is called the menstrual cycle and lasts approximately 28 days. It has four phases: Days 1–5: Menstruation — the uterine lining sheds if no fertilisation occurred. Days 6–13: Follicular phase — FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone) from the pituitary gland stimulates the development of a follicle in the ovary, which produces oestrogen. Day 14: Ovulation — a surge of LH (Luteinising Hormone) from the pituitary triggers the release of a mature egg from the ovary. Days 15–28: Luteal phase — the ruptured follicle becomes the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone to maintain the uterine lining for a potential pregnancy. Fertilisation occurs in the Fallopian tube (in the ampulla region). The fertilised egg (zygote) travels to the uterus and implants in the uterine wall after about 4–5 days. Gestation (pregnancy) lasts approximately 38–40 weeks. Parturition (childbirth) is triggered by the hormone Oxytocin, released by the posterior pituitary.

Common Misconceptions Corrected / సాధారణ తప్పు అభిప్రాయాలు
EN Students often confuse respiration with breathing. The correct understanding is that breathing is only the mechanical exchange of gases, while respiration includes both breathing and the biochemical process of energy release in cells using ATP. Students often think all arteries carry oxygenated blood. The correct fact is that arteries carry blood away from the heart; the pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs. Students often confuse the functions of the cerebellum and cerebrum. The correct distinction is that the cerebrum controls voluntary actions and intelligence, while the cerebellum controls balance and coordination. Students often mix up ligaments and tendons. The correct fact is that ligaments connect bone to bone, while tendons connect muscle to bone. Students often assume bile contains digestive enzymes. The correct fact is that bile contains no enzymes — it only emulsifies fats, making them accessible to lipase. Students often think neurons regenerate after injury. The correct fact is that mature neurons in the central nervous system cannot regenerate, which is why spinal cord injuries can cause permanent paralysis.
