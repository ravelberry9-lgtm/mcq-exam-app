1. Cell Theory కణ సిద్ధాంతం
EN The Cell Theory, one of the most fundamental principles of biology, states that (1) all living organisms are composed of one or more cells, (2) the cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life, and (3) all cells arise from pre-existing cells. Matthias Schleiden (1838) proposed that all plants are made of cells; Theodor Schwann (1839) extended this to animals; Rudolf Virchow (1855) added the third tenet — Omnis cellula e cellula (every cell from a pre-existing cell). Robert Hooke first observed and named cells in 1665 when he examined thin slices of cork under a primitive microscope and saw box-like compartments he called "cells." Anton van Leeuwenhoek was the first to observe living microorganisms (bacteria and protozoa) through a microscope in 1674, earning him the title "Father of Microbiology."
TE కణ సిద్ధాంతం జీవశాస్త్రంలో అత్యంత ప్రాథమిక సూత్రాల్లో ఒకటి: (1) అన్ని జీవులు ఒకటి లేదా అంతకు మించిన కణాలతో నిర్మితమై ఉంటాయి; (2) కణం జీవితానికి ప్రాథమిక నిర్మాణ మరియు క్రియాత్మక యూనిట్; (3) అన్ని కణాలు ముందే ఉన్న కణాల నుండి ఉద్భవిస్తాయి. స్లైడెన్ (1838) మొక్కలను, స్వాన్ (1839) జంతువులను, విర్చో (1855) మూడవ నియమాన్ని జోడించారు. రాబర్ట్ హుక్ (1665) మొట్టమొదట కాగ్‌లో కణాలను గమనించారు. లీవెన్‌హుక్ (1674) మొట్టమొదట జీవ సూక్ష్మజీవులను చూశారు.

2. Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells ప్రొకారియోటిక్ vs యూకారియోటిక్ కణాలు
EN All living cells fall into two fundamental categories based on the presence or absence of a membrane-bound nucleus. Prokaryotic cells (pro = before, karyon = nucleus) lack a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; their genetic material (DNA) floats freely in a region called the nucleoid. Examples include bacteria and cyanobacteria (blue-green algae). Eukaryotic cells (eu = true) possess a true nucleus enclosed in a nuclear envelope with nuclear pores, and contain numerous membrane-bound organelles such as mitochondria, chloroplasts, and the Golgi apparatus. All plants, animals, fungi, and protists are eukaryotes. Prokaryotes are generally much smaller (1–10 µm) than eukaryotes (10–100 µm) and are considered evolutionarily older.
TE అన్ని జీవ కణాలు రెండు మౌలిక వర్గాలుగా విభజించబడతాయి. ప్రొకారియోటిక్ కణాలు నిజమైన కేంద్రకం లేదా పొర-బద్ధ అంగాలు లేనివి — DNA న్యూక్లియోయిడ్ ప్రాంతంలో స్వేచ్ఛగా తేలుతూ ఉంటుంది. బ్యాక్టీరియా ఉదాహరణ. యూకారియోటిక్ కణాలు పరమాణు కవచంతో చుట్టబడిన నిజమైన కేంద్రకం మరియు అనేక పొర-బద్ధ అంగాలను కలిగి ఉంటాయి. మొక్కలు, జంతువులు, శిలీంధ్రాలు, ప్రోటిస్టులు యూకారియోట్లు.

3. Cell Organelles కణ అంగాలు
EN Eukaryotic cells contain specialised membrane-bound structures called organelles, each performing a distinct function that contributes to the overall survival and reproduction of the cell. The nucleus is the control centre — it stores the genetic information (DNA) and directs all cellular activities through the production of RNA. The mitochondria are the sites of cellular respiration, producing ATP (adenosine triphosphate) through the electron transport chain and the Krebs cycle; they possess their own DNA and ribosomes, supporting the endosymbiotic theory. Chloroplasts, found only in plant cells and algae, carry out photosynthesis; they also have their own DNA. The Golgi apparatus, discovered by Camillo Golgi in 1898, packages proteins for secretion.

4. Cell Division — Mitosis & Meiosis కణ విభజన
EN Cell division is the fundamental process by which living organisms grow, repair damaged tissues, and reproduce. There are two principal types: Mitosis results in two genetically identical daughter cells each with the same chromosome number as the parent (46 in humans) — it is used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction. The stages are Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, and Telophase (PMAT). Meiosis is a specialised two-stage cell division that produces four genetically unique gametes (sperm or egg cells) each with half the chromosome number (23 in humans — haploid). Crossing over during Prophase I of meiosis shuffles genetic material between homologous chromosomes, creating genetic variation — the raw material for evolution. Cytokinesis is the physical division of the cell cytoplasm that follows nuclear division.

5. DNA, RNA & Central Dogma DNA, RNA మరియు కేంద్రీయ సిద్ధాంతం
EN DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is the hereditary material in almost all living organisms. Its double-helix structure was discovered by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1953, building on X-ray crystallography data provided by Rosalind Franklin (whose critical contribution was long underacknowledged). DNA consists of two antiparallel strands held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs: Adenine pairs with Thymine (A-T, 2 hydrogen bonds) and Guanine pairs with Cytosine (G-C, 3 hydrogen bonds). The Central Dogma of molecular biology (Francis Crick, 1958) states that genetic information flows from DNA → RNA → Protein. This occurs through transcription (DNA → mRNA in the nucleus) and translation (mRNA → protein at ribosomes). The three types of RNA are: mRNA (messenger, carries the code), tRNA (transfer, brings amino acids), and rRNA (ribosomal, structural component of ribosomes).
TE DNA వంశపారంపర్య పదార్థం. దాని ద్విహెలిక్స్ నిర్మాణాన్ని వాట్సన్ మరియు క్రిక్ 1953లో కనుగొన్నారు — రోసలిండ్ ఫ్రాంక్లిన్ X-కిరణ ఛాయాచిత్రాల ఆధారంగా. A-T (2 హైడ్రోజన్ బంధాలు), G-C (3 హైడ్రోజన్ బంధాలు). కేంద్రీయ సిద్ధాంతం: DNA → RNA → ప్రోటీన్. RNA రకాలు: mRNA (సందేశం), tRNA (అమైనో ఆమ్లాలు), rRNA (రైబోజోమ్ నిర్మాణం).

6. Osmosis, Diffusion & Active Transport ద్రావణశాస్త్రం, వ్యాప్తి మరియు చురుకు రవాణా
EN Diffusion is the net movement of molecules from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration — it does not require energy (passive) and does not require a membrane. Osmosis is the special case of diffusion involving water molecules across a semi-permeable membrane — water moves from a region of lower solute concentration (hypotonic) to higher solute concentration (hypertonic). Plasmolysis occurs when a plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution: water leaves the cell, the cell membrane shrinks away from the cell wall, and the cell becomes flaccid. Turgidity is the state when a plant cell is fully inflated with water (in hypotonic solution), pressing the membrane firmly against the cell wall — this maintains rigidity in non-woody plants. Active transport moves substances against the concentration gradient using energy (ATP) through carrier proteins (e.g., sodium-potassium pump).

7. Ecosystems & Food Chains పర్యావరణ వ్యవస్థలు మరియు ఆహార గొలుసులు
EN An ecosystem is a functional unit of nature where living organisms (biotic components) interact with each other and with the non-living environment (abiotic components such as sunlight, water, temperature, and soil). Biotic components are classified as: Producers (autotrophs, mainly green plants that fix solar energy through photosynthesis), Consumers (heterotrophs — herbivores/primary, carnivores/secondary, tertiary consumers), and Decomposers (fungi and bacteria that break down dead organic matter and return nutrients to the soil). A food chain represents the linear transfer of energy from one organism to another: Producer → Herbivore → Carnivore → Top Carnivore. Lindeman's 10% Law (1942) states that only 10% of the energy at one trophic level is available to the next level — the rest is lost as heat. A food web represents the interconnected network of food chains in an ecosystem.

8. Biogeochemical Cycles జీవభూరాసాయనిక చక్రాలు
EN Biogeochemical cycles describe the movement of chemical elements between living organisms and the physical environment. The Carbon cycle involves the uptake of CO₂ by plants during photosynthesis, release through respiration and decomposition, and long-term storage in fossil fuels; burning fossil fuels returns this stored carbon rapidly, contributing to the greenhouse effect. The Nitrogen cycle involves nitrogen fixation (conversion of atmospheric N₂ to ammonia by Rhizobium in root nodules, Azotobacter in soil, lightning, and the industrial Haber process), nitrification (ammonia → nitrites → nitrates by Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter), assimilation (plants absorb nitrates), and denitrification (Pseudomonas converts nitrates back to N₂). The Water cycle (hydrological cycle) involves evaporation from oceans, condensation to form clouds, and precipitation as rain or snow. The Oxygen cycle is driven by photosynthesis (O₂ production) balanced by respiration and combustion (O₂ consumption).

9. Biodiversity & Environmental Issues జీవవైవిధ్యం మరియు పర్యావరణ సమస్యలు
EN Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth at all its levels — genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity. Biodiversity hotspots are regions with exceptional concentration of endemic species (found nowhere else) that face significant habitat loss. India has four biodiversity hotspots: the Himalayas, the Western Ghats, the Indo-Burma region, and the Sundaland. The IUCN Red List categorises species by extinction risk: Extinct (EX), Extinct in the Wild (EW), Critically Endangered (CR), Endangered (EN), Vulnerable (VU), Near Threatened (NT), Least Concern (LC). Project Tiger was launched in 1973 to conserve the Bengal tiger (India's national animal); as of recent counts, India has over 3,000 tigers. The Wildlife Protection Act 1972 provides the legal framework for conservation of wildlife in India, creating a system of protected areas including National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries, Biosphere Reserves, and Tiger Reserves.

10. Origin of Life & Theories of Evolution జీవితం యొక్క మూలం మరియు పరిణామ సిద్ధాంతాలు
EN The question of how life originated on Earth has fascinated scientists for centuries. The Oparin-Haldane hypothesis (1920s–1930s) proposed that life arose from simple organic molecules in a "primordial soup" of warm water containing inorganic chemicals. The Miller-Urey experiment (1953) provided the first experimental support: Stanley Miller and Harold Urey simulated early Earth conditions (CH₄, NH₃, H₂, H₂O vapour + electrical sparks to simulate lightning) and produced amino acids, showing that the building blocks of life can form abiotically. The first life forms were simple prokaryotes approximately 3.5 billion years ago. Cyanobacteria were the first oxygen-producing organisms (~2.7 billion years ago), gradually changing Earth's atmosphere from reducing to oxidising and enabling the evolution of complex aerobic life. The oldest fossils are stromatolites — layered structures formed by ancient cyanobacteria.
TE ఒపారిన్-హాల్డేన్ పరికల్పన (1920-30) జీవితం "ప్రాచీన సూప్"లో సరళ సేంద్రీయ అణువుల నుండి ఉద్భవించిందని ప్రతిపాదించింది. మిల్లర్-యురే ప్రయోగం (1953): ప్రాచీన భూమి పరిస్థితులను అనుకరించి అమైనో ఆమ్లాలను ఉత్పత్తి చేశారు — జీవితం అబయోటిక్‌గా ఏర్పడగలదని నిరూపించారు. మొట్టమొదటి జీవులు ప్రొకారియోట్లు (~3.5 బిలియన్ సంవత్సరాల క్రితం). సైనోబ్యాక్టీరియా మొట్టమొదట O₂ ఉత్పత్తి చేసింది.

11. Evidence for Evolution పరిణామానికి ఆధారాలు
EN Multiple independent lines of evidence support the theory of evolution. Fossil record shows a sequence of increasingly complex organisms over geological time — transitional fossils (like Archaeopteryx, which shows features of both reptiles and birds) are particularly important. Comparative anatomy reveals two key patterns: homologous organs have the same basic structure and evolutionary origin but different functions (e.g., human arm, bat wing, whale flipper, horse leg — all derived from the same pentadactyl limb), indicating common ancestry; analogous organs have similar functions but different structures and evolutionary origins (e.g., wings of birds and insects), indicating convergent evolution. Vestigial organs are reduced, non-functional remnants of organs that were functional in ancestors — examples in humans include the appendix, coccyx (tailbone), wisdom teeth, body hair, and ear muscles. Molecular evidence (DNA similarity) shows that the more closely related two species are, the more similar their DNA sequences — humans and chimpanzees share ~98% DNA.

12. Human Evolution మానవ పరిణామం
EN Human evolution traces our lineage from early primates to modern Homo sapiens sapiens. The key stages in hominid evolution begin with Dryopithecus (ape-like, walked on knuckles), followed by Ramapithecus (more human-like facial features, lived in Asia and Africa ~14 million years ago). Australopithecus ("Lucy," ~4 million years ago, discovered by Donald Johanson in Ethiopia in 1974) was bipedal (walked upright on two legs) and had a brain capacity of ~400–500 cc. Homo habilis (~2.5 million years ago) was the first to make and use stone tools — hence the name "handy man." Homo erectus (1.5 million years ago) had a larger brain (~900 cc), used fire, and is represented by Java Man and Peking Man. Homo sapiens neanderthalensis (~2 lakh years ago) had a larger brain than modern humans but is now extinct. Homo sapiens sapiens (Cro-Magnon, ~30,000 years ago) is anatomically modern humans — "Out of Africa" theory suggests they originated in Africa and spread worldwide.
TE మానవ పరిణామం మొట్టమొదటి ప్రైమేట్ల నుండి ఆధునిక మానవుల వరకు మన వంశాన్ని వెలికితీస్తుంది. డ్రయోపిథికస్ (వాన వంటిది) → రామాపిథికస్ → ఆస్ట్రలోపిథికస్ ("లూసీ", ద్విపాద, ~4 మిలియన్ సంవత్సరాలు) → హోమో హాబిలిస్ (రాతి పనిముట్లు, ~2.5 మిలియన్) → హోమో ఎరెక్టస్ (అగ్ని, జావా మ్యాన్, 1.5 మిలియన్) → నియాండర్తల్ (~2 లక్ష సంవత్సరాలు) → హోమో సేపియన్స్ (ఆధునిక మానవుడు, క్రో-మాగ్నన్).

1Cell theory: Schleiden (1838) + Schwann (1839) + Virchow (1855). Robert Hooke coined "cell" (1665).
2Prokaryotes: no nucleus, 70S ribosomes, peptidoglycan cell wall. Eukaryotes: true nucleus, 80S ribosomes.
3Mitochondria = powerhouse (ATP). Chloroplast = kitchen (photosynthesis). Both have own DNA — endosymbiotic theory.
4Lysosome = suicide bags (hydrolytic enzymes). Absent in RBCs and plant cells. Golgi = traffic police.
5Centriole: absent in plant cells and fungi. Needed for spindle formation in animal cell division.
6Mitosis (PMAT): 2 identical daughter cells, 46 chr→46. Meiosis: 4 unique gametes, 46→23. Crossing over in Prophase I.
7DNA: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds). Watson-Crick 1953. Rosalind Franklin = X-ray data. Human genome completed 2003.
8Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein. mRNA (messenger), tRNA (transfer), rRNA (ribosomal).
9Osmosis: hypotonic → hypertonic through semi-permeable membrane. Plasmolysis in hypertonic solution. Active transport needs ATP.
10Lindeman 10% law: only 10% energy passes to next trophic level. Energy pyramid always upright. Number pyramid can be inverted.
11Nitrogen fixation: Rhizobium (root nodules, symbiotic), Azotobacter (free-living). Denitrification: Pseudomonas.
12India's 4 biodiversity hotspots: Himalayas, Western Ghats, Indo-Burma, Sundaland. Project Tiger: 1973.
13Ozone depletion: CFCs. Montreal Protocol 1987. Ozone at 15–35 km (stratosphere). Acid rain: pH < 5.6 (SO₂ + NOₓ).
14Miller-Urey (1953): proved organic molecules (amino acids) can form abiotically. First life: prokaryotes ~3.5 billion years ago.
15Lamarck (1809) WRONG. Darwin (1859) natural selection CORRECT. "Survival of fittest" = Herbert Spencer's phrase.
16Australopithecus (Lucy) = first bipedal hominid (~4 Mya). Homo habilis = first tool user. Homo erectus = first fire user.
17Homologous = same structure, different function (common ancestry). Analogous = different structure, same function (convergent evolution).
18Hardy-Weinberg principle: allele frequencies constant in ideal population (no mutation, random mating, no selection, large population).
