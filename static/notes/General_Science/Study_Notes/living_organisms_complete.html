Introduction: What Are Living Organisms? జీవులు అంటే ఏమిటి?
EN — Characteristics of Life A living organism is any entity that exhibits all the fundamental characteristics of life. According to NCERT, the seven essential characteristics that distinguish living beings from non-living matter are: (1) Nutrition — obtaining and using food for energy and growth; (2) Respiration — releasing energy from food through oxidation; (3) Growth — an irreversible increase in mass and number; (4) Reproduction — producing offspring of the same kind; (5) Excretion — eliminating metabolic waste products; (6) Response to stimuli — reacting to changes in the environment; and (7) Movement — either of the whole body or of parts. Viruses are a unique exception: they are non-living crystals outside a host cell, but replicate inside host cells, placing them on the boundary of life.

Memory Aid — Five Kingdoms in Order
The five kingdoms from simplest to most complex are: Monera (no nucleus), Protista (single-celled eukaryotes), Fungi (absorb food externally), Plantae (make their own food), and Animalia (ingest and digest food). A useful sentence to remember the order is: "My Pet Fish Plays Around." మొనెరా → ప్రోటిస్టా → శిలీంధ్రాలు → వృక్షాలు → జంతువులు. సరళత నుండి సంక్లిష్టతకు పెరిగే క్రమం.

Microbes: Bacteria — Discovery, Structure & Classification బ్యాక్టీరియా — ఆవిష్కరణ, నిర్మాణం
EN — Bacteria Basics Bacteria are prokaryotic microorganisms belonging to Kingdom Monera. They were first observed in 1674 by the Dutch scientist Anton van Leeuwenhoek, who described moving microbes under his self-made microscope and named them animalcules ("little animals"). He is regarded as the Father of Microbiology. Bacteria have a simple cell lacking a true nucleus; genetic material is a single circular DNA molecule in the cytoplasm (nucleoid). The cell wall is made of peptidoglycan. Based on the Gram stain test, bacteria are classified into two groups: Gram-positive bacteria (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus) have a thick peptidoglycan wall and teichoic acid; they retain the violet dye and appear purple. Gram-negative bacteria (e.g., Escherichia coli) have a thin peptidoglycan layer and an outer membrane; they lose the violet dye and appear red/pink. All bacteria carry out respiration — aerobic (using oxygen), anaerobic (without oxygen), or facultative (both). Bacteria do not produce hormones; they regulate through chemical signalling called quorum sensing. Important examples: Rhizobium fixes atmospheric nitrogen in legume root nodules; Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) are photosynthetic bacteria that fix nitrogen; Lactobacillus ferments milk into curd.

EN — Important Bacteria Rhizobium lives in root nodules of leguminous plants (peas, beans, lentils) and converts atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) into ammonia (NH₃) that the plant uses for protein synthesis — this is called biological nitrogen fixation. Cyanobacteria (formerly blue-green algae) are the oldest known oxygen-producers; they photosynthesise and fix nitrogen simultaneously. Anabaena and Nostoc are common examples used in biofertilisers. Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae), a fungus, ferments glucose anaerobically: glucose → ethanol + CO₂. The CO₂ released causes bread dough to rise. Penicillin, the world's first antibiotic, was discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928 from the mould Penicillium notatum; it kills Gram-positive bacteria by inhibiting peptidoglycan synthesis.

Exam Trap — Hormones in Bacteria
Bacteria do not produce hormones. They regulate population behaviour through quorum sensing — chemical signals released by individual bacteria that accumulate as the population grows. Hormones are found only in multicellular eukaryotes (animals and plants). Candidates often incorrectly assign hormones to bacteria; the correct answer is always that bacteria are hormone-free. బ్యాక్టీరియాలో హార్మోన్లు ఉండవు. అవి క్వోరమ్ సెన్సింగ్ ద్వారా నియంత్రించబడతాయి. హార్మోన్లు బహుకణ యూకారియోట్‌లలో మాత్రమే ఉంటాయి.

Viruses — Structure, Genome & Replication వైరస్‌లు — నిర్మాణం, జన్యువు, ప్రతికృతి
EN — What Is a Virus? A virus is an acellular (non-cellular) entity that occupies the boundary between living and non-living matter. Outside a host cell, a virus exists as an inert crystalline particle with no metabolism. Inside a host cell, it hijacks the cell's machinery to replicate itself, which is why viruses are considered to exhibit the property of life — the ability to duplicate themselves. A virus consists of a protein coat (capsid) surrounding a core of either DNA or RNA, never both. Some viruses (enveloped viruses) also have a lipid envelope derived from the host cell membrane. Viruses cannot grow, metabolise food, or produce energy independently; they are obligate intracellular parasites. The tobacco mosaic virus (TMV), the first virus ever discovered (Ivanovsky 1892; Stanley crystallised it in 1935), contains RNA as its genetic material. Similarly, HIV, influenza, polio, dengue, Hepatitis C, and COVID-19 are RNA viruses.
TE — వైరస్ అంటే ఏమిటి? వైరస్ అకణ (కణ నిర్మాణం లేని) అస్తిత్వం; జీవ-నిర్జీవ సరిహద్దులో ఉంటుంది. అతిధేయి కణం వెలుపల జడమైన స్ఫటికం; లోపల కణ యంత్రాంగాన్ని వాడుకుని ప్రతికృతి చేసుకుంటుంది. నిర్మాణం: ప్రోటీన్ కోటు (క్యాప్సిడ్) + లోపల DNA లేదా RNA (రెండూ కాదు). స్వతంత్రంగా జీవించలేవు; తప్పనిసరిగా అతిధేయి కణంలోనే పెరుగుతాయి. తంబాకు మొజాయిక్ వైరస్ (TMV) — మొదట కనుగొన్న వైరస్ — RNA మాత్రమే జన్యు పదార్థంగా కలిగి ఉంటుంది. EN — HIV and AIDS HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a retrovirus with an RNA genome. It uses the enzyme reverse transcriptase to convert its RNA into DNA inside the host cell, then integrates into the host's chromosomes. HIV specifically targets CD4+ T-helper lymphocytes, the central coordinators of the immune response. As HIV destroys these cells, the immune system collapses — a condition called AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome). With a depleted immune system, the body becomes vulnerable to opportunistic infections such as tuberculosis, Pneumocystis pneumonia, and fungal infections. HIV is transmitted through blood, sexual contact, and mother-to-child during childbirth or breastfeeding. HIV is considered "alive" because it can auto-reproduce inside host cells.

HIV CD4+ T-సహాయక లింఫోసైట్‌లను నాశనం చేస్తుంది — ఫలితంగా రోగనిరోధక వ్యవస్థ కూలిపోతుంది; ఈ స్థితిని AIDS అంటారు. AIDS వల్ల TB, శిలీంధ్ర సోంకులు వంటి అవకాశవాద వ్యాధులు వస్తాయి. HIV రక్తం, లైంగిక సంపర్కం, ప్రసవం ద్వారా వ్యాపిస్తుంది. HIV అతిధేయిలో స్వయంగా పునరుత్పత్తి చేయగలిగినందున జీవిగా పరిగణించబడుతుంది.
Key Fact — Virus Genome is ALWAYS Either DNA or RNA, Never Both
Every virus carries genetic material in the form of either DNA or RNA — never both in the same particle. Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV), the first virus to be crystallised, carries only RNA. This fact is among the most frequently tested in APPSC and TSPSC examinations. RNA viruses mutate far faster than DNA viruses because RNA polymerase lacks the proofreading ability of DNA polymerase — this is why influenza vaccines must be reformulated each year. వైరస్‌లో జన్యు పదార్థం DNA లేదా RNA (రెండూ కాదు). TMV — RNA మాత్రమే. RNA వైరస్‌లు వేగంగా మ్యూటేట్ అవుతాయి కాబట్టి ఫ్లూ వ్యాక్సిన్ ప్రతి సంవత్సరం మార్చాలి.

Symbiosis — Lichen, Mycorrhiza & Rhizobium సహజీవనం — లైకెన్, మైకోరైజా, రైజోబియం
EN — Symbiosis Defined Symbiosis is a close, long-term interaction between two different species. When both species benefit from the association, it is called mutualism. Three of the most important mutualistic associations tested in competitive examinations are the lichen, mycorrhiza, and Rhizobium partnerships. Lichens are composite organisms formed by the intimate symbiosis of algae and fungi. The alga (phycobiont) performs photosynthesis and supplies carbohydrates; the fungus (mycobiont) provides physical structure, water, and minerals. Lichens are pioneer organisms that colonise bare rock surfaces and are extremely sensitive to air pollution — their presence or absence serves as a reliable bioindicator of air quality. Mycorrhiza is the mutualistic association between fungi and the roots of higher plants. The fungal hyphae extend far into the soil, greatly increasing the root's surface area for water and phosphorus absorption. The plant reciprocates by supplying photosynthetic sugars to the fungus. Approximately 90 percent of all land plant species form mycorrhizal associations; without them, terrestrial plant life as we know it would not be possible. Rhizobium is a nitrogen-fixing bacterium that lives in root nodules of leguminous plants such as peas, beans, and groundnuts. It converts atmospheric nitrogen gas into ammonia, enriching the soil and reducing the need for chemical fertilisers.

Lower Invertebrates — Evolution Sequence నిమ్న అకశేరుకాలు — పరిణామ క్రమం
EN — From Single Cell to Complex Body Plans The animal kingdom is organised from simplest to most complex in what biologists call the evolutionary sequence. Beginning with the single-celled Protozoa, each successive phylum represents an increase in structural and functional complexity. Porifera (sponges) are multicellular but lack true tissues; they are sessile filter feeders and are classified as animals despite their plant-like appearance. Coelenterata (Cnidaria) — including jellyfish, sea anemones, and sea fans — have tissue-level organisation with two body layers (diploblastic) and stinging cells (cnidocytes). Platyhelminthes (flatworms) introduced bilateral symmetry and a head-tail axis; they include the parasitic tapeworm and liver fluke. Nematoda (roundworms) are the most abundant animals on Earth; they have a pseudocoelom and a complete digestive tube. Annelida (segmented worms) such as the earthworm and leech introduced true coelom and metamerism (repetitive segmentation). Mollusca includes snails, clams, squid, and octopus; pearls form inside oysters (molluscs) when the mantle coats an irritant with layers of nacre. Echinodermata (starfish, sea urchin, sea cucumber) are exclusively marine and have a water-vascular system; starfish is not a true fish.

EN — The Three Important Worms The three worms most tested in examinations represent different phyla with very different lifestyles. The Earthworm (Pheretima posthuma) belongs to Phylum Annelida. It is a hermaphrodite — every individual possesses both male (testes) and female (ovaries) reproductive organs. Despite this, earthworms do not self-fertilise; two individuals align belly-to-belly and exchange sperm through the clitellum. Earthworm haemoglobin is unique: it is dissolved freely in the plasma rather than enclosed in red blood cells (earthworms have no RBCs). Special cells called chloragogen cells surround the intestine and function like a liver, storing glycogen and fat. The calciferous glands in the oesophagus neutralise soil acids by secreting calcium carbonate. Earthworms aerate and fertilise soil by ingesting it and excreting nutrient-rich castings; Darwin famously called them "nature's plough." The Tapeworm (Taenia solium) belongs to Phylum Platyhelminthes. It is an endoparasite living in the human small intestine, attaching itself by hooks and suckers on its scolex. It has no digestive system and absorbs nutrients directly through its body wall. The pig serves as the intermediate host. The Leech (Hirudo medicinalis) belongs to Phylum Annelida. It is an ectoparasite that feeds on blood and secretes hirudin, a powerful anticoagulant that prevents blood from clotting while it feeds. Hirudin has medical applications in preventing blood clots after microsurgery.

Insects and Arthropods — Orders, Bees & Sericulture కీటకాలు — క్రమాలు, తేనెటీగలు, పట్టు
EN — The Insect World Insects are the most species-rich class of animals, comprising approximately 75 percent of all known animal species. They are classified into 26 orders, including Coleoptera (beetles — the largest order), Lepidoptera (butterflies and moths), Hymenoptera (ants, bees, and wasps), Diptera (flies and mosquitoes), Hemiptera (bugs), and Orthoptera (grasshoppers and crickets). Insects display remarkable social organisation in eusocial species such as ants, honeybees, and termites. In a honeybee colony, the queen is the fertile female who lays all eggs; worker bees are sterile females that collect nectar, make honey, tend brood, and defend the hive; drones are males whose sole function is to fertilise the queen on the nuptial flight. Ants achieve near-360 degree vision through their compound eyes, which are composed of thousands of individual lenses (ommatidia). Sericulture is the commercial rearing of silkworms for silk production. The silk is produced by the larva (Bombyx mori) of the silk moth, which feeds exclusively on mulberry leaves. The larva spins a continuous silk thread approximately one kilometre long around itself before entering the pupal stage, forming the cocoon. Cocoons are boiled before the moth emerges to preserve the unbroken thread. Other silkworms include tasar (Antheraea mylitta), eri (Samia cynthia), and muga (Antheraea assamensis). Apiculture (bee-keeping) is the practice of maintaining honeybee colonies for honey, beeswax, propolis, and pollination. The commercial honeybee species is Apis mellifera in Europe and Apis cerana (Indian honeybee) in Asia. Honeybees communicate the location and distance of food sources through the waggle dance (Karl von Frisch, 1973 Nobel Prize): the angle of the dance indicates direction relative to the sun; the duration of the waggle run indicates distance. Aestivation is the summer dormancy entered by certain animals (snails, lungfish, earthworms) in response to extreme heat or drought, analogous to hibernation in winter.

Amphibians and Reptiles — Cold-Blooded Vertebrates ఉభయచరాలు మరియు సరీసృపాలు
EN — Poikilothermic Vertebrates Amphibians and reptiles are poikilothermic (cold-blooded or ectothermic) — their body temperature varies with the environmental temperature. They do not generate body heat internally through metabolism (unlike birds and mammals). Frogs and toads are both amphibians, but they differ in several ways. Frogs have smooth, moist skin and are more dependent on water; toads have dry, warty skin and can live in drier habitats. Adult amphibians excrete urea and their kidney type is the mesonephros — the intermediate stage in the evolution of vertebrate kidneys (pronephros in larvae, mesonephros in adult amphibians and fish, metanephros in reptiles, birds, and mammals). Reptiles include lizards, snakes, crocodilians, and turtles. All reptiles have a three-chambered heart — with the exception of crocodiles, which uniquely possess a four-chambered heart, the most advanced heart in the reptile class and the same structural arrangement as in birds and mammals. The King Cobra (Ophiophagus hannah) is the world's longest venomous snake (up to 5.5 m) and the only snake species that builds a nest — constructing a mound of leaves and soil to incubate eggs, which it guards vigilantly. Snakes detect odours using their forked tongue in conjunction with the Jacobson's organ (vomeronasal organ) in the palate — the tongue picks up scent particles from the air and delivers them to the Jacobson's organ for analysis. Snake venom glands are modified posterior salivary glands. A venomous snake bite typically leaves two puncture marks from the paired hollow fangs. The python is non-venomous; it kills prey by constriction. Among cold-blooded animals, the tortoise has the longest lifespan, with some Galapagos tortoises living more than 175 years.

Aquatic Life — Plankton, Benthos, Nekton & Records జల జీవులు — ప్లవకాలు, బెంథోస్, నెక్టన్
EN — Life in Water Aquatic life is organised into distinct ecological zones based on where organisms live and how they move. Plankton drift in the water column, unable to swim against currents. They include phytoplankton (microscopic algae that perform photosynthesis, forming the base of aquatic food chains) and zooplankton (microscopic animals). Nekton are active swimmers that can move against currents — fish, whales, dolphins, and squid belong to this group. Benthos are organisms that live on or in the bottom sediment — crabs, sponges, sea cucumbers, and clams are benthos. Neuston live at the water surface film, such as water striders. The blue whale (Balaenoptera musculus) holds two records: it is the largest living animal (up to 30 metres long, 180 tonnes) and produces the biggest baby of any animal (calves are about 7 metres long and 2.7 tonnes at birth). Among birds, the smallest egg is laid by the Bee Hummingbird (about 6 mm long), while the largest bird egg is laid by the ostrich. The human ovum (~0.1 mm) is the largest cell visible to the naked eye and the largest human cell, but it is not classified as an "egg" in the bird/reptile sense. Crustaceans such as crabs and prawns have copper-based haemocyanin as their blood pigment, giving their blood a blue colour (unlike the iron-based haemoglobin that gives vertebrate blood its red colour). Prawns are crustaceans, not insects — an examination trap. The camel has two distinct water-conservation adaptations: the hump stores fat (which is metabolised to produce metabolic water), and bulk water is stored in the intestinal cells (not the hump). These are two separate facts. Pearls form inside molluscs (primarily oysters and mussels) when the mantle secretes nacre around an irritant. Chlorella is a unicellular green alga used in space missions to maintain oxygen levels and recycle CO₂.

Birds — Characteristics, Records & Special Facts పక్షులు — లక్షణాలు, రికార్డులు
EN — Class Aves Birds (Class Aves) are warm-blooded (homeothermic) vertebrates uniquely characterised by the presence of feathers, hollow (pneumatic) bones that reduce weight for flight, a four-chambered heart, and the syrinx — the voice-producing organ located at the bifurcation of the trachea into the two bronchi. The syrinx is unique to birds; mammals produce sound using the larynx. Birds obtain thrust in flight by flapping their wings, which simultaneously generates lift and forward thrust. The cerebellum is the brain region responsible for balance and muscular coordination; a pigeon whose cerebellum is destroyed cannot fly because it loses coordination of wing movements. Birds have air sacs connected to the lungs, ensuring a continuous flow of fresh air through the lungs in both inhalation and exhalation — making bird respiration more efficient than mammalian. Ducks and geese have webbed feet with skin between the toes that acts as paddles for swimming. Several bird species are flightless: the ostrich (Africa, largest living bird, up to 2.7 m and 150 kg), the emu (Australia), the kiwi (New Zealand), and the penguin (which uses its wings as flippers for swimming). Mammals — Characteristics, Records & Special Facts క్షీరదాలు — లక్షణాలు, రికార్డులు
EN — Class Mammalia Mammals (Class Mammalia) are warm-blooded vertebrates defined by five key characteristics: (1) presence of mammary glands that produce milk to nourish offspring; (2) body covered with hair or fur for insulation; (3) a muscular diaphragm separating the thoracic and abdominal cavities and assisting in breathing; (4) a four-chambered heart ensuring complete separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood; (5) most give birth to live young (viviparous). The vast majority of mammals are placentals; however, monotremes are the remarkable egg-laying exceptions. The world's only two living monotreme species are the duck-billed platypus (Ornithorhynchus anatinus) and the spiny anteater (echidna) (Tachyglossus aculeatus) — both native to Australia and New Guinea. Although they lay leathery eggs, they are true mammals because they produce milk (through skin pores, as they have no nipples) and are warm-blooded. The male platypus has venomous spurs on its hind legs. The mule is a sterile hybrid produced by crossing a female horse (mare, 64 chromosomes) with a male donkey (jack, 62 chromosomes), yielding offspring with 63 chromosomes. The odd chromosome number prevents normal meiosis, making the mule sterile. The reverse cross (stallion + female donkey) produces a hinny, which is rarer and smaller. The cow has a gestation period of 280 days, similar to humans. An elephant has the longest gestation period of any land mammal — approximately 22 months (660 days). Colostrum is the thick, yellowish first milk produced by mammals in the two to three days after giving birth; it is rich in antibodies (IgA, IgG), lactoferrin, and immune cells, providing passive immunity to the newborn. Deer are not considered rabies vectors; rabies is primarily transmitted by dogs, cats, foxes, raccoons, and bats. Guinea pig (Cavia porcellus) is a short-tailed rodent widely used in biomedical research; like humans, guinea pigs cannot synthesise vitamin C and require it in their diet.

Microbiology Pioneers — Discoveries and Timeline సూక్ష్మజీవశాస్త్ర పయొనీర్లు — ఆవిష్కరణలు
EN — The Founders of Modern Biology The history of biology is marked by revolutionary discoveries that changed human understanding of life, disease, and the microscopic world. Robert Hooke (1635–1703) coined the word "cell" in 1665 after observing the dead cells in a thin slice of cork under a compound microscope. Anton van Leeuwenhoek (1632–1723), a Dutch cloth merchant who built the finest microscopes of his era (270x magnification), became in 1674 the first person to observe live microorganisms, which he called animalcules; among the organisms he described was Spirogyra. He is celebrated as the Father of Microbiology. Louis Pasteur (1822–1895) overturned the theory of spontaneous generation through his swan-neck flask experiments and established the Germ Theory of Disease, which holds that specific microorganisms cause specific diseases. He also developed pasteurisation and vaccines for chicken cholera, anthrax, and rabies. Robert Koch (1843–1910) discovered the causative agents of tuberculosis (Mycobacterium tuberculosis, 1882) and cholera (Vibrio cholerae, 1883) and formulated Koch's postulates — the scientific criteria for proving that a specific organism causes a specific disease. Edward Jenner (1749–1823) pioneered vaccination by using cowpox material to protect against smallpox in 1796 — the world's first vaccine. Alexander Fleming (1881–1955) discovered penicillin in 1928 when he noticed that the mould Penicillium notatum inhibited bacterial growth in a petri dish. Penicillin was the world's first antibiotic and revolutionised medicine. James Watson and Francis Crick, aided by X-ray crystallography data from Rosalind Franklin, proposed the double helix structure of DNA in 1953.

Evolution Sequence — Simple to Complex పరిణామ క్రమం — సరళత నుండి సంక్లిష్టతకు
EN — The Great Chain of Animal Life Biological evolution proceeds from simpler to more complex organisms over geological time. The evolutionary sequence of animal phyla — from the earliest single-celled organisms to the most complex vertebrates — reflects increasing levels of structural organisation: cellular, tissue, organ, and organ-system. Protozoa represent the base: single-celled eukaryotic animals (Amoeba, Plasmodium). Porifera (sponges) are the simplest multicellular animals; they have no true tissues or organs, and no nervous system. Despite their sessile, plant-like appearance, sponges are classified as animals. Coelenterata (Cnidaria) exhibit tissue-level organisation with two cell layers (diploblastic); their specialised stinging cells (cnidocytes) are unique. Platyhelminthes introduced bilateral symmetry, cephalisation (head formation), and the beginnings of organ-level organisation. Nematoda added a complete digestive system (mouth-to-anus) and a pseudocoelom. Annelida represent a major advance with a true coelom, metamerism (body segmentation), and a closed circulatory system. Arthropoda — the largest animal phylum — added a chitinous exoskeleton and jointed appendages; insects, crustaceans, arachnids, and myriapods all belong here. Mollusca are the second-largest phylum; the mantle, shell, and radula are defining features. Echinodermata are exclusively marine, have a water-vascular system, and show deuterostome development (like vertebrates). Finally, Chordata — defined by the presence of a notochord at some stage — includes all vertebrates: fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals. The notochord becomes the vertebral column in vertebrates. Peripatus, the living fossil, serves as the connecting link between Annelida and Arthropoda; it has remained structurally similar for approximately 500 million years and is one of the few remaining examples of a transition form in the fossil record.

EN — Plant Hormones and Algae Plant biology contributes several important examination topics. Phycology is the study of algae (from Greek phukos = seaweed); not to be confused with Mycology (fungi study), Melittology (bee study), or Acarology (mite study). Auxins (indole-3-acetic acid, IAA) are plant hormones that promote cell elongation and root initiation, but at high concentrations they inhibit the normal development of fleshy fruits. Other plant hormones include gibberellins (stem elongation, seed germination), cytokinins (cell division, delays leaf ageing), ethylene (fruit ripening, a gas), and abscisic acid/ABA (stress response, dormancy induction). The herbivorous animals digest cellulose in the cecum (caecum), where symbiotic bacteria break down cellulose that the host's own enzymes cannot digest. The chameleon changes skin colour using chromatophore cells (pigment-containing cells) under combined nervous and hormonal control, serving both camouflage and intra-species communication. Spirogyra is a filamentous green alga named for the spiral arrangement of its chloroplasts; it was among the organisms first described by Leeuwenhoek in 1674 and is commonly found in freshwater ponds. The plant micronutrient most tested is Zinc (Zn); phosphorus, magnesium, and calcium are macronutrients required in larger quantities. Zinc deficiency causes "khaira" disease in rice and "little leaf" disease in mango.
