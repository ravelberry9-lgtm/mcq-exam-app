Introduction — What Is a Plant? పరిచయం — మొక్క అంటే ఏమిటి?
EN The plant kingdom encompasses all organisms that carry out photosynthesis using chlorophyll, possess a rigid cell wall made of cellulose, and are generally fixed in one place. While animals also breathe, reproduce, and respond to stimuli, the single most distinctive feature that separates plants from all other kingdoms is the presence of a cellulose cell wall. Cellulose is a long-chain polymer of glucose linked by beta-1,4 glycosidic bonds. It gives rigidity to the cell, prevents the cell from bursting in hypotonic solutions, and is absent in animal cells, which have only a flexible plasma membrane. Some non-photosynthetic plants exist (such as Cuscuta, the dodder), and some plants are not fixed (aquatic floating plants), so the cellulose wall remains the truly universal and defining trait. The plant kingdom is broadly divided into five major sub-divisions arranged from simple to complex: Algae, Bryophytes, Pteridophytes, Gymnosperms, and Angiosperms.
TE వృక్ష రాజ్యంలో క్లోరోఫిల్ సహాయంతో కిరణజన్యక్రియ చేసే, సెల్యులోజ్‌తో నిర్మించిన దృఢమైన కణ గోడ కలిగిన మరియు సాధారణంగా స్థిరంగా ఉండే జీవులు ఉంటాయి. జంతువులు కూడా శ్వాసిస్తాయి, పునరుత్పత్తి చేస్తాయి, ప్రతిస్పందిస్తాయి, కానీ మొక్కలను అన్ని ఇతర రాజ్యాల నుండి వేరు చేసే అత్యంత ప్రత్యేక లక్షణం సెల్యులోజ్ కణ గోడ. సెల్యులోజ్ అనేది బీటా-1,4 గ్లైకోసిడిక్ బంధాల ద్వారా అనుసంధానించబడిన గ్లూకోజ్ పాలిమర్. ఇది కణానికి దృఢత్వం ఇస్తుంది మరియు జంతు కణాలలో ఉండదు. వృక్ష రాజ్యం సరళమైన నుండి సంక్లిష్టమైన వరకు ఐదు ప్రధాన ఉప-విభాగాలుగా విభజించబడింది: శైవలాలు, బ్రయోఫైట్‌లు, టెరిడోఫైట్‌లు, వివృతబీజాలు మరియు ఆవృతబీజాలు.
Classification Order Mnemonic — "ABPGA"
The five sub-divisions in ascending order of complexity are: Algae → Bryophytes → Pteridophytes → Gymnosperms → Angiosperms. Vascular tissue first appears in Pteridophytes. Seeds first appear in Gymnosperms. Flowers and fruits first appear in Angiosperms.
వర్గీకరణ క్రమం: శైవలాలు → బ్రయోఫైట్‌లు → టెరిడోఫైట్‌లు → వివృతబీజాలు → ఆవృతబీజాలు. నాళికా కణజాలం మొదట టెరిడోఫైట్‌లలో, విత్తనాలు మొదట వివృతబీజాలలో, పువ్వులు+పండ్లు మొదట ఆవృతబీజాలలో కనిపిస్తాయి.
Plant Kingdom Classification వృక్ష రాజ్య వర్గీకరణ
EN Algae are the simplest plants, predominantly aquatic, and lack true roots, stems, or leaves. They contain chlorophyll and perform photosynthesis, yet they have no organised tissues. Bryophytes (mosses and liverworts) are often called the "amphibians of the plant kingdom" because they require water for reproduction but can live on moist land. They still lack vascular tissue. Pteridophytes (ferns) were the first true vascular plants — they possess xylem and phloem for water and food transport — but they reproduce by spores rather than seeds. Gymnosperms are the first seed-bearing plants; their seeds are "naked" (not enclosed in a fruit) and are borne on the scales of cones. Pine, Cycas, and Cedar are classic examples. Angiosperms are the most advanced and most numerous group — flowering plants whose seeds are enclosed inside fruits derived from the ovary wall. They dominate nearly every terrestrial ecosystem on Earth.

Exam Trap — Pine Bears Seeds but NOT Fruits
Gymnosperms such as Pine, Cycas, and Cedar produce seeds on the exposed scales of cones. Because there is no ovary wall enclosing the seeds, no fruit is formed. Angiosperms such as mango, peepal, eucalyptus, and cotton all produce fruits. If an exam question asks which plant "bears seeds but no fruits," the answer is always a gymnosperm — most commonly Pine.
వివృతబీజాలు (పైన్, సైకాస్) = విత్తనాలు ఉంటాయి, పండు ఉండదు. పరీక్షలో "విత్తనాలున్నాయి కానీ పండు లేదు" అంటే — వివృతబీజం (పైన్).
Special Plant Types — Habitat-Based Classification ప్రత్యేక మొక్క రకాలు — నివాస ఆధారిత వర్గీకరణ
EN Plants are also classified by the special habitats in which they grow and the adaptations they have evolved. Epiphytes grow on the surface of other plants — using them only as a physical support and not for nutrition, so they are not parasites. Orchids and Vanda are classic epiphytes. Halophytes are salt-tolerant plants adapted to saline soils, such as the mangroves of the Sundarbans. They use specialised salt glands and pneumatophores (breathing roots) for gas exchange. Xerophytes are drought-adapted plants found in deserts, with features like thick waxy cuticles, sunken stomata, and water-storing succulent tissues (cacti). Hydrophytes are aquatic plants such as Hydrilla, lotus, and water lily, adapted with air spaces (aerenchyma) for buoyancy and gaseous exchange. Insectivorous plants such as Drosera (sundew), Venus flytrap, and Nepenthes (pitcher plant) trap and digest insects to supplement their nitrogen intake, because they grow in nitrogen-poor soils such as bogs.

Epiphytes Are NOT Parasites
A common exam trap is to classify epiphytic orchids as parasites. Epiphytes use other plants only as a physical surface to anchor themselves above the ground and closer to sunlight. They absorb water and nutrients from rainfall, air, and debris — not from the host plant's tissues. Parasites (such as Cuscuta or mistletoe) actively penetrate host tissue and steal water and nutrients. An epiphyte causes no harm to its host.
వృక్షోపజీవులు (ఆర్కిడ్) పరాన్నజీవులు కావు — ఆతిథేయి మొక్క నుండి పోషణ తీసుకోవు. పరాన్నజీవులు (కస్కుటా, మిస్టిల్‌టో) ఆతిథేయి కణజాలాన్ని చొచ్చుకొని నీరు + పోషణ తీసుకుంటాయి.
Algae in Detail శైవలాలు — వివరణాత్మక అధ్యయనం
EN Algae are the most ancient group of photosynthetic organisms. The study of algae is called phycology. They are predominantly aquatic — found in freshwater (ponds, rivers) and marine (ocean) environments — though some grow on rocks, soil, or tree bark in moist conditions. Algae lack true roots, stems, and leaves; their body is simply called a thallus. They are grouped into four major types based on their photosynthetic pigments. Green algae (Chlorophyta) contain chlorophyll a and b and are considered the ancestors of land plants; examples include Chlamydomonas (unicellular), Spirogyra (filamentous), and Volvox (colonial). Red algae (Rhodophyta) contain phycoerythrin (a red pigment) that allows them to photosynthesize at greater ocean depths. Brown algae (Phaeophyta) contain fucoxanthin and include Sargassum and Laminaria — important in marine ecosystems. Blue-green algae are technically Cyanobacteria, belonging to kingdom Monera (prokaryotes), not true algae; examples include Nostoc, Anabaena (which fixes atmospheric nitrogen symbiotically with Azolla), and Oscillatoria. The stonewort Chara is a freshwater alga whose calcium-encrusted surface gives it a stony texture. Chlorella is a unicellular green alga tested for space travel because it produces oxygen efficiently and can serve as food.
TE శైవలాల అధ్యయనాన్ని ఫైకాలజీ అంటారు. వీటికి నిజమైన వేర్లు, కాండం, ఆకులు లేవు; శరీరాన్ని థాల్లస్ అంటారు. నాలుగు ప్రధాన రకాలు: హరిత శైవలాలు (క్లోరోఫైటా) — క్లోరోఫిల్ a+b ఉంటాయి; ఉదా: క్లమైడోమోనాస్, స్పైరోగైరా. ఎర్ర శైవలాలు (రోడోఫైటా) — ఫైకోఎరిత్రిన్; లోతైన సముద్రంలో కిరణజన్యక్రియ. గోధుమ శైవలాలు (ఫీయోఫైటా) — ఫ్యూకోజాంతిన్; సర్గాస్సమ్, లామినారియా. నీలి-ఆకుపచ్చ శైవలాలు = సైనోబ్యాక్టీరియా (మొనేరా రాజ్యానికి చెందినవి; నిజమైన శైవలాలు కావు): నాస్టాక్, అనాబీనా (N₂ స్థిరీకరణ), ఆసిలటోరియా. కారా = స్టోన్‌వోర్ట్; కాల్షియం పూత వల్ల రాయిలా ఉంటుంది. క్లోరెల్లా = ఏకకణ హరిత శైవలం; అంతరిక్ష ప్రయాణంలో O₂ ఉత్పత్తి మరియు ఆహారంగా.

Blue-Green Algae Are NOT True Algae
Blue-green algae (Cyanobacteria) are prokaryotic organisms that belong to kingdom Monera, not the plant kingdom. They lack a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles. However, they do possess chlorophyll and perform photosynthesis. Anabaena fixes atmospheric nitrogen in its specialised cells called heterocysts and lives symbiotically inside the leaves of the water fern Azolla — making Azolla a powerful biofertiliser for paddy fields.

Plant Cell Structure వృక్ష కణ నిర్మాణం
EN The plant cell is a eukaryotic cell with several features that distinguish it sharply from the animal cell. The most fundamental difference is the cellulose cell wall — a rigid outer layer made of a beta-1,4 glucose polymer that provides structural support and prevents osmotic lysis. Inside the cell wall lies the cell membrane. Chloroplasts are the organelles responsible for photosynthesis; they contain an internal membrane system called thylakoids (arranged in stacks called grana) embedded in a fluid called stroma. Light reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes; dark reactions (Calvin cycle) occur in the stroma. Chloroplasts contain their own circular DNA and ribosomes, suggesting an endosymbiotic origin. The large central vacuole occupies up to 90% of the plant cell's volume and is bounded by the tonoplast membrane; it maintains cell turgor pressure and stores water, ions, pigments, and waste products. Plant cells contain ribosomes (about 20 nm in size, visible as dots under the electron microscope) which synthesise all cellular proteins. Plant cells lack centrioles and lysosomes — structures found in animal cells.

Chlorophyll Composition — Calcium is NOT Present
Chlorophyll contains the elements Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, and Magnesium. The magnesium ion sits at the centre of the porphyrin ring and is essential for the light-absorbing function. Calcium is NOT part of the chlorophyll molecule. Confusing Mg with Ca is a common exam trap. Compare: Haemoglobin has Iron (Fe) at its porphyrin centre; Haemocyanin (arthropod/mollusc blood) uses Copper (Cu).

Photosynthesis — The Chemistry of Life కిరణజన్య సంయోగక్రియ — జీవన రసాయనం
EN Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and cyanobacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. The overall balanced equation is: 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂. The net result of photosynthesis is the reduction of carbon dioxide to glucose and the simultaneous release of molecular oxygen from water. Plants generate approximately 80% of all atmospheric oxygen through photosynthesis — the remainder comes primarily from marine algae. This is why ecologists warn that the death of all plant life would lead first and most critically to a shortage of atmospheric oxygen. Photosynthesis occurs inside chloroplasts in two linked stages. The light reactions take place on the thylakoid membranes: chlorophyll molecules absorb photons, water molecules are split (photolysis) releasing O₂, and the energy is captured as ATP and NADPH. The dark reactions (Calvin cycle or C3 cycle) take place in the stroma: the enzyme RuBisCO fixes CO₂ onto the five-carbon acceptor RuBP, forming the three-carbon compound 3-Phosphoglyceric Acid (PGA) as the first stable product, which is then converted via G3P into glucose.

EN — C3, C4 and CAM Plants Most plants fix CO₂ directly into the three-carbon compound PGA via RuBisCO — these are C3 plants (rice, wheat, cotton). They suffer from photorespiration at high temperatures. C4 plants (sugarcane, maize, sorghum) first fix CO₂ into a four-carbon compound (oxaloacetate) in the mesophyll cells, then concentrate it in the bundle-sheath cells where the Calvin cycle runs at elevated CO₂ levels — dramatically reducing photorespiration. This makes C4 plants significantly more efficient in tropical conditions, which is why sugarcane has the highest rate of photosynthesis among crop plants. C4 plants use the Hatch-Slack pathway. CAM plants (cacti, agave, pineapple) open their stomata only at night to fix CO₂ and run the Calvin cycle by day — a water-conserving strategy for arid environments.

First Stable Product of Calvin Cycle = PGA, NOT Glucose
A very common exam trap is to say that photosynthesis directly produces glucose. In reality, the Calvin cycle produces 3-Phosphoglyceric Acid (PGA) as its first stable product when RuBisCO catalyses the reaction: CO₂ + RuBP (5C) → 2 molecules of PGA (3C each). PGA is then phosphorylated and reduced to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), which is eventually used to synthesise glucose and starch. PGA forms within milliseconds; glucose only after many Calvin cycle turns.
కాల్విన్ చక్రం మొదటి స్థిర ఉత్పత్తి = PGA (3-ఫాస్ఫోగ్లిసరిక్ ఆమ్లం; 3 కార్బన్). గ్లూకోజ్ కాదు. RuBP + CO₂ → 2 PGA → G3P → గ్లూకోజ్.
Photosynthetic Pigments కిరణజన్య వర్ణ ద్రవ్యాలు
EN Photosynthesis depends on pigments that absorb specific wavelengths of light. The primary pigment in all green plants is Chlorophyll-a, a blue-green pigment that directly participates in the light reactions of photosynthesis. Chlorophyll-b is an accessory yellow-green pigment that absorbs light at slightly different wavelengths and transfers the energy to chlorophyll-a. Both chlorophylls absorb strongly in the blue region (~430 nm) and the red region (~660-680 nm), and reflect green light (~550 nm) — which is why leaves appear green. Carotenoids are accessory pigments that absorb blue-green wavelengths and reflect orange and yellow light. They are responsible for the brilliant orange and yellow colours of autumn leaves when chlorophyll breaks down. Anthocyanins are water-soluble red and purple pigments found in the vacuoles of flower petals and some leaves; they are responsible for the red/purple/blue colours of flowers and fruits (e.g., red roses, blueberries). Unlike chlorophylls and carotenoids, anthocyanins contain no metal ion at their centre.

Plant Anatomy and Tissues వృక్ష అనాటమీ మరియు కణజాలాలు
EN Plants have three tissue systems: the dermal system (epidermis — the outer protective layer with cuticle and stomata), the vascular system (xylem and phloem — for transport), and the ground system (cortex, pith, and mesophyll — for storage, photosynthesis, and support). Xylem is the vascular tissue that conducts water and dissolved minerals upward from the roots to the leaves. It consists of tracheids (elongated dead cells) and vessel elements. The lumen is empty because xylem cells die at maturity, leaving only the thick lignified walls. Phloem conducts dissolved organic food (mainly sucrose) from the leaves (where it is produced by photosynthesis) downward to the roots and other growing parts. Phloem consists of living sieve tube elements and companion cells. Sclerenchyma is dead, thick-walled, lignified tissue that provides mechanical strength and rigidity — found in the husk of coconut and the hard shells of nuts. Meristem is actively dividing tissue found at growth points. Apical meristem at shoot and root tips drives primary growth (increase in height/length); lateral meristem (the vascular cambium) drives secondary growth (increase in girth). Wood is simply the common name for secondary xylem — the tissue deposited by the vascular cambium in concentric annual layers, creating the growth rings visible in a tree trunk cross-section.

Annual Rings and Tree Age
In temperate trees, the vascular cambium deposits a wide-pored layer of xylem in spring (spring wood) when growth is fast, and a narrow-pored layer in summer/autumn (autumn wood) when growth slows. This alternation creates one visible ring per year. Counting the rings in a transverse cross-section of the trunk gives the exact age of the tree. The science of dating wooden objects using ring patterns is called dendrochronology.

Xylem vs Phloem — Direction of Transport
Xylem carries water and minerals from roots to leaves — always UPWARD (against gravity, powered by transpiration pull). Phloem carries food (sugars produced by photosynthesis) from leaves to all other parts — both upward to growing shoots and downward to roots. This bidirectional food movement in phloem is called translocation. Xylem is dead tissue; phloem is living tissue. This distinction frequently appears in matching-type exam questions.

Modified Plant Organs రూపాంతరం చెందిన వృక్ష భాగాలు
EN Plants frequently modify their roots, stems, or leaves to perform specialised functions such as food storage, vegetative propagation, or support. These modifications are among the most frequently tested topics in competitive examinations because they look like one thing but are biologically another. The key to identifying a modified stem (as opposed to a modified root) is the presence of nodes and internodes. Roots never possess nodes, internodes, or scale leaves. Ginger (Zingiber officinale) is a horizontally growing underground stem called a rhizome. Its nodes, internodes, scale leaves, and buds clearly prove it is a stem, not a root. Turmeric (Curcuma longa) is also a rhizome and is obtained from the underground stem — not the root. Potato (Solanum tuberosum) is a tuber — a swollen, fleshy underground stem that stores starch. The "eyes" of the potato are actually buds sitting at nodes, and they serve as the primary means of vegetative propagation. Carrot is a modified taproot (main root) that stores food (carotene, sugars). Onion is a bulb — a compressed underground stem surrounded by fleshy leaf bases (the white layers), which store food.

Plant Reproduction and Pollination వృక్ష పునరుత్పత్తి మరియు పరాగసంపర్కం
EN Plants reproduce by two broad routes. Vegetative (asexual) reproduction involves the development of new individuals from somatic (non-reproductive) parts of the plant, without fertilisation. Common methods include: runners (mint, strawberry), bulbs (onion), tubers (potato eyes), suckers (banana), cuttings (rose), grafting (mango, citrus), and layering. Vegetative reproduction produces genetically identical clones of the parent — ideal for preserving fruit quality. Sexual reproduction involves the formation of male (pollen) and female (ovule) gametes, followed by pollination and fertilisation to produce seeds and fruits. Pollination is the transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma. It is performed by wind (anemophily), water (hydrophily), insects (entomophily — which represents a mutualistic relationship since both plant and insect benefit), or animals. Self-pollination (Autogamy) occurs when pollen from a flower lands on the stigma of the same flower. Cross-pollination (Allogamy) occurs when pollen is transferred between different flowers, often on different plants. Cleistogamy is a specialised form of self-pollination in which the flower never opens — pollination occurs inside the closed bud (e.g., Commelina). This guarantees self-fertilisation. Parthenocarpy is the development of a fruit from the ovary without fertilisation, producing seedless fruits such as banana and pineapple. It is commercially induced using auxin and gibberellin sprays.

Plant Hormones వృక్ష హార్మోన్లు
EN Plants regulate their growth, development, and responses to the environment using five major classes of chemical messengers called plant hormones (or phytohormones). Auxin (Indole-3-Acetic Acid / IAA) is produced at the shoot tip (apical meristem). It promotes cell elongation by loosening cell walls. In phototropism, when light falls on one side of a stem, auxin migrates to the shaded side, causing those cells to elongate more and bending the shoot toward the light. Auxin also causes apical dominance — the shoot tip suppresses lateral bud growth. Gibberellins promote stem elongation and internode elongation, trigger seed germination, and can override dormancy. Cytokinins (also called kinins) stimulate cell division (cytokinesis) and are used in plant tissue culture. They delay the senescence (aging) of leaves. Ethylene is the only gaseous plant hormone. It promotes fruit ripening — which is why placing a ripe banana near unripe fruits causes them to ripen quickly. It is commercially used to ripen bananas and tomatoes during transport. Abscisic Acid (ABA) is the "stress hormone" — it promotes leaf abscission (shedding), causes stomata to close under drought conditions to conserve water, and maintains seed dormancy. It is the antagonist of the growth-promoting hormones.

Phototropism Mechanism — Why Shoots Bend Toward Light
When sunlight falls on only one side of a stem, the hormone auxin (IAA) produced at the shoot tip migrates laterally toward the shaded side. The higher auxin concentration on the shaded side causes those cells to elongate more rapidly than the illuminated side. The net result is that the shoot curves toward the light source. This response is called positive phototropism. Roots show negative phototropism — they grow away from light. This differential growth in response to a directional stimulus is a fundamental mechanism of plant movement.

Gymnosperms in Detail వివృతబీజాలు — వివరణాత్మక అధ్యయనం
EN Gymnosperms are seed-bearing vascular plants whose seeds are "naked" — not enclosed inside a fruit. The word "gymnosperm" literally means "naked seed" (Greek: gymnos = naked, sperma = seed). These plants reproduce by cones (strobili) — male cones produce pollen and female cones bear the ovules on the surface of their scales. After pollination by wind, the ovule develops into a seed directly on the cone scale, with no surrounding fruit wall. This is why Pine, Cycas, Cedar, Sequoia, and Ginkgo are said to "bear seeds but not fruits." Gymnosperms first appeared approximately 300 million years ago and dominated terrestrial ecosystems during the Jurassic period. Cycas is called a "living fossil" because it has remained structurally unchanged for hundreds of millions of years. Ginkgo biloba is considered the oldest surviving tree species on Earth. Sequoia sempervirens (Coast Redwood) is the tallest living tree species, reaching over 115 metres. Many gymnosperms produce needle-like leaves adapted to cold climates — the narrow, waxy leaves reduce water loss and snow accumulation. Gymnosperms produce softwood (as opposed to the hardwood of angiosperm trees).

Useful Plants and Crops ఉపయోగకర మొక్కలు మరియు పంటలు
EN Plants have served humanity across medicine, food, industry, and textiles since ancient times. Indigo (Indigofera tinctoria) is a plant that yields the deep-blue dye indigo — the most stable and widely used natural blue dye in history, famously cultivated in Bengal under British colonial rule (Nil Darpan episode). Quinine, the oldest anti-malarial drug, is extracted from the bark of the Cinchona tree, native to the Andes mountains of South America. British colonial expansion in tropical regions was critically dependent on quinine to prevent malaria. Bamboo is classified as a grass (family Poaceae), not a tree; it is the world's fastest-growing plant, growing up to 91 cm per day. Banana is the world's largest herb — not a tree, because its "trunk" is a pseudostem formed from tightly wrapped leaf sheaths. Banana produces both edible fruit and fibre (Manila hemp/abaca from leaf sheaths). Soybean contains approximately 40% high-quality protein, including all nine essential amino acids, making it the most protein-rich plant crop and an invaluable food for vegetarians. Eucalyptus is a controversially fast-growing tree that absorbs very large quantities of groundwater (allelopathy and high transpiration), leading to its restriction in several Indian states. Azolla is a water fern that hosts nitrogen-fixing Anabaena cyanobacteria and is used as a biofertiliser in flooded paddy fields. Saffron is the world's most expensive spice — it consists of the dried stigmas of Crocus sativus flowers; each flower yields only three stigmas, and approximately 150,000 flowers are needed to produce 1 kilogram of saffron. Cinnamon is derived from the inner bark of Cinnamomum verum.

Soil and Plant Nutrition నేల మరియు వృక్ష పోషణ
EN Plants require 17 essential mineral elements for normal growth and development. These are divided into macronutrients (required in large amounts) and micronutrients (required in trace amounts). The three primary macronutrients are Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Potassium (K) — collectively called NPK. Nitrogen is a constituent of all proteins, nucleic acids, and chlorophyll — its deficiency causes yellowing of leaves (chlorosis). Phosphorus is essential for ATP synthesis, DNA and RNA structure, and root development — its deficiency causes purple discolouration of leaves. Potassium regulates enzyme activation, water balance (opening/closing of stomata), and carbohydrate translocation. The secondary macronutrients are Calcium, Magnesium, and Sulfur. Micronutrients include Iron, Manganese, Zinc, Copper, Boron, Molybdenum, Chlorine, and Nickel. Sodium is NOT an essential nutrient for most plants. Carbon, Hydrogen, and Oxygen are obtained from CO₂ and water — not from the soil. Soil water exists in three forms: Capillary water is held by surface tension in small pore spaces and is the form available to plant roots. Gravitational water drains downward through large pores under gravity and is not available to roots. Hygroscopic water is bound so tightly to soil particles that it cannot be absorbed by plant roots. Plants absorb nitrogen primarily as nitrate (NO₃⁻) from the soil — produced from ammonia by nitrifying bacteria such as Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter.

Three Types of Soil Water
Capillary water — held in small pores by surface tension; available to plant roots; the most important form for plant growth.
Gravitational water — in large pores; drains away under gravity after rain; temporarily fills pores but roots cannot hold it.
Hygroscopic water — forms a thin film around soil particles; held so tightly by adsorption forces that roots cannot absorb it.

Crop Rotation Logic — Why Legumes Come First
Legumes (peas, beans, groundnut) host Rhizobium bacteria in root nodules that fix atmospheric N₂ into ammonia — enriching the soil with nitrogen. After a legume crop, the soil is nitrogen-rich, supporting heavy-feeding cereals (barley, wheat) and tuber crops (potato). The ideal rotation sequence is: Pea → Barley → Wheat → Potato. After exhausting crops, restart with a legume. This avoids the need for synthetic nitrogen fertilisers.

Ecology and Plant-Environment Relationships ఆవరణ శాస్త్రం మరియు వృక్ష-పర్యావరణ సంబంధాలు
EN Ecology is the study of interactions between organisms and their environment. Plants occupy the foundational level of nearly all terrestrial food webs as primary producers — converting solar energy and inorganic matter into organic biomass. The relationship between flowering plants and insect pollinators is a classic example of mutualism — both parties benefit (the plant is cross-pollinated; the insect obtains nectar and pollen as food). Plants are classified by their light requirements: Heliophytes require full sunlight for optimal growth (most crops, teak, sunflower); Sciophytes (or shade plants) thrive in low-light conditions (forest ferns, mosses). Transpiration — the evaporation of water through stomata — is vital for pulling water upward through xylem and for cooling the leaf surface. Excess water is released as water vapour through stomata. Crop rotation with legumes restores soil nitrogen. Jhum cultivation (slash-and-burn farming) is practiced in the northeast India hill tribes — trees are felled and burned; crops are grown for 2-3 years before the plot is abandoned to regenerate; this is ecologically damaging when rotation periods are shortened. Wetlands are internationally protected under the Ramsar Convention (1971). Water hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes) is an invasive aquatic plant that chokes waterways but can absorb pollutants and produce biogas. Phanerogams are seed-bearing plants (gymnosperms + angiosperms). Cryptogams are non-seed-bearing plants (algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes).

Seed Germination — Three Essentials, One Non-essential
A dormant seed requires three things to germinate: Water (imbibition activates enzymes), Oxygen (for aerobic respiration to power germination), and Suitable temperature (typically 25-30°C). It does NOT require light — most seeds germinate underground in complete darkness. Only a small number of "photoblastic" seeds (such as lettuce) need light. Dormant seeds are among the most resistant structures in nature — lotus seeds have germinated after 1,300 years of dormancy.

Quick Reference — Plant Records and Superlatives త్వరిత సూచన — వృక్ష రికార్డులు
EN Competitive examinations frequently ask about plant record-holders — the largest, smallest, tallest, fastest-growing, most expensive, and most extreme plants. Rafflesia arnoldii holds the record for the world's largest flower — reaching up to 1 metre in diameter and weighing up to 10 kilograms. It is a parasitic plant (grows on the roots of Tetrastigma vines in Indonesian rainforests), lacks chlorophyll entirely, and produces no leaves or stems — only the enormous flower. It emits a smell resembling rotting meat to attract carrion flies for pollination. Wolffia (watermeal) holds the record for the world's smallest flower — approximately 0.3 millimetres in size, visible only under magnification. Sequoia sempervirens (Coast Redwood, California) is the world's tallest tree — the tallest known individual "Hyperion" is 115.7 metres tall. Victoria amazonica produces the world's largest leaf — up to 3 metres across, strong enough to support the weight of a small child. Bamboo is the world's fastest-growing plant overall — recorded at 91 cm per day. Eucalyptus is the fastest-growing tree species. The Bristlecone Pine (Pinus longaeva) is the world's oldest living tree — specimens over 5,000 years old have been documented. Among spices, Saffron (stigmas of Crocus sativus) is the world's most expensive, at approximately $10,000 per kilogram.

Plant Kingdom & Classification
  • Defining feature of plants = cellulose cell wall (beta-1,4 glucose polymer).
  • ABPGA order: Algae → Bryophytes → Pteridophytes → Gymnosperms → Angiosperms.
  • Bryophytes = "amphibians of plant kingdom."
  • Pteridophytes = first vascular plants (xylem + phloem); spore reproduction.
  • Gymnosperms = naked seeds on cones; no fruit. Pine bears seeds, NOT fruits.
  • Angiosperms = enclosed seeds in fruit; double fertilisation.
  • Phanerogams = seed-bearing (gymnosperms + angiosperms).
  • Cryptogams = non-seed (algae + bryophytes + pteridophytes).
  • Cycas = living fossil. Ginkgo = oldest surviving tree species.
  • Sequoia = tallest tree (115.7 m, California).
Algae
  • Phycology = study of algae.
  • Chara = stonewort (freshwater; CaCO₃ encrusted).
  • Blue-green algae = Cyanobacteria (Monera — prokaryotes, NOT true algae).
  • Anabaena fixes N₂; lives symbiotically in Azolla leaves.
  • Chlorella = unicellular green alga used for space O₂ production.
  • Green algae = ancestors of land plants.
Plant Cell
  • Cellulose = beta-1,4 glucose; only in plants (and some bacteria/fungi).
  • Chloroplast = site of photosynthesis; thylakoid + stroma.
  • Chlorophyll contains C, H, O, N, Mg — NOT calcium.
  • Central metal: Chlorophyll = Mg; Haemoglobin = Fe; Haemocyanin = Cu.
  • Ribosomes appear as dots under electron microscope; protein synthesis.
  • Plant cells have large central vacuole; lack centrioles and lysosomes.
Photosynthesis
  • Equation: 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂.
  • Net result = reduction of CO₂ + release of O₂.
  • Light reactions (thylakoid): photolysis, O₂ release, ATP + NADPH.
  • Dark reactions / Calvin cycle (stroma): RuBisCO fixes CO₂ into PGA.
  • First step = excitation of chlorophyll by sunlight.
  • First stable product of Calvin cycle = PGA (3-Phosphoglyceric Acid).
  • Chlorophyll absorbs blue (~430 nm) + red (~660-680 nm); reflects green.
  • Green light = least effective; red light = most active for photosynthesis.
  • Sugarcane = highest photosynthesis rate (C4 plant; Hatch-Slack pathway).
  • C3 plants = rice, wheat. C4 = sugarcane, maize. CAM = cactus, pineapple.
  • Plants produce ~80% of all atmospheric O₂.
Plant Hormones
  • Auxin = cell elongation; phototropism (bends toward light); apical dominance.
  • Gibberellin = stem elongation; seed germination; breaks dormancy.
  • Cytokinin (kinin) = cell division; delays leaf senescence; tissue culture.
  • Ethylene (gas) = fruit ripening; only gaseous plant hormone.
  • ABA (Abscisic Acid) = leaf abscission; stomatal closure; seed dormancy — the "stress hormone."
Reproduction & Pollination
  • Autogamy = self-pollination (same flower).
  • Allogamy = cross-pollination (different flowers).
  • Cleistogamy = pollination inside closed flower (Commelina).
  • Parthenocarpy = fruit without fertilisation = seedless (banana, pineapple).
  • Insect-plant pollination = mutualism (both benefit).
  • Vegetative propagation = clones; best for fruit quality (grafting).
  • Potato eyes = buds = vegetative propagation.
  • Seed germination needs water + air + temperature (NOT light).
Modified Organs
  • Ginger = rhizome (underground stem); proof = nodes and internodes.
  • Turmeric = rhizome (stem, not root).
  • Potato = tuber (swollen underground stem).
  • Carrot = modified taproot.
  • Onion = bulb (compressed stem + leaf bases).
  • Groundnut = NOT a tuber; it is a geocarpic legume (underground fruit).
Soil, Nutrition & Useful Plants
  • NPK = primary macronutrients: Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium.
  • Secondary macros = Ca, Mg, S. Micronutrients = Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu, B, Mo, Cl, Ni.
  • Sodium = NOT essential for most plants.
  • Plants absorb nitrogen as nitrate (NO₃⁻) from soil.
  • Capillary water = available to plants; gravitational = drains away; hygroscopic = unavailable.
  • Compost = most balanced fertiliser (all nutrients).
  • Crop rotation: Pea (legume, N-fixation) → Barley → Wheat → Potato.
  • Quinine = Cinchona bark (anti-malarial).
  • Saffron = stigma of Crocus sativus (most expensive spice; Kashmir).
  • Cinnamon = inner bark of Cinnamomum verum.
  • Soybean = ~40% protein; all 9 essential amino acids.
  • Azolla (water fern + Anabaena) = biofertiliser for rice/paddy.
  • Bamboo = grass; fastest-growing plant (91 cm/day).
  • Banana = largest herb; pseudostem; fruit + fibre; parthenocarpy.
  • Eucalyptus = fastest-growing tree; drains maximum groundwater.
